### 1. Faecalibacterium prausnitzii enzyme reprograms PD-L1 trafficking and sensitizes colorectal cancer to immunotherapy in mice

- **Journal:** Nature Microbiology (IF ~28)
- **Highlights:** Ji, Liu, Xu et al. identify phosphoribosyl pyrophosphate synthetase (PRPS) secreted by *Faecalibacterium prausnitzii* as a gut-microbiome-derived effector that directly inhibits PD-L1 trafficking to the colorectal cancer cell surface. By preventing PD-L1 from reaching the plasma membrane, PRPS preserves cytotoxic CD8⁺ T cell activity within the tumour microenvironment. In preclinical CRC mouse models, administration of F. prausnitzii or recombinant PRPS potentiated anti-PD-1 checkpoint inhibitor therapy, dramatically reducing tumour burden—establishing a mechanistic link between the presence of this commensal anaerobe and immunotherapy response in microsatellite-stable colorectal cancer.
- **Link:** [doi:10.1038/s41564-026-02326-2](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41564-026-02326-2)

### 2. Multiomics-guided discovery of protective microbiome signatures in lupus-prone mice treated with Faecalibacterium prausnitzii

- **Journal:** Nature Communications (IF ~17)
- **Highlights:** Zhao, Geng, Jimenez et al. use metagenomics, metatranscriptomics, and metabolomics to profile gut dysbiosis in the B6.Sle1.Yaa male mouse model of systemic lupus erythematosus (SLE). Oral administration of *F. prausnitzii* strain UT1 partially reversed lupus-associated microbiome alterations and rescued carbohydrate metabolic deficiency: treated mice showed restricted mucin-degrading gene expression, elevated pentose phosphate pathway and bile-acid-modifying activities, and a shift in tryptophan catabolism toward indoleacetic acid and indoleacrylic acid—both known AhR agonists that promote regulatory T-cell differentiation. The multiomics framework provides a blueprint for identifying mechanistic microbiome-based interventions in autoimmune disease.
- **Link:** [doi:10.1038/s41467-026-71718-z](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41467-026-71718-z)
