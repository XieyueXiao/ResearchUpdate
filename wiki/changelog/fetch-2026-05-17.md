### 1. Indole-3-carboxaldehyde from *Limosilactobacillus reuteri* targets the DUSP1/ERK/NOX2/ROS axis to enhance the bactericidal activity of macrophages and protects against sepsis

- **Journal:** Gut Microbes (IF ~12)
- **Published:** 2026-05-06
- **Authors:** Zhiwang Li et al.
- **Highlights:** This study mechanistically links a gut probiotic metabolite to systemic innate immune protection against sepsis. Indole-3-carboxaldehyde (IAld), a tryptophan-derived secondary metabolite produced by *Limosilactobacillus reuteri*, was found to enhance macrophage bactericidal activity by inhibiting the dual-specificity phosphatase DUSP1. DUSP1 normally dephosphorylates and thereby inactivates ERK; IAld-mediated DUSP1 suppression sustained ERK phosphorylation, which in turn upregulated NOX2-dependent reactive oxygen species (ROS) generation and intracellular pathogen killing. In murine cecal ligation and puncture (CLP) and LPS-challenge sepsis models, L. reuteri supplementation or direct IAld administration significantly reduced systemic bacterial burden and improved survival. The findings extend the known immunomodulatory repertoire of L. reuteri beyond AhR/tryptophan-indole signalling and position the DUSP1/ERK/NOX2/ROS axis as a druggable target in microbiome-informed sepsis management.
- **Link:** [Gut Microbes — Li et al. 2026](https://www.tandfonline.com/journals/kgmi20) *(full DOI not yet indexed; confirmed via Taylor & Francis search)*

### 2. Parental microbiome programming of early-life neurodevelopment: multi-niche contributions through the microbiome–gut–brain axis

- **Journal:** Gut Microbes (IF ~12)
- **Published:** 2026-05-14
- **Authors:** Jurga Skrabulyte-Barbulescu et al. (Institute of Psychiatry, Psychology and Neuroscience, King's College London)
- **Highlights:** A comprehensive multi-niche review synthesising how the microbiomes of both parents — across gut, oral, skin, and vaginal compartments — programme offspring brain development through the microbiome–gut–brain axis (MGBA). Maternal gut dysbiosis is shown to transmit altered microbial colonisation at birth, disrupting early neuroimmune education, myelination, HPA axis calibration, and blood–brain barrier permeability. Paternal contributions emerge as an underappreciated but mechanistically plausible axis, with evidence for sperm epigenetic marks and seminal microbiota influencing offspring phenotype. Key metabolite mediators reviewed include SCFAs (particularly butyrate), tryptophan/indole catabolites (IAA, IPA), secondary bile acids, LPS/TLR4 signalling, and the vagus nerve as a structural conduit. The authors identify critical developmental windows spanning preconception through the perinatal period as optimal targets for microbiome-based neurodevelopmental interventions, and advocate multi-omics approaches for personalised strategies addressing ASD, ADHD, and anxiety disorders.
- **Link:** [Gut Microbes — Skrabulyte-Barbulescu et al. 2026](https://www.tandfonline.com/journals/kgmi20) *(full DOI not yet indexed; confirmed via search metadata)*
