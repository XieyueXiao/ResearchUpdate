### 1. Cross-kingdom microbiome interactions along the gut–lung axis: immune–microecological coordination, shared mechanisms, and disease-context dependence in respiratory disorders
- **Journal:** Gut Microbes (IF ~15)
- **Highlights:** This comprehensive review by Jiang Yu et al. examines how bacteria, fungi, and viruses in the gut interact across kingdoms to shape pulmonary immunity via the gut–lung axis. The authors synthesise immune-microecological coordination mechanisms — including microbially derived SCFAs, secondary bile acids, and indole metabolites that modulate lung-resident immune cells — and map shared regulatory circuits relevant to asthma, COPD, pulmonary fibrosis, and respiratory infections. A key contribution is the framing of cross-kingdom interplay (bacterial–fungal competition for mucosal niches, viral dysbiosis of the bacteriome) as disease-context dependent rather than uniformly protective or pathogenic, with implications for therapeutic targeting of the gut–lung axis.
- **Link:** [Gut Microbes (online ahead of print, 2026-06-21)](https://www.tandfonline.com/journals/kgmi20) — DOI pending full indexing

### 2. Indole-3-acetic acid production is rare among gut bacteria and reflects OFOR-driven amino acid oxidation in acetogens
- **Journal:** Gut Microbes (IF ~15)
- **Highlights:** Mary E. DeFeo et al. screened 206 human gut bacterial isolates for indole-3-acetic acid (IAA) production and found that high-capacity synthesis is exceptionally rare, confined to the acetogens *Blautia hydrogenotrophica* and *Intestinibacter bartlettii*. Across multiple growth conditions, IAA emerged as a minor byproduct alongside dominant branched-chain and aromatic organic acids generated through carbohydrate-sensitive catabolism. Genomic analyses and heterologous enzyme assays identified expanded repertoires of 2-oxoacid:ferredoxin oxidoreductases (OFORs) — enzymes spanning pyruvate/oxaloacetate, branched-chain, and aromatic 2-oxoacid substrates — as the mechanistic basis, with indolepyruvate conversion to indoleacetyl-CoA as the terminal step. The findings revise prevailing assumptions about which gut taxa supply IAA to the host and reposition OFOR-rich acetogens as the primary microbial source of this AhR ligand.
- **Link:** [bioRxiv preprint](https://doi.org/10.64898/2026.01.29.702580); [Gut Microbes published version](https://www.tandfonline.com/journals/kgmi20) — journal DOI pending full indexing; PMC12873857

### 3. Inhibition of Aryl hydrocarbon receptor Interleukin-22 signaling and worsening of intestinal inflammation by Sutterella species
- **Journal:** Gut Microbes (IF ~15)
- **Highlights:** Louise Dupraz et al. establish *Sutterella* species as pathobionts that suppress the AhR–IL-22 axis in both mice and humans and thereby worsen intestinal inflammation. Using colonisation and cell-based experiments, the authors show that *Sutterella* secretes large (>30 kDa) protein-based effectors that inhibit IL-22 production from innate lymphoid cells and reduce AhR transcriptional activity, leading to impaired epithelial barrier function and aggravated colitis. The study fills a mechanistic gap for a genus long associated with IBD and neurodevelopmental conditions; it positions tryptophan-AhR-IL-22 circuit disruption as *Sutterella*'s pathogenic mode of action and suggests that AhR-agonist supplementation or *Sutterella* depletion could be explored as adjunct IBD therapies.
- **Link:** [bioRxiv preprint](https://doi.org/10.1101/2025.03.20.644103); [Gut Microbes published version](https://www.tandfonline.com/journals/kgmi20) — journal DOI pending full indexing
