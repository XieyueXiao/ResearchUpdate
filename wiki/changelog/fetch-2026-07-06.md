### 1. Vitamin B6 produced by gut microbiome regulates host behavioral phenotypes through dopaminergic metabolism
- **Journal:** Gut Microbes (IF ~12)
- **Highlights:** Gut-brain-axis study identifying gut-microbiome-derived vitamin B6 as a regulator of host behavioral phenotypes via dopaminergic metabolism. Extends the growing body of work on microbial B-vitamin biosynthesis as a mechanistic node in gut-brain signaling, alongside prior tryptophan/serotonin and GABA-pathway findings already in this knowledge base. ### 2. Microbial metabolite Urolithin A protects against inorganic arsenic-induced gut barrier dysfunction in humanized AS3MT mice
- **Journal:** Gut Microbes (IF ~12)
- **Highlights:** Using humanized AS3MT (arsenic methyltransferase) mice and colon organoid models, shows the microbial metabolite urolithin A mitigates inorganic-arsenic-induced oxidative stress, epithelial cytotoxicity, and tight-junction loss (ZO-1/occludin/claudin-4). A distinct mechanism/target from the already-catalogued Ghosh et al. urolithin A–AhR–NLRP6 paper (Nature Communications, June 23) — same first author, different journal and different pathway (arsenic detoxification/barrier protection vs. AhR–NLRP6 mucosal immunity axis), not a duplicate.
- **Link:** DOI pending indexing (Ghosh, S. et al.; published 2026-07-03)

### 3. An ubiquitous antibacterial toxin from human gut bacteria engenders neonatal colonization advantage
- **Journal:** Nature Communications (IF ~17)
- **Highlights:** Sequencing of ~900 clinical Bacteroides fragilis isolates identifies a widespread T6SS effector toxin ("Bte3") that confers a competitive colonization advantage during neonatal gut assembly in a murine model. Mechanistic contribution to understanding early-life microbiome establishment and interbacterial competition dynamics. Exact publication date could not be confirmed (direct fetch to nature.com/crossref.org blocked by HTTP 403); DOI sequence positioning suggests early-to-mid June 2026 — flagged as a belated indexing-lag catch, not previously captured in any prior fetch.
- **Link:** [DOI](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41467-026-74062-4) (Valguarnera, E., Tung, J., Pak, J.J. et al.)

### 4. Prevalent gut phages encode modular adhesins mediating epithelial binding and endoplasmic reticulum trafficking
- **Journal:** Nature Communications (IF ~17)
- **Highlights:** High-throughput screen identifies Ig-like adhesin domains on abundant human gut phages (including crAss-like phages and Flandersviridae-related myoviruses) that mediate epithelial cell binding and ER trafficking. Reframes phage–host interaction in the gut mechanistically, with direct implications for engineering phage-based therapeutics that must navigate epithelial barriers. Published 2026-06-04 — a genuine miss across ~4 weeks of prior fetches, not previously captured.
- **Link:** [DOI](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41467-026-74031-x) (Apjok, G., Sári, T., Méhi, O. et al.; published 2026-06-04)
