### 1. Cross-body site microbial interactions influence the human plasma metabolome

- **Journal:** Microbiome (Springer, IF ~15)
- **Highlights:** Zhang, Jiang, Zhou, Gao, Wong, Snyder, Shen et al. conducted a systematic
  multi-body-site study in 435 individuals across three independent cohorts, profiling gut,
  oral, skin, and nasal microbiomes alongside 814 annotated plasma metabolites. The combined
  microbiomes collectively explained 30.13 % of inter-individual plasma metabolome variation,
  with the gut microbiome contributing most (18.44 %), followed by oral (14.70 %), skin
  (11.5 %), and nasal (5.88 %) microbiomes. Despite markedly distinct taxonomic compositions,
  the oral and gut microbiomes showed strikingly similar associations with circulating
  metabolites, pointing to shared functional metabolic outputs across anatomical sites.
  These findings reframe systemic metabolomics as an integrated readout of multi-site
  host–microbiome interactions rather than a gut-centric signal, with implications for
  biomarker discovery and precision microbiome medicine.
- **Link:** [DOI: 10.1186/s40168-026-02405-w](https://doi.org/10.1186/s40168-026-02405-w)
- **Published:** 2026-04-09 (not captured in prior fetches)

### 2. The gut–joint axis in osteoarthritis

- **Journal:** Nature Reviews Rheumatology (bonus — not on primary target list; IF ~25)
- **Highlights:** This comprehensive review synthesises mounting evidence that gut microbiota
  dysbiosis is a causal contributor to osteoarthritis (OA) pathogenesis, establishing the
  gut–joint axis as a tractable therapeutic target. Mechanistically, dysbiosis driven by ageing,
  obesity, or diet disrupts intestinal tight junctions, allowing LPS translocation into the
  circulation; circulating LPS engages CD14/MD-2–TLR4 on macrophages and chondrocytes,
  activating the NFκB pathway and triggering the release of pro-inflammatory cytokines
  (IL-1β, TNF-α) and matrix-degrading enzymes that erode cartilage and remodel subchondral
  bone. Conversely, SCFA-producing anaerobes alleviate synovial inflammation and limit
  chondrocyte catabolism; AhR activation by tryptophan-derived indoles supports mucosal
  barrier integrity. The review evaluates therapeutic strategies targeting the axis —
  prebiotics, probiotics, synbiotics, FMT, and dietary interventions — and highlights
  unresolved questions around causality direction, OA subtype specificity, and clinical
  trial design.
- **Link:** [DOI: 10.1038/s41584-026-01378-2](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41584-026-01378-2)
- **Published:** ~2026-05-07
