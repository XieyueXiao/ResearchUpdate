### 1. Altered SIgA-targeting of gut microbiota is associated with long-term dysbiosis in pediatric solid organ transplant recipients
- **Journal:** Gut Microbes (IF ~12)
- **Highlights:** Secretory IgA (SIgA) is a key mucosal antibody that coats and shapes gut microbial communities, but its role in post-transplant dysbiosis has been understudied. Schulze et al. profiled gut microbiota composition (16S rRNA) and SIgA-targeting (SIgA-seq) in 48 pediatric solid organ transplant recipients (heart, kidney, or liver) and age-matched healthy controls. Transplant recipients showed persistent dysbiosis — reduced microbial diversity and altered community structure — that correlated with perturbed SIgA-coating patterns; specific pathobionts evaded SIgA targeting while commensal taxa were over-targeted relative to controls. Immunosuppressive therapy was identified as a key driver of the disrupted SIgA repertoire, suggesting a mechanistic link between immunosuppression → altered mucosal immunity → long-term dysbiosis in pediatric transplant patients. The findings open avenues for SIgA-guided microbiome monitoring and potential mucosal immunotherapy in transplant settings.
- **Link:** [doi:10.1080/19490976.2026.2675078](https://doi.org/10.1080/19490976.2026.2675078)

### 2. Increasing plant protein sources in the diet modulates gut microbiota and tryptophan metabolism in men at cardiometabolic risk
- **Journal:** Gut Microbes (IF ~12)
- **Highlights:** Dietary protein source (animal vs. plant) is increasingly recognised as a modulator of the gut microbiome, with downstream effects on host metabolism. Lépine et al. conducted a dietary intervention substituting animal proteins with plant protein sources in adult men at cardiometabolic risk and assessed changes in gut microbiota composition and tryptophan metabolite profiles. Increasing plant protein intake restructured the gut microbiome toward taxa associated with plant polysaccharide fermentation and altered the tryptophan-to-indole/serotonin metabolic axis, with implications for gut barrier integrity and systemic cardiometabolic risk. The study provides mechanistic evidence linking plant-based dietary shifts to microbiome-mediated tryptophan metabolism as a potential driver of cardiometabolic benefit, adding to the body of evidence supporting plant-forward dietary patterns for at-risk populations.
