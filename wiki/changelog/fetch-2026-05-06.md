### 1. Ketogenic diet exacerbates DSS-induced colitis through a β-hydroxybutyrate–Thomasclavelia spiroformis–γδ17 T cell axis in mice
- **Journal:** Nature Communications (IF ~17)
- **Highlights:** This mechanistic study resolves the contested effects of the ketogenic diet (KD) on inflammatory bowel disease by demonstrating that KD maintains gut homeostasis under physiological conditions but exacerbates chemically induced colitis through a three-step microbiome–immune cascade: KD elevates luminal β-hydroxybutyrate (β-HB), which selectively promotes expansion of *Thomasclavelia spiroformis*; cell-wall components of this Clostridiales bacterium then activate colonic γδ17 T cells, driving IL-17A-mediated mucosal inflammation. Abolishing ketogenesis pharmacologically or blocking IL-17A prevents KD-exacerbated colitis, while β-HB supplementation recapitulates it. Clinically, T. spiroformis abundance correlates with fecal β-HB and serum IL-17A specifically in ulcerative colitis patients (not Crohn's disease), suggesting that KD interventions warrant disease-subtype stratification in IBD management.
- **Link:** [https://doi.org/10.1038/s41467-026-72044-0](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41467-026-72044-0)

### 2. Meta-analysis of the uncultured gut microbiome across 11,115 global metagenomes reveals a candidate signature of health
- **Journal:** Cell Host & Microbe (IF ~30)
- **Highlights:** This global meta-analysis of 11,115 human gut metagenomes from 39 countries and 13 noncommunicable disease cohorts (IBD, obesity, CFS, T2D, and others) identifies the uncultured bacterial genus CAG-170 as the strongest health-associated lineage across geographies and disease contexts — its abundance is consistently higher in healthy individuals and is the most central taxon in ecological networks of healthy microbiomes. CAG-170 species encode high vitamin B12 biosynthesis capacity, broad carbohydrate-degrading enzyme repertoires, and cross-feeding potential, positioning them as keystone health-promoting taxa. The study provides a globally validated health signature from the uncultured fraction of the gut microbiome, offering a candidate probiotic target and a pan-disease health biomarker that transcends any single disease or cohort.
- **Link:** [https://www.cell.com/cell-host-microbe/fulltext/S1931-3128(26)00038-7](https://www.cell.com/cell-host-microbe/fulltext/S1931-3128(26)00038-7)

### 3. The Gut Microbiome at the Onset of Inflammatory Bowel Disease: A Systematic Review and Unified Bioinformatic Synthesis
- **Journal:** Gastroenterology (IF ~29)
- **Highlights:** This study addresses the replication crisis in IBD microbiome research by re-analysing raw 16S rRNA amplicon data from 36 eligible studies (18 contributing to unified bioinformatic reanalysis, 24 to supplementary meta-analysis) through a single harmonized QIIME2 pipeline, including 1,743 patients (678 Crohn's disease, 399 ulcerative colitis, 130 healthy controls, 405 symptomatic controls). Core perturbations consistent across new-onset CD and UC are depletion of strict anaerobes (Firmicutes, *Faecalibacterium*, butyrate producers) and enrichment of oxygen-tolerant, orally-derived bacteria (*Streptococcus*, *Escherichia*), independent of methodology. The authors propose that altered mucosal oxygen availability is a unifying mechanism driving dysbiosis at IBD onset, pointing to luminal oxygen modulation as a novel therapeutic avenue and baseline microbiome profiling as a tool for pre-symptomatic risk stratification.
- **Link:** [https://www.gastrojournal.org/article/S0016-5085(25)06015-9/fulltext](https://www.gastrojournal.org/article/S0016-5085(25)06015-9/fulltext)

### 4. Gut microbiota and diet in colorectal cancer: Converging determinants of carcinogenesis
- **Journal:** Gut Microbes (IF ~12)
- **Highlights:** This comprehensive review synthesises how diet and the gut microbiota act as converging and mutually reinforcing determinants of colorectal cancer carcinogenesis: Western-pattern diets (high fat/sugar, low fibre) directly promote CRC-associated microbial blooms (*Fusobacterium nucleatum*, pks⁺ *E. coli*, enterotoxigenic *Bacteroides fragilis*) while simultaneously depleting protective species (butyrate-producing Firmicutes), creating a self-amplifying dysbiotic state that promotes genotoxicity, chronic inflammation, and epithelial barrier disruption. The authors detail specific oncogenic mechanisms — colibactin-mediated DNA double-strand breaks, ETBF toxin NF-κB activation, and Fusobacterium adhesin FadA-β-catenin signalling — and contrast these with anti-CRC pathways mediated by dietary fibre fermentation to butyrate and secondary bile acid regulation. Dietary modulation, microbiome-targeted prebiotics/probiotics, and FMT are discussed as integrative preventive and adjuvant-therapeutic strategies.
- **Link:** [https://www.tandfonline.com/toc/kgmi20/18/1](https://www.tandfonline.com/toc/kgmi20/18/1)

### 5. Early postnatal antibiotic-associated gut microbiota alterations might promote long-term lipid metabolism via brown adipose tissue metabolic programming
- **Journal:** Gut Microbes (IF ~12)
- **Highlights:** Using a neonatal mouse model of broad-spectrum antibiotic exposure during the critical gut colonization window, this study demonstrates that early-life microbiota disruption persistently reprogrammes brown adipose tissue (BAT) thermogenic capacity and systemic lipid metabolism through a microbiota–BAT axis operating via gut-derived metabolic signals. Antibiotic-treated neonates exhibited altered SCFA profiles and reduced bile acid diversity, correlating with sustained changes in BAT UCP1 expression, altered fatty acid oxidation gene programs, and modified white adipose tissue beiging. The findings position early-life microbiota as an essential epigenetic regulator of adipose tissue identity, with implications for understanding how perinatal antibiotic exposure contributes to metabolic syndrome and obesity risk in later life.
- **Link:** [https://www.tandfonline.com/toc/kgmi20/18/1](https://www.tandfonline.com/toc/kgmi20/18/1)

### 6. Intestinal epithelial Syndecan-1 maintains mucosal homeostasis in inflammatory bowel disease by enhancing Faecalibacterium prausnitzii biofilm formation
- **Journal:** Gut Microbes (IF ~12)
- **Highlights:** This study identifies Syndecan-1 (SDC1), a heparan sulfate/chondroitin sulfate proteoglycan, as an epithelial scaffold for *Faecalibacterium prausnitzii* colonisation: Sdc1 knockout mice showed selective depletion of F. prausnitzii as their most significantly altered microbiome change, while exogenous glycosaminoglycans (heparan/chondroitin sulfate) rescued F. prausnitzii biofilm formation and cobalamin biosynthesis in vitro. Human IBD biopsies confirm a positive SDC1–F. prausnitzii mucosal correlation and inverse correlation with bacterial translocation and LPS⁺ cell infiltration, establishing SDC1 as a host molecular bridge between epithelial integrity and the most consistently IBD-depleted commensal. The findings suggest SDC1 supplementation or preservation as a strategy to restore F. prausnitzii colonisation in IBD.
- **Link:** [https://www.tandfonline.com/toc/kgmi20/18/1](https://www.tandfonline.com/toc/kgmi20/18/1)

### 7. Gut microbiome composition and function reflect socioeconomic deprivation *(bonus — npj Biofilms and Microbiomes, not on primary list)*
- **Journal:** npj Biofilms and Microbiomes (Nature family)
- **Highlights:** This shotgun metagenomic analysis of 1,390 women from the TwinsUK cohort links area-level socioeconomic deprivation (Townsend Deprivation Index) to measurable gut microbiome shifts: higher deprivation is associated with reduced alpha diversity, depletion of SCFA-producing Firmicutes (particularly *Roseburia*, butyrate producers), and disruption of energy-regulating metabolic pathways. Two species — *Lawsonibacter* sp. NSJ_51 and *Intestinimonas massiliensis* — partly mediate associations between deprivation and both anxiety and type 2 diabetes risk, positioning the gut microbiome as a biological channel linking socioeconomic stressors to downstream mental and metabolic health disparities. The study establishes a "sociobiome" signal independent of diet, BMI, and medication, with public-health implications for microbiome-informed health equity interventions.
- **Link:** [https://doi.org/10.1038/s41522-026-00917-9](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41522-026-00917-9)
