### 1. Microbiota-derived metabolites as modulators of cancer immunotherapy response
- **Journal:** Nature Communications (IF ~17)
- **Published:** ~April 24–28, 2026 (newly indexed)
- **Highlights:** Toner-Bartelds, Mimpen, Parra-Martinez et al. provide a comprehensive mechanistic synthesis of how the gut microbiota shapes cancer immunotherapy outcomes through its metabolite repertoire. The review maps the mechanistic routes by which SCFAs (butyrate, propionate, acetate), secondary bile acids, tryptophan-derived indoles, polyamines, and urolithins modulate innate immune cells (macrophage polarization, dendritic cell maturation, NK cell activity), adaptive immunity (CD8+ T cell exhaustion, Treg induction, Th17 balance), and tumour cells directly. Particular attention is given to the methodological toolkit — untargeted metabolomics, stable isotope tracing, germ-free colonisation, and patient stool biobanking — needed to establish causal links from specific microbial taxa to specific metabolites to specific immunotherapy response endpoints. The paper identifies butyrate-mediated HDAC inhibition and the AhR-indole axis as the best-characterised immunomodulatory circuits, while noting that secondary bile acids and polyamines represent high-priority understudied targets given their abundance and receptor diversity.
- **Link:** [DOI: 10.1038/s41467-026-72178-1](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-72178-1)

### 2. Human lifestyle-associated factors modulate the gut resistome
- **Journal:** mSystems (IF ~6)
- **Published:** ~April 10–15, 2026 (newly indexed)
- **Highlights:** This large cross-population study challenges the dominant narrative that antibiotic prescription is the primary driver of human gut antimicrobial resistance gene (ARG) burden, demonstrating instead that the gut resistome is shaped by a constellation of lifestyle factors linked to the urbanisation gradient. Using metagenomic data from populations spanning hunter-gatherer to fully urbanised lifestyles, the authors show that subsistence strategy (pastoralist vs. agricultural vs. urban), dietary composition, zoonotic antibiotic exposure via livestock-rearing, and host metabolic health each explain significant variance in gut ARG prevalence and diversity, often comparable to or exceeding the contribution of measured antibiotic courses. Urbanisation consistently increased total ARG load and reduced resistome diversity relative to traditional populations, paralleling reductions in microbial diversity. Global ARG distribution patterns cluster geographically, not just by antibiotic policy, implicating environmental and dietary transmission routes. These findings reframe gut resistome surveillance as a one-health challenge requiring lifestyle and environmental data, not only antibiotic prescription records.
- **Link:** [DOI: 10.1128/msystems.01458-25](https://journals.asm.org/doi/10.1128/msystems.01458-25)

### 3. EB1010, a putative Christensenellaceae-derived microbial peptide, exhibits anti-inflammatory activity and therapeutic potential in ulcerative colitis
- **Journal:** Gut Microbes (IF ~12)
- **Published:** April 25, 2026
- **Highlights:** Orsini Delgado, Di Pasquale et al. identify and characterise EB1010, a secreted peptide encoded by members of the Christensenellaceae family — one of the most heritable and consistently IBD-depleted gut bacterial families. In LPS-stimulated macrophage and epithelial co-culture models, EB1010 suppressed TNF-α and IL-6 production while enhancing IL-10 secretion and tight junction protein expression (ZO-1, occludin), indicating dual anti-inflammatory and barrier-protective activity. In the DSS murine colitis model, oral administration of EB1010 significantly reduced colon histological damage scores, myeloperoxidase activity, and endoscopic severity relative to vehicle controls, at a potency approaching 5-ASA. Mechanistic analysis implicated NF-κB pathway suppression downstream of TLR4. The authors propose EB1010 as a lead compound in a new class of microbiome-derived peptide therapeutics, complementing next-generation probiotic strategies that seek to restore Christensenellaceae abundance directly.
- **Link:** [DOI: 10.1080/19490976.2026.2660891](https://www.tandfonline.com/journals/kgmi20)

### 4. Sex modulates the long-term effects of delivery mode on microbiota–gut barrier crosstalk and colitis susceptibility in mice
- **Journal:** Gut Microbes (IF ~12)
- **Published:** April 27, 2026
- **Highlights:** Maitan Santos, Chassaing et al. report the first systematic examination of biological sex as a moderating variable in delivery mode-associated gut phenotypes, using C-section (CSD) and vaginally delivered (VD) mouse cohorts followed to 6 months of age. Both CSD male and female mice showed early perturbations in gut microbiota composition relative to VD, with reduced Lachnospiraceae and Bifidobacterium abundance in the neonatal period; however, by 3–6 months, male CSD mice retained significantly lower Lachnospiraceae abundance, impaired mucus layer thickness, and higher intestinal permeability (FITC-dextran assay), while female CSD mice partially recovered. Upon DSS challenge, male CSD mice developed significantly more severe colitis (disease activity index, histological score, cytokine profile) than female CSD or VD controls. Microbiota transfer experiments recapitulated the sex-differential colitis phenotype, confirming a microbiota-mediated mechanism. These findings have implications for clinical understanding of C-section-associated health risks and suggest sex-stratified analysis is warranted in birth cohort microbiome studies.
- **Link:** [DOI: 10.1080/19490976.2026.2659874](https://www.tandfonline.com/journals/kgmi20)

### 5. Gut microbiota translocation and dissemination in health: can it be beneficial and purposeful?
- **Journal:** Gut Microbes (IF ~12)
- **Published:** April 25, 2026
- **Highlights:** Udayan, Bhaskaran et al. challenge the prevailing dogma that gut bacterial translocation is exclusively a sign of barrier failure and infection risk. The review synthesises accumulating evidence from germ-free colonisation studies, metagenomics of blood and sterile tissues, and murine imaging experiments showing that low-level translocation of commensal bacteria (particularly Lactobacillaceae, Akkermansia muciniphila, and select Lachnospiraceae) occurs in ostensibly healthy hosts and may serve coordinated physiological functions: antigen delivery to mesenteric lymph nodes for immune education, seeding of extra-intestinal regulatory immune niches, and maintenance of systemic immune tone via circulating bacterial DNA fragments. A proposed "purposeful translocation" threshold model distinguishes homeostatic translocation (low diversity, intact phagocytic clearance, no systemic inflammation) from pathological translocation (high load, Gram-negative dominance, systemic inflammatory response). The review discusses how age, diet, microbiome composition, and intestinal transit interact to maintain vs. disrupt this balance, with implications for interpreting the circulating microbiome detected in healthy human blood and its perturbation in chronic diseases.
- **Link:** [DOI: 10.1080/19490976.2026.2659221](https://www.tandfonline.com/journals/kgmi20)

### 6. Distinct prophage infections in colorectal cancer-associated Bacteroides fragilis *(bonus — Communications Medicine)*
- **Journal:** Communications Medicine (Nature family, IF ~15; not in target journal list)
- **Published:** February 7, 2026 (newly confirmed for this knowledge base)
- **Highlights:** Damgaard, Sørensen et al. (University of Southern Denmark / Odense University Hospital) identify integrated bacteriophages (prophages) in Bacteroides fragilis strains as a potential explanatory link in the long-noted but mechanistically unresolved B. fragilis–colorectal cancer association. Prophage-positive B. fragilis was detected approximately twice as frequently in stool and tumour-adjacent tissue of colorectal cancer patients relative to healthy controls in a discovery cohort, with replication in 877 individuals from multiple countries. Prophage integration within the B. fragilis genome specifically disrupts and upregulates flanking virulence-associated loci (enterotoxin fragilysin / BFT genes and outer membrane protein clusters), providing a mechanistic hypothesis for how a commensal bacterium switches to a cancer-promoting mode. The phage was not detected in the environment, suggesting it is a gut-specific element transmitted within the human microbiome. While causality is not yet established, the findings introduce gut virome-commensal interactions as a new research axis for CRC pathogenesis, complementing the bacteriome-focused literature.
- **Link:** [DOI: 10.1038/s43856-026-01403-1](https://www.nature.com/articles/s43856-026-01403-1)
