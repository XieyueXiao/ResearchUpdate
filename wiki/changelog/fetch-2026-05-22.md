### 1. Epigenetic phase variation in the gut microbiome enhances bacterial adaptation

- **Journal:** Cell Host & Microbe (IF ~30)
- **Published:** 2026-05-19
- **Authors:** Mi Ni, Katerina Junker, Yujie Liu, Yu Fan, Yangmei Li, Wanjin Qiao, Xue-Song Zhang, Magdalena Ksiezarek, Edward A. Mead, Alan Tourancheau, Wenyan Jiang, Martin J. Blaser, Raphael H. Valdivia, Lauren E. Davey, Gang Fang (Mount Sinai / Duke)
- **Highlights:** This study provides the first systematic, genome-wide characterisation of epigenetic phase variation (ePV) — reversible, heritable gene-expression switching driven by bacterial DNA methylation — across the human gut microbiome. Using a combination of long-read metagenomic sequencing (to detect structural rearrangements in DNA methyltransferase genes), large-scale short-read public metagenome analysis, and single-molecule methylation quantification, the authors catalogued ePVs in infant and adult gut communities. Longitudinal stool samples from infants before and after antibiotic treatment, as well as FMT donor–recipient pairs, revealed genome-wide ePV dynamics: perturbation induces rapid, reversible methylation-state switching in multiple species simultaneously, generating phenotypic heterogeneity within genetically identical populations — a "bet-hedging" strategy allowing a fraction of cells to pre-adapt to environmental shifts. In-depth characterisation of an *Akkermansia muciniphila* isolate from an infant demonstrated that ePV modulates surface-adhesion and stress-response gene networks. The work establishes a previously hidden epigenetic layer of microbiome biology and proposes that ePV heterogeneity may underlie the inconsistent clinical outcomes of probiotics and FMT across individuals, pointing toward methylation-state profiling as a future biomarker.
- **Link:** [doi:10.1016/j.chom.2026.04.019](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.chom.2026.04.019)

### 2. Early life bacteria and sibling exposure associate with restoration of the infant gut microbiome after cesarean section

- **Journal:** Nature Communications (IF ~17)
- **Published:** article in press (accepted 2026-03-13; online publication date not yet confirmed; not captured in any prior fetch)
- **Authors:** Jiang J., Poulsen C.S., Boulund U. et al. (COPSAC / University of Copenhagen)
- **Highlights:** Cesarean section (CS) delivery persistently disrupts early-life gut microbiome composition and has been linked to elevated asthma risk. Using the COPSAC2010 Danish birth cohort (N = 700), the authors developed a "restoration score" — a quantitative index of how closely the 1-year gut microbiome of CS-born infants resembles that of vaginally delivered peers — and then identified its environmental and microbial predictors in the 1-week stool microbiome. Having older siblings was the strongest independent predictor of a higher restoration score, an effect mediated by elevated early-life abundances of restoration-associated bacterial taxa transmitted via household contact; the mechanism is consistent with ecological succession seeded by sibling-derived microbes that partially compensate for vaginal inoculation missed at CS birth. The primary finding was replicated in the independent Canadian CHILD birth cohort. Critically, higher restoration scores in CS-born infants tracked with lower rates of subsequent asthma diagnosis, directly linking microbiome composition at 1 year to a clinically meaningful respiratory outcome. The study supports targeted sibling-exposure or microbiome-seeding interventions as scalable strategies to normalise early-life gut colonisation after CS delivery.
- **Link:** [doi:10.1038/s41467-026-71185-6](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41467-026-71185-6)
