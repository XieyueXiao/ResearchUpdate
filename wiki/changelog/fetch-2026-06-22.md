## Papers Found (2026-06-22)

### 1. Phase variation-mediated bacterial functional plasticity as a lens for understanding microbe–host interactions
- **Journal:** Gut Microbes (IF ~12)
- **Highlights:** This open-access review by Shaqed Carasso et al. synthesises how phase variation — the reversible ON/OFF switching of gene expression, predominantly via DNA segment inversions in Bacteroidales — shapes microbe–host interactions in the gut. The authors frame surface-molecule diversity generated by phase variation as a primary mechanism by which dominant gut commensals present a continually shifting interface to the immune system, competing microbes, and bacteriophages. Building on the group's prior discovery that intestinal inflammation and phage activity shift DNA inversion states of B. fragilis and other Bacteroidales (Cell Host & Microbe 2024), this review articulates phase variation as a population-level bet-hedging strategy with direct relevance to dysbiosis, colonisation resistance, and the interpretation of microbiome surveys where functional plasticity may outpace taxonomic variation.

### 2. High thrombin activity associated with Crohn's disease induces microbiota pathogenicity contributing to mucosal inflammation
- **Journal:** Gut Microbes (IF ~12)
- **Highlights:** Le Cosquer et al. demonstrate that the elevated mucosal thrombin activity characteristic of active Crohn's disease directly reprogrammes gut microbiota towards a more pathogenic phenotype, amplifying mucosal inflammation. Using ex vivo bacterial cultures exposed to CD-relevant thrombin concentrations and germ-free/gnotobiotic mouse models, the authors show that thrombin-conditioned bacteria exhibit enhanced adherence, invasion of epithelial cells, and pro-inflammatory cytokine induction compared to controls; host protease-activated receptor (PAR) signalling is implicated in bidirectional crosstalk. The findings identify host-derived coagulation proteases as underappreciated shapers of gut microbiota virulence potential, opening a new angle on the CD inflammation-dysbiosis feedback loop and suggesting PAR-targeted or thrombin-inhibitory strategies as adjunct microbiome therapies.
- **Link:** [doi:10.1080/19490976.2026.XXXXXX](https://www.tandfonline.com/journals/kgmi20) *(DOI pending full indexing; published online 2026-06-17)*

### 3. The gut microbiome and ageing trajectories: mechanisms and clinical implications *(bonus — Nature Reviews Endocrinology)*
- **Journal:** Nature Reviews Endocrinology (IF ~40; not on primary list)
- **Highlights:** This comprehensive review positions the gut microbiome as a key modulator of ageing trajectories, operating through multiple gut–organ axes (gut–brain, gut–liver, gut–immune, gut–musculoskeletal). Ageing is consistently accompanied by declining microbial diversity and loss of SCFA-producing taxa (Faecalibacterium, Roseburia, Bifidobacterium), weakening intestinal barrier integrity and sustaining the low-grade pro-inflammatory state termed inflammaging; the review distinguishes population-specific microbiome aging patterns and evaluates the evidence for causal rather than correlative microbiome contributions to frailty, sarcopenia, cognitive decline, and metabolic disease. Clinical implications include multiparametric microbiome indexes as frailty/mortality predictors, and the review critically appraises interventional evidence for probiotics, prebiotics, FMT, and dietary strategies aimed at restoring a "younger" microbiome signature.
- **Link:** [doi:10.1038/s41574-026-01236-x](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41574-026-01236-x) *(Nat Rev Endocrinol vol. 22, pp. 323–337, June 2026)*
