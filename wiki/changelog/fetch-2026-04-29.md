### 1. The gut microbiome as a fingerprint of antibiotic use history

- **Journal:** Nature Medicine (IF ~82)
- **Published:** 14 April 2026
- **Highlights:** Using shotgun metagenomic profiles from 14,979 individuals linked to individual-level prescription data from the Swedish National Prescribed Drug Register spanning eight years, Björk et al. demonstrate that the gut microbiome encodes a readable record of prior antibiotic exposure. Antibiotic use in the year before fecal sampling was associated with the greatest reduction in species diversity, but significant compositional shifts persisted for antibiotic courses taken 1–4 and even 4–8 years earlier; clindamycin, fluoroquinolones, and flucloxacillin accounted for 10–15% of species-level abundance changes at the 4–8 year window, while the most commonly prescribed agent, penicillin V, showed only small and transient effects. The findings establish antibiotic class and recency of use as determinants of long-term microbiome dysbiosis and provide a population-level evidence base for antibiotic stewardship policies aimed at preserving microbiome integrity.
- **Link:** [10.1038/s41591-026-04360-3](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41591-026-04360-3)

### 2. Multi-omics reveal vitamin D regulation of immune–gut microbiome interactions and tolerogenic pathways in inflammatory bowel disease

- **Journal:** Cell Reports Medicine (IF ~11)
- **Published:** 26 March 2026
- **Highlights:** A Mayo Clinic–led clinical trial enrolled 48 IBD patients with documented vitamin D insufficiency and administered weekly high-dose supplementation for 12 weeks, capturing stool metagenomics, serum metabolomics, flow cytometry, and BCR/TCR immune repertoire data at baseline and endpoint. Vitamin D supplementation decreased disease activity indices and shifted the dominant immunoglobulin coating of gut bacteria from IgG toward IgA, with increased IgA binding to Lachnospiraceae and Blautia and decreased IgG binding to Proteobacteria and Enterococcaceae. Multi-omic integration revealed that vitamin D enhanced BAFF-mediated plasmacytoid dendritic cell–B cell signalling, expanded α4β7+CX3CR1+ regulatory T and B cells, and remodelled BCR/TCR clonotype repertoires linked to specific microbiota, collectively pointing to restoration of mucosal immune tolerance as a plausible mechanism for the clinical benefit of vitamin D in IBD.
- **Link:** [10.1016/j.xcrm.2026.101988](https://www.cell.com/cell-reports-medicine/fulltext/S2666-3791(26)00120-5)

### 3. An oral–gut microbial metabolite links Fusobacterium nucleatum to aggravated myocardial ischemia–reperfusion injury

- **Journal:** Gut Microbes (IF ~12)
- **Published:** 27 April 2026
- **Highlights:** Li et al. report that Fusobacterium nucleatum, an oral pathobiont increasingly recognised as a gut coloniser in disease states, transmits a defined metabolite via the oral-gut axis that significantly worsens myocardial ischemia-reperfusion (I/R) injury in rodent models. Germ-free and antibiotic-treated mice colonised with F. nucleatum showed larger infarct areas and heightened inflammatory infiltration after I/R surgery compared with controls; metabolomic profiling of portal and cardiac blood identified the responsible metabolite and pharmacological blockade partially rescued the cardiac phenotype. The findings extend the pathological reach of F. nucleatum beyond its established roles in colorectal cancer and periodontal disease, implicating the oral-gut axis as a novel mediator of acute cardiovascular injury.
- **Link:** [https://www.tandfonline.com/journals/kgmi20](https://www.tandfonline.com/journals/kgmi20) *(full DOI pending indexing; search Gut Microbes + Li + Fusobacterium + 2026)*

### 4. Invasion of gut-derived Escherichia coli extracellular vesicles exacerbates myocardial ischemia/reperfusion injury

- **Journal:** Gut Microbes (IF ~12)
- **Published:** 2026
- **Highlights:** This study demonstrates that bacterial extracellular vesicles (EVs) produced by gut-resident Escherichia coli can translocate through the damaged gut barrier during ischemic conditions and home to cardiac tissue, where they amplify myocardial I/R injury. Using fluorescently labelled EVs in mouse I/R models and ex vivo cardiac perfusion systems, the authors show that E. coli EVs activate TLR4/NF-κB–dependent inflammatory cascades in cardiomyocytes, increasing apoptosis and reducing contractile function. Antibiotic pre-treatment targeting gram-negative gut bacteria or blocking EV translocation with gut barrier stabilisers markedly reduced infarct size, establishing gut-derived bacterial EVs as a previously unrecognised route of gut-heart crosstalk and a potential therapeutic target.
- **Link:** [10.1080/19490976.2026.2635818](https://doi.org/10.1080/19490976.2026.2635818)

### 5. Amelioration of colitis through restored gut ecology using *Christensenella intestinihominis* AF73-05CM02 as a probiotic in mice

- **Journal:** Communications Biology (Springer Nature; IF ~6)
- **Published:** 2026
- **Highlights:** Wu et al. show that *Christensenella intestinihominis*, a member of the health-associated Christensenellaceae family that is consistently depleted in ulcerative colitis patients, can be administered as a live biotherapeutic to restore gut ecological balance. The candidate strain AF73-05CM02, isolated from a healthy Chinese volunteer and characterised by whole-genome sequencing, significantly reduced clinical and histological colitis scores in DSS-treated mice by competitively excluding pathogenic Enterobacteriaceae, producing exopolysaccharides that reinforce the mucus layer, and skewing mucosal macrophage polarisation toward an anti-inflammatory M2 phenotype. This ecology-based approach—targeting community composition rather than single-metabolite supplementation—provides proof of concept for next-generation Christensenellaceae probiotics in IBD.
- **Link:** [10.1038/s42003-026-10031-3](https://doi.org/10.1038/s42003-026-10031-3)
