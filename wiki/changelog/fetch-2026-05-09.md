### 1. Biogeography-associated emergence of enhanced oxygen tolerance in the abundant human gut commensal *Segatella copri*

- **Journal:** Cell Host & Microbe (IF ~30)
- **Highlights:** El Mouali et al. demonstrate that a subset of *Segatella copri* strains (formerly *Prevotella copri*) has acquired the OxyR transcription regulator via horizontal gene transfer, conferring up to 1,000-fold greater oxygen tolerance than strains lacking it. Whole-genome sequencing and metagenomic analyses of global cohorts reveal that OxyR-positive strains are disproportionately prevalent in populations from industrialized countries, while OxyR-negative strains predominate in populations with traditional lifestyles in Africa and many parts of Asia. Mechanistically, OxyR upregulates a suite of antioxidant defense genes that allow *S. copri* to persist in the more oxygenated microenvironments associated with industrialized guts. The findings establish a concrete molecular mechanism by which a keystone mucosal commensal adapts to the altered gut redox landscape of Western-industrialized hosts, providing a potential explanation for the biogeographic variation in *S. copri*-associated disease phenotypes (e.g., rheumatoid arthritis, IBD).
- **Link:** [doi.org/10.1016/j.chom.2026.04.006](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.chom.2026.04.006)

### 2. Gut microbiota-derived indole-3-lactic acid suppresses anti-PD-1 efficacy in esophageal squamous cell carcinoma

- **Journal:** Cell Host & Microbe (IF ~30)
- **Highlights:** Analysis of 122 fecal samples from esophageal squamous cell carcinoma (ESCC) patients receiving neoadjuvant anti-PD-1 immunotherapy identified enrichment of *Ligilactobacillus salivarius* in non-responders. Using humanized-microbiome orthotopic ESCC mouse models and single-cell RNA sequencing, the authors confirm that *L. salivarius*-produced indole-3-lactic acid (ILA) activates the aryl hydrocarbon receptor (AhR), suppressing NF-κB signaling and triggering terminal exhaustion of NKG7⁺CD8⁺ Tpex cells at the tumor site. Targeted ablation of *L. salivarius* or pharmacological AhR inhibition restored anti-tumor immunity and improved checkpoint blockade efficacy. The study maps a gut microbiota → metabolite → AhR → T cell exhaustion axis as a tractable mechanism of immunotherapy resistance in ESCC, with potential relevance to other cancers associated with oral-gut microbial translocation.
- **Link:** [doi.org/10.1016/j.chom.2026.02.019](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.chom.2026.02.019)

### 3. Gut microbiota in chronic inflammation: the interplay with lipid mediators

- **Journal:** Gut Microbes (IF ~12)
- **Highlights:** Bao et al. provide a comprehensive mechanistic review of bidirectional crosstalk between gut microbial communities and bioactive lipid mediators in chronic inflammatory disease. The review covers how dysbiotic shifts alter production of pro-inflammatory eicosanoids (prostaglandins, leukotrienes), sphingolipids, and lysophospholipids, while SCFA-producing commensals promote synthesis of specialized pro-resolving mediators (SPMs) including resolvins, protectins, and maresins. Specific mechanistic nodes are detailed: butyrate-mediated HDAC inhibition of arachidonic acid pathway enzymes; secondary bile acids signalling through TGR5/FXR to suppress IL-6-driven leukotriene synthesis; and microbially-processed dietary lipids (omega-3-derived SPM precursors) whose availability depends heavily on microbiome composition. Therapeutic implications for IBD, metabolic syndrome, and neuroinflammation are discussed.
- **Link:** [doi.org/10.1080/19490976.2026.2510184](https://doi.org/10.1080/19490976.2026.2510184)

### 4. A novel bile salt hydrolase-producing *Ligilactobacillus salivarius* prevents diet-induced obesity via regulation of bile acid metabolism and glucagon-like peptide 1 restoration

- **Journal:** Gut Microbes (IF ~12)
- **Highlights:** Lv et al. characterize a BSH-producing *Ligilactobacillus salivarius* strain isolated from healthy human feces and test its capacity to prevent diet-induced obesity (DIO) in a high-fat-diet mouse model. Gavage of the strain over 12 weeks significantly attenuated body weight gain, adiposity, and metabolic dysfunction compared to vehicle-treated controls. Mechanistically, increased BSH activity shifted the bile acid pool toward unconjugated and secondary bile acids that preferentially activate TGR5 in enteroendocrine L-cells, restoring GLP-1 secretion and improving glucose homeostasis. FXR-mediated signaling in the liver was also enhanced, reducing lipogenic gene expression. 16S rRNA sequencing and targeted metabolomics confirmed strain engraftment and bile acid remodeling, identifying the BSH→bile acid→TGR5/FXR→GLP-1 axis as the primary anti-obesity mechanism.
- **Link:** [doi.org/10.1080/19490976.2026.2509857](https://doi.org/10.1080/19490976.2026.2509857)

### 5. Host metabolism shapes the intestinal microbiota: a top-down paradigm of environmental selection pressure

- **Journal:** Gut Microbes (IF ~12)
- **Highlights:** Ma et al. present a conceptual review reframing the direction of gut microbiota research: rather than focusing exclusively on how microbes and diet ("bottom-up" factors) shape host physiology, the authors systematically document how host metabolic state acts as a dominant "top-down" environmental selection pressure that sculpts microbial community composition. Key metabolic factors reviewed include: luminal oxygen tension (elevated in IBD, selecting for aero-tolerant Proteobacteria); primary bile acid composition (shaped by host FXR/TGR5 activity, selecting for BSH-expressing taxa); intestinal pH and transit time (altered by host hormonal and autonomic regulation); mucosal glycan availability (linked to host metabolic status and immune tone, selecting for mucin-degraders); and host-secreted antimicrobial peptides. The framework has direct implications for understanding why systemic metabolic diseases (obesity, T2D) are associated with convergent dysbiotic signatures even across genetically diverse human populations.
- **Link:** [doi.org/10.1080/19490976.2026.2511042](https://doi.org/10.1080/19490976.2026.2511042)
