### 1. Large-scale metagenomics of the canine gut microbiome reveals age-associated composition shifts and a microbiome-based aging clock

- **Journal:** Nature Communications (IF ~17)
- **Published:** 2026-05-19
- **Authors:** Dog Aging Project Precision cohort team
- **Highlights:** The largest population-wide study of the canine gut microbiome to date, coupling fecal shotgun metagenomic sequencing from over 900 companion dogs of diverse breeds and living conditions across the United States with comprehensive phenotypic surveys, environmental questionnaires, and clinical laboratory data. Analyses identified a gradual, consistent shift in gut microbiome composition with host age, with specific microbial taxa enriched or depleted in older dogs in a manner that parallels microbial aging signatures observed in humans. Using random forest regression with 10-fold cross-validation on data from healthy dogs only, the authors developed a metagenomics-based "aging clock" that predicts a dog's biological age from its gut microbial features — extending the concept of microbiome-derived aging biomarkers to a non-human companion-animal model. Beyond age, microbiome composition associated with diet, behaviour, health status, and living conditions, demonstrating that the companion dog is a tractable, well-monitored population model for studying microbiome–ageing interactions in vivo.

### 2. Gut microbes shape the body-wide distribution of glucuronidated metabolites

- **Journal:** Nature Communications (IF ~17)
- **Published:** 2026-05-20
- **Authors:** Nina R. Boyle, Josh J. Sekela, Andrew D. Patterson
- **Highlights:** This study establishes a systems-level framework for mapping how gut microbial β-glucuronidase (GUS) activity controls the body-wide distribution of glucuronidated metabolites — the large class of compounds conjugated to glucuronic acid for excretion. Using germ-free mouse colonization experiments supported by multi-tissue metabolomics and human clinical data, Boyle, Sekela, and Patterson demonstrate that the presence and composition of the gut microbiome fundamentally determines whether glucuronidated compounds are excreted in feces or de-conjugated by GUS enzymes and reabsorbed as the active parent compound. Both colonization status and dietary composition modulate GUS enzyme abundance and activity, meaning that the same ingested compound can have markedly different systemic exposures across individuals depending on their microbiome. The work has direct relevance to pharmacomicrobiomics (drug metabolism and toxicity variation) and to interpreting metabolomics studies that do not account for microbial glucuronidase activity.
- **Link:** [Nature Communications — Boyle et al. 2026](https://www.nature.com/ncomms/) *(full DOI pending indexing; confirmed via search metadata May 20, 2026)*

### 3. Inorganic nitrogen metabolic reprogramming of the gut microbiome drives fecal microbiota transplantation outcomes in ulcerative colitis

- **Journal:** Nature Communications (IF ~17)
- **Published:** 2026-05-20
- **Authors:** [authors not yet indexed]
- **Highlights:** This study identifies inorganic nitrogen utilization capacity as a critical determinant of microbial community assembly and donor microbe engraftment in the oxidative-stress-rich colonic environment of ulcerative colitis (UC). Working from the observation that reactive nitrogen species in the inflamed colon create a colonization barrier for donor microbiota during FMT, the authors engineered PM-mix14 — a probiotic-metabolite consortia designed to catalyze the conversion of excess reactive nitrogen species, promote L-glutamate biosynthesis, and boost ATP production, thereby relieving oxidative nitrogen stress. In multiple male murine models of colitis, PM-mix14 supplementation during FMT significantly improved microbial engraftment fidelity (greater donor-recipient similarity post-transplant), which correlated with enhanced anti-inflammatory immune responses and attenuated colonic pathology. The findings provide a mechanistic rationale for pre-conditioning the recipient gut environment to maximize FMT efficacy and support combining microbiome-metabolic interventions with standard FMT protocols for IBD.
- **Link:** [doi:10.1038/s41467-026-73290-y](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41467-026-73290-y)

### 4. Minimally invasive capsule-string device enables spatially resolved microbiome profiling across the upper gastrointestinal tract

- **Journal:** Gut Microbes (IF ~12)
- **Published:** 2026-05-19
- **Authors:** Kathryn Garvey et al.
- **Highlights:** Garvey et al. describe a novel "capsule-string" sampling device that allows minimally invasive, spatially resolved profiling of the microbiome across multiple anatomical sites of the upper gastrointestinal tract — a region that remains poorly characterised relative to the fecal/colonic microbiome due to the inaccessibility of traditional sampling methods. The device couples a swallowable capsule with an externally retained string that positions multiple sampling units at distinct GI segments, enabling simultaneous collection from sites such as the duodenum, jejunum, and ileum in a single non-endoscopic procedure. Microbiome profiling of paired samples from different GI regions revealed region-specific microbial communities distinct from stool, with characteristic taxa enriched in each segment. The platform offers a scalable, patient-tolerable alternative to endoscopic sampling for studying small-intestinal dysbiosis in diseases such as SIBO, Crohn's disease, and coeliac disease.
- **Link:** [Gut Microbes — Garvey et al. 2026](https://www.tandfonline.com/journals/kgmi20) *(full DOI pending indexing; confirmed via search metadata May 19, 2026)*

### 5. *Limosilactobacillus reuteri* promotes melatonin release from human intestinal organoids via 5′-ectonucleotidase activity

- **Journal:** Gut Microbes (IF ~12)
- **Published:** 2026-05-19
- **Authors:** Micah D. Forshee et al.
- **Highlights:** This study uses human intestinal organoids to demonstrate that *Limosilactobacillus reuteri* stimulates melatonin secretion from intestinal epithelial cells through a mechanism dependent on bacterial 5′-ectonucleotidase activity. 5′-ectonucleotidase on the bacterial surface cleaves extracellular nucleotides, generating extracellular adenosine, which in turn activates adenosine receptor signalling on intestinal epithelial cells to drive melatonin biosynthesis and release. The findings identify a novel gut-microbiome-to-enteroendocrine signalling axis — specifically for melatonin, a molecule with established roles in circadian rhythm regulation, gut motility, immune modulation, and mucosal protection — and add to a growing body of work on how *L. reuteri* modulates enteroendocrine cell function and gut hormone secretion. Because intestinal melatonin production is largely independent of the pineal gland and operates on diurnal cycles synchronized by both light and feeding cues, this microbiome-driven melatonin circuit may link probiotic colonization to circadian gut physiology.
- **Link:** [Gut Microbes — Forshee et al. 2026](https://www.tandfonline.com/journals/kgmi20) *(full DOI pending indexing; confirmed via search metadata May 19, 2026)*
