## Papers Found (2026-07-19)

Searched all 22 target journals via 25+ web search queries for gut microbiome papers published 2026-07-16–07-19, widened slightly beyond the normal 24h window to re-check for misses given three genuine gaps surfaced this cycle from the prior (2026-07-18) fetch. Found 4 confirmed new papers, all Gut Microbes:

### 1. From signals to systems: the epigenetic-microbiome-mitochondrial axis in IBD pathogenesis
- **Journal:** Gut Microbes (IF ~12)
- **Highlights:** Review by Nesa Kazemifard et al. synthesizing how microbial short-chain fatty acids and other metabolites act as epigenetic effectors — inhibiting histone deacetylases, donating acetyl/methyl groups for histone and DNA modification — while also intersecting with host mitochondrial function in intestinal epithelial cells. Frames IBD pathogenesis as an integrated "epigenetic-microbiome-mitochondrial axis" in which dysbiosis-driven epigenetic dysregulation and mitochondrial dysfunction reinforce one another to compromise barrier integrity and sustain inflammation, proposing this axis as a target for mechanistic and therapeutic investigation.
- **Link:** Gut Microbes (Taylor & Francis), published online ~2026-07-16/17; DOI unresolved (tandfonline.com returned HTTP 403 to automated fetch this cycle — see access note below)

### 2. Gut-derived genistein from Parabacteroides distasonis alleviates psoriatic inflammation via CD200-mediated NF-κB inhibition in mice
- **Journal:** Gut Microbes (IF ~12)
- **Highlights:** Demengjie Le et al. show that Parabacteroides distasonis, a common gut commensal, produces the isoflavone genistein as a metabolite that reaches the skin and dampens psoriatic inflammation in a mouse model. Mechanistically, genistein upregulates CD200 signaling in keratinocytes/immune cells, which suppresses NF-κB activation and downstream pro-inflammatory cytokine production, linking a specific gut bacterial metabolite to a gut-skin axis mechanism relevant to psoriasis.
- **Link:** Gut Microbes (Taylor & Francis), published online 2026-07-16; DOI unresolved (access blocked, see note below)

### 3. Eukaryotic and bacterial gut communities vary along a lifestyle-associated urbanization gradient: comparative analysis of Germany and Madagascar
- **Journal:** Gut Microbes (IF ~12)
- **Highlights:** Johanna Saalfrank et al. profile both bacterial and eukaryotic (parasite/protist) components of the gut microbiome across a lifestyle-associated urbanization gradient spanning industrialized Germany and rural/urban Madagascar. Report coordinated shifts in bacterial diversity and eukaryotic (parasite) prevalence with urbanization, arguing that cross-kingdom (bacteria + eukaryotes) profiling is needed to fully characterize how industrialization reshapes the human gut ecosystem — bacteria-only surveys likely understate the scope of lifestyle-driven microbiome change.
- **Link:** Gut Microbes (Taylor & Francis), published online 2026-07-16; DOI unresolved (access blocked, see note below)

### 4. Dynamic flexibility of the murine gut microbiota during morphine disturbance enables escape from the stable dysbiosis associated with addiction-like behavior
- **Journal:** Gut Microbes (IF ~12)
- **Highlights:** Izabella Sall et al. use a longitudinal voluntary oral morphine self-administration paradigm in mice to track gut microbial network dynamics through drug exposure and withdrawal. In compulsive (addiction-like) mice, morphine fragmented microbiota co-occurrence networks, which then reorganized into novel but less resilient configurations; non-compulsive mice's communities remained more interconnected during disturbance and reverted more fully post-morphine, suggesting microbiome network flexibility/resilience tracks with resistance to addiction-like behavior. Originated as a 2025-06-01 bioRxiv preprint (doi:10.1101/2025.06.01.657215); this is the peer-reviewed Gut Microbes version, published online 2026-07-17.
- **Link:** Gut Microbes (Taylor & Francis), published online 2026-07-17; DOI unresolved (access blocked, see note below); preprint: [bioRxiv](https://doi.org/10.1101/2025.06.01.657215)
