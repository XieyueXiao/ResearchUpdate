### 1. Gut microbiome is associated with recurrence-free survival in patients with resected high-risk melanoma receiving adjuvant immune checkpoint blockade
- **Journal:** Cell (IF ~64)
- **Highlights:** This phase 3 translational study analyzed pre-treatment stool microbiomes from 674 patients enrolled in CheckMate 915 (nivolumab + ipilimumab vs. nivolumab monotherapy; resected stage IIIB–IV melanoma across five geographic regions). Recurrence-free survival was associated with taxa including Eubacterium, Ruminococcus, Firmicutes, and Clostridium in region-specific and cross-region meta-analyses; predictive accuracy was highest when discovery and validation cohorts shared similar microbiome composition (AUC 0.78–0.94 in individuals with Jensen-Shannon divergence ≤ 0.11), establishing a "microbial fingerprint" dependency that explains prior cross-cohort replication failures. Microbiome composition was stable from pre-treatment to post-treatment, positioning baseline gut profiling as a clinically actionable biomarker for adjuvant immunotherapy selection in high-risk melanoma.
- **Link:** [https://doi.org/10.1016/j.cell.2026.03.429](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.cell.2026.03.429)

### 2. A drug–microbiome–drug interaction impacts co-prescribed medications for Parkinson's disease
- **Journal:** Nature Microbiology (IF ~28)
- **Highlights:** This study reveals that COMT inhibitors (e.g., tolcapone), co-prescribed with levodopa in Parkinson's disease, exert antibacterial properties that reshape the gut microbiome by killing susceptible bacteria and allowing bloom of Enterococcus faecalis, which encodes a levodopa-metabolizing enzyme (tyrosine decarboxylase) that degrades levodopa in the gut before it can reach the brain. The finding demonstrates that drug–drug interactions can be microbiome-mediated rather than hepatic, and that the gut microbiome can paradoxically diminish the therapeutic efficacy of a co-administered drug. Microbial features (Enterococcus abundance, COMT-I susceptibility profile) could predict individual variation in levodopa bioavailability and guide more targeted drug regimens.
- **Link:** [https://doi.org/10.1038/s41564-026-02299-2](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41564-026-02299-2)

### 3. Effector–host interactome map links type III secretion systems in healthy gut microbiomes to immune modulation
- **Journal:** Nature Microbiology (IF ~28)
- **Highlights:** The authors find that ~80% of Pseudomonadota (Proteobacteria) genomes from healthy human gut metagenomes encode intact type III secretion systems (T3SS), previously considered hallmarks of pathogens, and use machine learning plus experimental validation to construct a protein–protein meta-interactome map between commensal T3SS effectors and human host proteins. Commensal effectors display sequence and structural features distinct from pathogen effectors and preferentially target human immune-signalling hubs; effector-targeted protein neighbourhoods are enriched for genetic variation linked to autoimmune and metabolic disease. Metagenomic analysis reveals that commensal T3SS effectors are enriched in Crohn's disease patients but depleted in ulcerative colitis, implicating these secretion systems in disease-specific immune perturbations.
- **Link:** [https://doi.org/10.1038/s41564-025-02241-y](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41564-025-02241-y)

### 4. Mucin-degrading gut bacteria: context-dependent roles in intestinal homeostasis and disease
- **Journal:** Gut Microbes (IF ~12)
- **Highlights:** This comprehensive review examines the dual roles of key mucin-degrading gut bacteria — Akkermansia muciniphila, Bacteroides thetaiotaomicron, Mediterraneibacter (Ruminococcus) gnavus, and others — in intestinal health and disease, emphasising that their effects on gut barrier integrity, immune tone, and microbiota composition fluctuate dynamically based on host diet, immunity, and disease state rather than being uniformly protective or harmful. The authors detail the enzymatic machinery (mucinases, glycosidases, sialidases) enabling mucin degradation, the bioactive metabolites produced (SCFAs, indoles, secondary bile acids), and how their abundance and activity shift across conditions including IBD, IBS, and colorectal cancer. The review synthesises evidence that context — rather than species identity alone — determines whether mucin degraders promote or compromise intestinal homeostasis, with implications for microbiome-based therapeutic targeting of the mucus layer.
- **Link:** [https://doi.org/10.1080/19490976.2026.2614054](https://doi.org/10.1080/19490976.2026.2614054)

### 5. Microbiome-mediated colonization resistance to and countermeasures of Klebsiella pneumoniae
- **Journal:** Nature Communications (IF ~17)
- **Highlights:** This review synthesises recent mechanistic understanding of how the gut microbiota suppresses intestinal colonization by Klebsiella pneumoniae — a leading cause of hospital-acquired infections and an emerging contributor to non-infectious metabolic disease — through nutrient competition (particularly β-glucoside utilization), production of inhibitory metabolites, and stimulation of host immune defenses, with Klebsiella oxytoca highlighted as a particularly potent competitor. The paper systematically catalogues how K. pneumoniae overcomes colonization resistance via antimicrobial resistance acquisition, capsular/LPS immune evasion, type VI secretion system-mediated competitor killing, and metabolic adaptations. Emerging microbiome-based countermeasures — commensals, phages, peptides, metabolites, and monoclonal antibodies — are reviewed as promising therapeutic strategies to prevent colonization and downstream infection.
- **Link:** [https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-69690-9](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-69690-9)

### 6. The HUNT study identifies host genetic factors reproducibly associated with human gut microbiota composition *(bonus — Nature Genetics, not on primary list)*
- **Journal:** Nature Genetics (IF ~31)
- **Highlights:** A companion GWAS to the Swedish cohort paper captured in the 2026-05-02 fetch, this study of 12,652 individuals from the Norwegian Trøndelag Health Study (HUNT) identifies 12 reproducible SNP–species associations across six genomic loci, confirming known signals at LCT, ABO, and FUT2 and adding novel loci at HLA-DQB1, MUC12, SLC37A2, and FUT3-FUT6 — genes involved in mucosal layer composition, bile acid metabolism, and immune presentation. Follow-up Mendelian randomisation analyses support a causal effect of BMI on gut microbiota composition and link host–microbiota associations to the pathogenesis of celiac disease and hemorrhoidal disease. Together with the Swedish paper, this study establishes that genetic control of the intestinal molecular environment (mucus composition, glycan display, immune factors) is a primary determinant of gut microbiota variation across Northern European populations.
- **Link:** [https://www.nature.com/articles/s41588-026-02502-4](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41588-026-02502-4)
