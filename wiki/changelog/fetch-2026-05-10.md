### 1. Gut Microbiota-Regulated Glutathione Metabolic Rhythms Restore Obesity-Induced Colonic Inflammatory Oscillations

- **Journal:** Gut Microbes (IF ~12)
- **Highlights:** Zhenting Zhao and colleagues demonstrate that gut dysbiosis associated with diet-induced obesity disrupts the normal diurnal oscillation of colonic inflammatory markers, and that this disruption is mediated through impaired microbiota-driven glutathione (GSH) metabolic rhythms. Using germ-free and conventionalized mouse models alongside time-resolved metabolomics, the study shows that specific commensal bacteria maintain circadian patterns of GSH biosynthesis precursors in the colon, and that obesity-associated taxa replacement abolishes these rhythms and locks the colon in a pro-inflammatory state. Restoring microbial GSH-metabolic cycling — through prebiotic supplementation or fecal microbiota transplantation from lean donors — re-established colonic inflammatory oscillations and reduced markers of chronic low-grade colitis. The work adds a temporally resolved layer to gut microbiota–obesity–inflammation interactions and suggests chrono-microbiome interventions as a therapeutic avenue.
- **Link:** [Gut Microbes Vol. 18 (2026) — tandfonline.com](https://www.tandfonline.com/journals/kgmi20/current) (published online 9 May 2026; specific DOI pending full indexing)

### 2. Host Metabolism Shapes the Intestinal Microbiota: A Top-Down Paradigm of Environmental Selection Pressure

- **Journal:** Gut Microbes (IF ~12)
- **Highlights:** Ziyu Ma and colleagues present a conceptual and empirical synthesis arguing that the directionality of the host–microbiota relationship is underappreciated in the bottom-up (microbiota-to-host) direction: the host's own metabolic state — particularly systemic energy balance, bile acid flux, oxygen tension in the gut lumen, and mucosal immune tone — acts as the primary environmental selection pressure that sculpts microbiota composition. Drawing on gnotobiotic, dietary perturbation, and disease-model experiments, the authors show that manipulating host metabolic parameters (e.g., high-fat diet, pharmacological FXR activation, or induced hypoxia) predictably remodels microbiota composition independently of direct dietary substrate availability, and that these host-driven changes precede and cause downstream microbiome shifts rather than following them. The review challenges purely microbiota-centric therapeutic frameworks and calls for metabolic normalization as a prerequisite for durable microbiome modulation.
- **Link:** [Gut Microbes Vol. 18 (2026) — tandfonline.com](https://www.tandfonline.com/journals/kgmi20/current) (published online 9 May 2026; specific DOI pending full indexing)

### 3. Gut Microbiota in Chronic Inflammation: The Interplay with Lipid Mediators

- **Journal:** Gut Microbes (IF ~12)
- **Highlights:** Suxia Bao and colleagues provide a comprehensive review of the bidirectional interactions between the gut microbiota and host lipid mediators — including pro-inflammatory eicosanoids (prostaglandins, leukotrienes), specialized pro-resolving mediators (SPMs: resolvins, protectins, maresins), and bioactive sphingolipids. The review synthesizes evidence that commensal bacteria modulate arachidonic acid and omega-3 PUFA-derived mediator production through direct enzymatic biotransformation, SCFA-mediated regulation of lipid-metabolizing immune cells, and bile acid–lipid crosstalk, and that this microbial influence on the lipidome is a key determinant of whether chronic low-grade inflammation resolves or persists. Relevant disease contexts covered include IBD, metabolic syndrome, colorectal cancer, and cardiovascular disease. The authors highlight SPM-producing bacterial strains as candidate next-generation probiotics targeting resolution of chronic inflammation.
- **Link:** [Gut Microbes Vol. 18 (2026) — tandfonline.com](https://www.tandfonline.com/journals/kgmi20/current) (published online 6–7 May 2026; specific DOI pending full indexing)

### 4. A Novel Bile Salt Hydrolase-Producing Ligilactobacillus salivarius Prevents Diet-Induced Obesity via Regulation of Bile Acid Metabolism and Glucagon-Like Peptide 1 Restoration

- **Journal:** Gut Microbes (IF ~12)
- **Highlights:** Jiayao Lv and colleagues identify a novel strain of Ligilactobacillus salivarius with unusually high bile salt hydrolase (BSH) activity and characterize its anti-obesity mechanism in a high-fat-diet mouse model. The strain promoted deconjugation and subsequent bacterial 7α-dehydroxylation of bile acids, shifting the bile acid pool toward unconjugated secondary species that activate intestinal farnesoid X receptor (FXR) and TGR5 signaling. TGR5 activation in L-cells stimulated GLP-1 secretion, improving glucose tolerance and reducing adiposity; FXR activation in the ileum reduced bile acid reabsorption and hepatic lipogenesis. Antibiotic depletion experiments and GLP-1 receptor antagonist co-treatment confirmed the BSH–bile acid–GLP-1 axis as the primary effector pathway. Germ-free mouse colonization with the strain recapitulated the anti-obesity phenotype. The findings position BSH activity as a key probiotic selection criterion and illuminate a tractable microbiota–bile acid–incretin axis for obesity pharmacotherapy.
- **Link:** [Gut Microbes Vol. 18 (2026) — tandfonline.com](https://www.tandfonline.com/journals/kgmi20/current) (published online 6 May 2026; specific DOI pending full indexing)
