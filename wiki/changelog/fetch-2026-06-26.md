## Papers Found (2026-06-26)

### 1. Gut microbiome drives glycodeoxycholic acid-mediated attenuation of hypertension
- **Journal:** Gut Microbes (IF ~12)
- **Highlights:** Sachin Aryal et al. demonstrate that gut bacteria transform primary bile acids into glycodeoxycholic acid (GDCA), a secondary bile acid that attenuates hypertension. Using gnotobiotic and dietary intervention models, they show that microbiome-driven GDCA production reduces blood pressure, mechanistically linking specific microbial bile acid transformations to cardiovascular regulation via gut epithelial and vascular signalling pathways. The study positions microbial bile acid metabolism as a druggable target for microbiome-based antihypertensive strategies.
- **Link:** [doi:10.1080/19490976.2026 (DOI pending full indexing)](https://www.tandfonline.com/journals/kgmi20)

### 2. Effect of pasteurized *Akkermansia muciniphila* MucT on insulin sensitivity, body composition, and GLP-1 production in subjects with metabolic syndrome: impact of low baseline gut *Akkermansia* levels
- **Journal:** Gut Microbes (IF ~12)
- **Highlights:** Peter Suenaert et al. report a clinical study of pasteurized *A. muciniphila* MucT supplementation in subjects with metabolic syndrome, assessing insulin sensitivity, body composition, and GLP-1 production. Beneficial metabolic effects were most pronounced in participants with low baseline gut *Akkermansia* abundance, establishing colonisation status as a key stratification biomarker. The results support precision probiotic prescription — matching supplementation to individuals most likely to benefit based on their baseline microbiome profile — and mechanistically link *Akkermansia*-driven improvements to GLP-1 secretion and insulin sensitivity.
- **Link:** [doi:10.1080/19490976.2026 (DOI pending full indexing)](https://www.tandfonline.com/journals/kgmi20)

### 3. Microbiome-driven alterations in tryptophan metabolism contribute to behavioral comorbidities in the Muc2 knockout mouse model of chronic colitis
- **Journal:** Gut Microbes (IF ~12)
- **Highlights:** Jessica K. Josephson et al. use the Muc2−/− mouse model of chronic colitis to dissect how dysbiosis-driven changes in tryptophan metabolism underlie IBD behavioral comorbidities. Muc2-deficient mice exhibit microbiome-driven depletion of serotonergic tryptophan metabolites and enrichment of the kynurenine pathway, correlating with anxiety- and depression-like behaviors. The study provides mechanistic evidence linking colitis-associated dysbiosis to gut-brain axis dysfunction via tryptophan metabolic reprogramming, and suggests microbiome-targeted interventions as a strategy to ameliorate the psychiatric burden of IBD.
- **Link:** [doi:10.1080/19490976.2026 (DOI pending full indexing)](https://www.tandfonline.com/journals/kgmi20)

### 4. Fermented food microbiome: influence on oral and gut microbiota, and human health
- **Journal:** Nature Reviews Microbiology (IF ~69)
- **Highlights:** Kim D., Joe H.I., Bae J.W. et al. synthesize evidence on how the microbial consortia and bioactive compounds in plant-based fermented foods modulate the oral-gut microbial axis, support immune function, and enhance metabolic resilience. The review covers strain-specific colonisation dynamics, SCFA and postbiotic transfer, and oral-gut microbial interactions that amplify or attenuate fermented food benefits. A strategic precision nutrition roadmap is proposed for integrating the fermented food microbiome into personalised dietary interventions, positioning fermented foods as accessible tools to sustain microbiome diversity and host immunometabolic health. Published June 23, 2026.
- **Link:** [doi:10.1038/s41579-026-01333-8](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41579-026-01333-8)

### 5. Beyond estrobolome 1.0: Unraveling endocrine-microbiome axis as a driver and therapeutic target in hormone-driven cancers *(bonus — npj Biofilms and Microbiomes)*
- **Journal:** npj Biofilms and Microbiomes (IF ~9; bonus journal)
- **Highlights:** Mou E., Guo R., Yi Y., Li Y., Yao Y., & Xu J. extend the estrobolome concept to a comprehensive endocrine-microbiome axis framework, reviewing how gut microbial β-glucuronidase activity regulates circulating estrogen levels and how dysbiosis-driven shifts in this activity influence estrogen receptor-positive breast cancer, endometrial cancer, and other hormone-dependent malignancies. The review covers mechanisms including altered hormone availability, microbiota-derived hormone-like compounds, and microbiome-mediated immune and inflammatory reprogramming. Probiotic, prebiotic, FMT, and phage-based strategies to modulate the estrobolome are evaluated, positioning the gut-endocrine axis as a druggable interface that may partly explain variability in hormone therapy and immunotherapy outcomes.
- **Link:** [doi:10.1038/s41522-026-01074-9](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41522-026-01074-9)
