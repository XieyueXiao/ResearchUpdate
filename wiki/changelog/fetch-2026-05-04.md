### 1. Spatial transcriptomics maps host–gut microbiome biogeography at high resolution
- **Journal:** Nature Microbiology (IF ~28)
- **Highlights:** Researchers at Cornell University developed a technique combining enzymatic in situ polyadenylation of both bacterial and host RNA with spatial RNA sequencing (Visium), enabling capture of microbial transcripts at 1 µm resolution — far exceeding prior spatial metagenomics tools. Applied in a mouse model of intestinal neoplasia, the method revealed the biogeography of the gut microbiome as a function of intestinal location, uncovered frequent strong intermicrobial interactions at short length scales (< 5 µm), and documented tumour-associated remodelling of the host–microbiome interface including preferential enrichment of specific Proteobacteria near neoplastic crypts. Bulk polyadenylation substantially increased bacterial RNA capture efficiency. This approach provides the first transcriptome-level view of spatially resolved microbiome–host co-expression in situ and is immediately applicable to human biopsy material on commercial spatial platforms.
- **Link:** [DOI: 10.1038/s41564-026-02286-7](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41564-026-02286-7)

### 2. Fecal microbiota transplantation plus pembrolizumab and axitinib in metastatic renal cell carcinoma: the randomized phase 2 TACITO trial
- **Journal:** Nature Medicine (IF ~82)
- **Highlights:** The TACITO trial (multicenter, randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled phase 2a; n = 53 mRCC patients) tested whether FMT from donors who had achieved complete responses to immunotherapy could improve outcomes in first-line mRCC when combined with the standard pembrolizumab + axitinib regimen. FMT from responder donors improved median progression-free survival from 9.0 months (placebo) to 24.0 months, and increased objective response rate from 32% to 52%. A surprising mechanistic finding was that clinical benefit correlated more strongly with the *elimination* of specific deleterious bacterial taxa from the recipient's microbiome than with engraftment of donor-derived bacteria per se — challenging the prevailing "add good bugs" model and pointing to dysbiotic communities as active immunotherapy suppressors. Published alongside the companion PERFORM and FMT-LUMINate trials in *Nature Medicine* Vol. 32 No. 4 (April 2026), completing a landmark trio of FMT-immunotherapy trials across three tumour types.
- **Link:** [DOI: 10.1038/s41591-025-04189-2](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41591-025-04189-2)

### 3. The gut microbiome–bile acid-FXR interplay: a pivotal axis in metabolic and gastrointestinal diseases
- **Journal:** Gut Microbes (IF ~12)
- **Highlights:** This comprehensive review by Shou et al. synthesises the bidirectional crosstalk between the gut microbiome and bile acid signalling, foregrounding the farnesoid X receptor (FXR) and TGR5 as central transducers. The authors detail how primary bile acids produced in the liver are transformed by microbial 7α-dehydroxylation, epimerization, and conjugation reactions into secondary bile acids (DCA, LCA, UDCA, isoUDCA), which selectively agonise or antagonise intestinal and hepatic FXR, thereby modulating lipid metabolism, glucose homeostasis, intestinal barrier integrity, and mucosal immunity. Dysregulated bile acid–FXR signalling is mapped across MASLD/MASH, type 2 diabetes, cholestatic liver disease, IBD, and colorectal cancer. Emerging therapeutic strategies reviewed include microbial enzyme engineering, bacteriotherapy targeting bile acid-converting taxa (e.g., *Christensenella minuta*, *Clostridium scindens*), and selective intestinal FXR agonists/antagonists. The review provides a mechanistic framework for designing microbiome-informed bile acid therapeutics.
- **Link:** [DOI: 10.1080/19490976.2026.2643373](https://doi.org/10.1080/19490976.2026.2643373)

### 4. Saccharomyces boulardii CNCM I-745 synergizes with the small intestinal microbiota to boost AhR signalling in celiac disease
- **Journal:** Gut Microbes (IF ~12)
- **Highlights:** Impaired aryl hydrocarbon receptor (AhR) activation — driven by reduced microbial tryptophan-to-indole conversion in the small intestine — is a recognised feature of active celiac disease (CeD). Using NOD/DQ8 gluten-sensitised mice and ex vivo cultures of small intestinal microbiota from CeD patients, Kan et al. show that *Saccharomyces boulardii* CNCM I-745 acts synergistically with resident bacteria to increase free tryptophan availability (by enzymatic protein hydrolysis) and suppress competing tryptophan-degradation pathways, yielding higher luminal indole concentrations and markedly elevated mucosal AhR activation (up to ~280-fold vs. community-alone controls). In vivo, Sbou administration improved villus-to-crypt ratios, reduced intraepithelial lymphocyte counts, and decreased local pro-inflammatory cytokines; all effects were blocked by the AhR antagonist CH-223191. These results establish a probiotic–microbiota–metabolite circuit as a targetable mechanism for adjunctive management of CeD beyond a gluten-free diet.
- **Link:** [DOI: 10.1080/19490976.2026.2652476](https://doi.org/10.1080/19490976.2026.2652476)

### 5. Gut microbiome remodeling induced by microplastic exposure in humans
- **Journal:** Gut Microbes (IF ~12)
- **Highlights:** Microplastic contamination of the human gut is now near-universal, yet the consequences for gut microbiome composition and function are only beginning to be characterised in human (vs. animal) subjects. This systematic review by researchers at Sichuan Agricultural University synthesises evidence from human fecal metagenomics and 16S rRNA studies in populations with documented microplastic exposure (fecal particle quantification or dietary exposure surrogates). Across studies, microplastic exposure was consistently associated with enrichment of opportunistic taxa (Enterobacteriaceae, Dethiosulfovibrionaceae, Moraxellaceae) and depletion of Bacillota and Bacteroidota communities including butyrate-producing Lachnospiraceae and Ruminococcaceae — mirroring a dysbiotic pattern seen in IBD and metabolic disease. Functional metagenomics identified reduced capacity for SCFA biosynthesis and elevated encoding of pro-inflammatory lipopolysaccharide biosynthesis genes. The review highlights contradictions across studies for taxa such as *Alistipes* and *Faecalibacterium* and calls for standardized particle-size reporting and longitudinal human cohorts to establish causal dose–response relationships.
- **Link:** [DOI: 10.1080/19490976.2026.2617696](https://doi.org/10.1080/19490976.2026.2617696)

### 6. Lactic acid bacteria and endogenous ethanol mediate proton pump inhibitor-associated MASLD: a multicohort cross-sectional mediation analysis
- **Journal:** Gut Microbes (IF ~12)
- **Highlights:** Proton pump inhibitor (PPI) use is independently associated with metabolic-dysfunction-associated steatotic liver disease (MASLD), but the biological intermediary has been unclear. Davids et al. analysed fecal metagenomes and serum metabolomics from three independent European cohorts (combined n > 4,500) and performed formal mediation analysis to test whether PPI-induced gut dysbiosis drives MASLD via endogenous ethanol production. PPI use shifted the gut microbiome toward Small Intestinal Bacterial Overgrowth (SIBO)-like profiles enriched in lactic acid bacteria (LAB; particularly Lactobacillaceae and Streptococcaceae), which are potent ethanol fermenters. Serum and urinary endogenous ethanol levels were significantly elevated in PPI users relative to matched non-users, and mediation analysis demonstrated that approximately 31% of the PPI–MASLD association was mediated through the LAB enrichment→endogenous ethanol production pathway. Findings were replicated across all three cohorts and remained significant after adjustment for alcohol consumption, BMI, and comorbidities, positioning LAB-derived endogenous ethanol as a novel, clinically actionable mechanism linking PPI use to hepatic steatosis.
- **Link:** [DOI: 10.1080/19490976.2026.2649487](https://doi.org/10.1080/19490976.2026.2649487)
