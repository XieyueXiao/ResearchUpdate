## Papers Found (2026-06-21)

### 1. Fatty diets disrupt mucus–microbiome–metabolite interactions to increase intestinal lipid uptake
- **Journal:** Nature Microbiology (IF ~28)
- **Highlights:** High-fat dietary intake disrupts the protective colonic mucus layer and its associated microbial community, altering how gut bacteria transform host-derived bile acids. The authors (Holmberg & Schroeder, Umeå University) show that these diet-induced perturbations to the colonic mucus–microbiome–metabolite axis alter the composition of the intestinal bile acid pool, which in turn drives increased lipid absorption in the small intestine. The findings reveal a previously unrecognised cross-regional link—from the colonic mucus niche to small intestinal fat uptake—providing a mechanistic framework for how high-fat diets may promote metabolic dysfunction through microbiome-mediated bile acid remodelling.
- **Link:** [doi:10.1038/s41564-026-02402-7](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41564-026-02402-7)
