### 1. Clostridia from preterm infants metabolize human milk oligosaccharides to suppress pathobionts and modulate intestinal function in organoids

- **Journal:** Nature Microbiology (IF ~28)
- **Highlights:** Chapman, Masi, Beck, Watson, Young, Browne, Shao, Kiu, Hall, Embleton, Stewart et al. used genomics, proteomics, and metabolomics to show that *Clostridium perfringens* strains isolated from preterm infants (<32 weeks gestation) — specifically strains lacking the toxin perfringolysin O (pfoA−) — can metabolize human milk oligosaccharides (HMOs), producing short-chain fatty acids and tryptophan catabolites at levels exceeding those of *Bifidobacterium* species in vitro. Cell-free supernatant from these pfoA− *C. perfringens* strains was non-toxic to colonic cell lines, promoted commensal Bifidobacterium growth, inhibited pathobiont expansion, and suppressed inflammation in preterm-derived intestinal organoids. These findings challenge the conventional Bifidobacterium-centric view of HMO utilization and suggest that select non-toxic Clostridia contribute meaningfully to protective microbiome assembly in the fragile preterm gut, with implications for probiotic design in neonatal intensive care.
- **Link:** [doi:10.1038/s41564-026-02297-4](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41564-026-02297-4) — Published March 17, 2026 (Vol. 11, pp. 940–959)

### 2. Human milk oligosaccharide mediates mutualism between Escherichia coli and Bifidobacterium bifidum

- **Journal:** Nature Communications (IF ~17)
- **Highlights:** Analyzing longitudinal fecal samples from 41 healthy, term-born, breastfed infants and 30 related mothers via shotgun metagenomics and novel computational approaches, researchers at the University of Birmingham demonstrated that *E. coli* and *Bifidobacterium bifidum* engage in obligate mutualism within the infant gut: *E. coli* supplies cysteine to its auxotrophic partner *B. bifidum*, enabling cooperative degradation of 2′-fucosyllactose (the predominant HMO), while *B. bifidum* in turn provides simple sugars that sustain *E. coli* colonization. Strain-resolved profiling revealed that *Bifidobacterium* strains are frequently shared within families (vertical transmission), whereas *E. coli* strains derive largely from environmental sources yet persist stably in individuals. This cross-feeding partnership keeps *E. coli* at low stable abundances while supporting a Bifidobacterium-dominant ecosystem, providing a mechanistic explanation for why formula-fed infants — who receive fewer HMOs — show reduced *Bifidobacterium* and elevated *E. coli* levels.
- **Link:** [doi:10.1038/s41467-026-71764-7](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41467-026-71764-7) — Published April 22, 2026 (Nat Commun 17, Article 3489)
