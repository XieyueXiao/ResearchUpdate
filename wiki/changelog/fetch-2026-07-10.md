## Papers Found (2026-07-10)

### 1. Gut microbiota associates with frailty in older women
- **Journal:** Nature Communications (IF ~17)
- **Highlights:** Whether gut microbiome features track with frailty in aging populations at scale has been unclear. University of Gothenburg researchers profiled shotgun metagenomes from the SUPERB cohort of 2,081 Swedish women aged 75–80 and related microbiome composition and function to an internally developed Frailty Mortality Index (FMI). A higher FMI — indicating greater estimated frailty and mortality risk — was associated with lower microbial diversity, fewer microbial genes, and reduced predicted functional capacity; 404 bacterial species were significantly associated with FMI, and associations extended to physical function and fall-related injury outcomes. Many associations replicated in an independent Chinese cohort of 1,448 older adults, suggesting shared microbial signatures of frailty across geographically distinct older populations and nominating gut microbiome features as candidate biomarkers/targets for frailty risk stratification.
- **Link:** [DOI: 10.1038/s41467-026-75176-5](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41467-026-75176-5)

### 2. Meta-analysis reveals microbiome signatures for colorectal cancer that are universal across age groups and sequencing methods
- **Journal:** Cell Host & Microbe (IF ~30)
- **Highlights:** Stool-based microbiome biomarkers for colorectal cancer (CRC) have been hampered by inconsistent signatures across studies and sequencing platforms. An international Mi-EOCRC consortium team (Pekel et al.; EMBL Heidelberg Zeller and Zimmermann groups, with German/Swiss/Dutch collaborators) consistently re-processed and meta-analyzed 6,779 amplicon and shotgun metagenomic stool/tumor profiles across 27 studies. Association and machine-learning analyses identified a CRC microbiome signature — enriched for taxa including *Fusobacterium nucleatum*, *Parvimonas micra*, *Peptostreptococcus stomatis*, *Porphyromonas asaccharolytica*, and *Gemella morbillorum*, with depletion of SCFA-producing commensals — that generalizes robustly across cohorts and sequencing approaches and is nearly identical between early- and late-onset CRC. The work also links the signature to dietary fiber intake and maps the biogeography of cancer-enriched *Fusobacterium* subspecies, offering a more universal candidate biomarker panel than prior single-cohort studies. Online-first coverage began circulating ~2026-06-24; formal issue publication is Vol. 34, Issue 7 (2026-07-08) — not captured in any prior fetch (2026-04-28 through 2026-07-09).
- **Link:** [DOI: 10.1016/j.chom.2026.05.030](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.chom.2026.05.030)
