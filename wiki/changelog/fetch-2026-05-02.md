### 1. A gut microbiome-kidney-heart axis predictive of future cardiovascular diseases

- **Journal:** Nature Communications (IF ~17)
- **Highlights:** Researchers from Imperial College London, the METACARDIS Consortium, and the Canadian Longitudinal Study on Aging analysed metabolomic, metagenomic, and clinical data from more than 8,000 adults to characterise a gut microbiome–kidney–heart axis. Gut microbial amino acid metabolism produces circulating metabolites that interact with kidney function in a bidirectional manner; Mendelian randomisation confirmed causal directionality for several microbial metabolite–kidney–CVD pathways. Protective microbiome features were strongest in metabolically healthy individuals and eroded progressively with overweight and early metabolic dysfunction, positioning gut microbiome profiling as a potential pre-symptomatic risk biomarker for cardiovascular disease.
- **Link:** [DOI: 10.1038/s41467-026-69405-0](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41467-026-69405-0)

### 2. Gut microbial enzymes shape the health benefits of dietary phytonutrients

- **Journal:** Nature Microbiology (IF ~28)
- **Highlights:** A large-scale integrative study systematically mapped the enzymatic biotransformation of 775 dietary phytonutrients from edible plants using 5,554 global gut metagenomes, biochemical databases, and in vitro validation. Biotransformation capacity was encoded by enzymes distributed across diverse gut microbial taxa, with Eubacterium ramulus and close relatives showing broad phytonutrient-processing activity. Machine learning models trained on the microbiome-phytonutrient transformation landscape discriminated health versus disease status across multiple disease contexts, providing a mechanistic link between dietary plant compounds, microbiome enzyme repertoires, and host health outcomes. A companion paper (DOI: 10.1038/s41564-025-02197-z) reports corresponding epidemiological associations across population cohorts.
- **Link:** [DOI: 10.1038/s41564-025-02220-3](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41564-025-02220-3)

### 3. Benchmarking of shotgun sequencing depth reveals the potential and limitations of shallow metagenomics and strain-level analysis

- **Journal:** Nature Microbiology (IF ~28)
- **Highlights:** Treichel et al. systematically benchmarked shotgun metagenomic sequencing at multiple depths using complex defined mock communities to establish minimum sequencing standards required for accurate microbial community profiling. The study reveals that species-level composition and functional pathway coverage are robust at modest depths, but strain-level resolution and metagenome-assembled genome (MAG) quality degrade substantially below a defined depth threshold. Shallow metagenomics (1–5 Gbp) is validated for epidemiological composition surveys, but strain-level analyses require ≥10 Gbp per sample; the work provides concrete benchmarks to guide study design and inter-study comparisons across the field.
- **Link:** [DOI: 10.1038/s41564-026-02334-2](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41564-026-02334-2)

### 4. Cayman enables large-scale analysis of gut microbiome carbohydrate-active enzyme repertoires

- **Journal:** Nature Microbiology (IF ~28)
- **Highlights:** Cayman is a new computational tool purpose-built for large-scale profiling of carbohydrate-active enzymes (CAZymes) in metagenomic data, addressing a major gap in microbiome bioinformatics where substrate-level interpretation of CAZyme annotations has been intractable at cohort scale. Application across thousands of gut metagenomes revealed novel mucus-foraging microorganisms, previously uncharacterised CAZyme families, and consistent differences in CAZyme repertoire composition associated with urbanisation, dietary patterns, and inflammatory bowel disease. Cayman's integration with existing pipelines makes strain- and species-resolved glycan-metabolism profiling scalable for large epidemiological and clinical studies.
- **Link:** [DOI: 10.1038/s41564-026-02318-2](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41564-026-02318-2)

### 5. Metagenome-assembled genomes from a population-based cohort uncover novel gut species and within-species diversity, revealing prevalent disease associations

- **Journal:** mSystems (IF ~6)
- **Highlights:** From 1,878 deeply sequenced stool samples of the Estonian microbiome cohort (EstMB-deep), researchers reconstructed 84,762 MAGs representing 2,257 species clusters, of which 353 (15.6%) were previously uncharacterised — reaching up to 30% relative abundance in individual hosts. The study introduces the genome unit number (GUN), a scalable metric for quantifying within-species genomic diversity, and demonstrates that within-species structure can completely reverse disease associations: two dominant genome units of Odoribacter splanchnicus showed divergent associations with gastritis and hypertensive heart disease. Integration with the UHGG reference collection produced an expanded catalogue (GUTrep) profiling 2,509 individuals against 33 prevalent diseases, revealing widespread disease associations invisible to species-level analyses.
- **Link:** [DOI: 10.1128/msystems.00114-26](https://doi.org/10.1128/msystems.00114-26)

### 6. Phenotypic profiling of neutrophils in acute Clostridioides difficile infection identifies a TNF-induced activation signature associated with epithelial damage

- **Journal:** Gut Microbes (IF ~12)
- **Highlights:** Huber et al. performed in-depth phenotypic characterisation of neutrophil populations in patients with acute Clostridioides difficile infection (CDI), finding a distinct TNF-α-driven activation signature absent in healthy controls and patients with non-CDI diarrhoea. This hyperactivated neutrophil state correlated with intestinal epithelial damage biomarkers and disease severity scores, implicating neutrophil-TNF-α crosstalk as a key driver of CDI immunopathology beyond microbial toxin effects. The findings raise the possibility that anti-TNF adjuvant strategies or neutrophil-targeted interventions could complement antibiotics in severe CDI, and provide a new immune signature framework for patient stratification.
- **Link:** [DOI: 10.1080/19490976.2026.2494687](https://doi.org/10.1080/19490976.2026.2494687) *(provisional DOI — confirm on publication)*

### 7. Genome-wide association analyses highlight the role of the intestinal molecular environment in human gut microbiota variation *(bonus — Nature Genetics)*

- **Journal:** Nature Genetics (IF ~40; not on primary target list)
- **Highlights:** A GWAS of harmonised metagenomic data from 16,017 adults across four Swedish population-based cohorts, with replication in 12,652 Norwegian HUNT individuals, identified 15 study-wide significant associations spanning eight genomic loci and 14 common bacterial species. A novel signal at the OR51E1–OR51E2 locus — encoding olfactory receptors that sense microbiome-derived short-chain fatty acids — was associated with microbial richness, linking host sensory biology to microbiome ecology. Known loci (LCT, ABO, FUT2) were confirmed, and new loci (MUC12, CORO7–HMOX2, SLC5A11, FOXP1, FUT3–FUT6) implicate intestinal glycan display and mucosal environment as primary determinants of microbiota composition. A companion paper (DOI: 10.1038/s41588-026-02502-4) reports the complementary HUNT-primary analysis.
- **Link:** [DOI: 10.1038/s41588-026-02512-2](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41588-026-02512-2)
