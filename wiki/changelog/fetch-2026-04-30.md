### 1. The microbiome regulates host metabolic health and diseases through microbial enzymes
- **Journal:** Nature Reviews Gastroenterology & Hepatology (IF ~45)
- **Published:** April 7–8, 2026
- **Highlights:** This comprehensive review by Ding et al. addresses how gut microbial enzymes — including bile salt hydrolases, β-glucuronidases, microbial lipases, and proteases — are the principal mechanistic link between the gut microbiota and host metabolic health. The authors survey omics evidence implicating the microbiota in metabolic dysfunction, then pivot to enzyme-level causality: structural and functional diversity of key enzyme classes, assay methodologies (metagenomics, metaproteomics, activity-based probes), and emerging therapeutic strategies that target specific microbial enzyme activities to modulate host metabolism. The review explicitly flags the field's causality gap and outlines criteria for translating enzyme-centric microbiome findings into clinical applications.
- **Link:** [https://doi.org/10.1038/s41575-026-01195-8](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41575-026-01195-8)

### 2. Interspecies interaction controls Escherichia coli growth in human gut microbiome samples
- **Journal:** PNAS (IF ~12)
- **Published:** ~April 22–23, 2026 (accepted March 18, 2026)
- **Highlights:** Using replicated anaerobic microcosm experiments with human fecal microbiomes, the authors showed that E. coli growth performance — both absolute and relative to other strains — varies substantially across microbiomes from healthy individuals, partly due to intraspecific competition with resident conspecifics. A single Clostridium butyricum strain was identified as the primary driver of this variation through microbially mediated acidification: transfer of C. butyricum into other microbiomes reproducibly shifted pH and fermentation product profiles (boosting butyrate +14.8 mM on average, depleting acetate/lactate), and suppressed E. coli growth while reshaping overall community composition. The findings demonstrate that inter-individual microbiome variation produces fundamentally different ecological contexts for incoming bacteria — and that this variability can be explained by, and manipulated via, a single generalizable interspecies interaction.
- **Link:** [https://doi.org/10.1073/pnas.2527793123](https://doi.org/10.1073/pnas.2527793123)

### 3. Exploring differences in the human gut microbiome between Han Chinese and non-Chinese populations
- **Journal:** Genome Biology (IF ~12)
- **Published:** January 17, 2026 (newly confirmed for this knowledge base)
- **Highlights:** Wang, Zhang, Chen et al. leveraged the Human Gut Microbiome Reference Genome Catalog (HGMRGC) — 478,588 high-quality microbial genomes from Chinese and non-Chinese populations — to conduct a systematic comparison at both taxonomic abundance and SNP levels. Geography emerged as the primary driver of inter-population microbial variation. The study identified 689 population-specific genome clusters within the Collinsella genus with functional differences in carbohydrate utilization, and 108 species with distinct prevalence linked to vitamin biosynthesis, antibiotic resistance, and carbohydrate metabolism between Han Chinese and non-Chinese cohorts. Alpha diversity was higher in non-Chinese populations for abundance metrics, while Han Chinese populations showed greater within-species SNP diversity, highlighting the need for ethnicity- and geography-aware microbiome reference databases.
- **Link:** [https://doi.org/10.1186/s13059-026-03932-3](https://doi.org/10.1186/s13059-026-03932-3)

### 4. Circadian synchronization between diet and gut microbiota in modulating host's health
- **Journal:** Gut Microbes (IF ~12)
- **Published:** March 31, 2026 (newly confirmed for this knowledge base)
- **Highlights:** This review synthesizes the bidirectional crosstalk among diet, gut microbiota, and the host circadian clock, positioning the diet–microbiota–circadian axis as a key determinant of metabolic health. Gut microbes modulate circadian gene expression through multiple routes: (1) SCFA/lactate production from dietary polysaccharides shifts the phase of peripheral clocks via HDAC inhibition and histone acetylation of clock gene promoters; (2) microbial synthesis of melatonin from tryptophan provides a potential humoral link between the microbiome and host circadian timing; (3) conversion of dietary proteins into polyamines influences clock gene transcription. Reciprocally, the host's feeding-fasting cycle and circadian clock determine the rhythmic composition and activity of the gut microbiota. The review discusses implications for chrono-nutrition and microbiome-targeted interventions for metabolic syndrome.
- **Link:** [https://doi.org/10.1080/19490976.2026.2647602](https://doi.org/10.1080/19490976.2026.2647602)

### 5. Modulating the gut microbiota to enhance immune checkpoint inhibitor efficacy in colorectal cancer: mechanisms, therapeutic strategies, and clinical perspectives
- **Journal:** Gut Microbes (IF ~12)
- **Published:** April 14, 2026
- **Highlights:** ICI therapy in CRC remains effective only in the MSI-H minority (~15% of patients); the MSS majority is largely unresponsive. This review frames gut microbiota as a pivotal determinant of ICI response and catalogs the evidence: specific taxa (Akkermansia muciniphila, Bifidobacterium spp., Faecalibacterium spp., Alistipes shahii, Eubacterium limosum) are enriched in ICI responders; outer membrane vesicles from A. muciniphila activate B cells and dendritic cells, induce mucosal IgA, and upregulate tight junctions to preserve barrier integrity. Bacterial metabolites (SCFAs, indoles, bile acids) tune T-cell differentiation and PD-L1 expression. Clinical strategies reviewed include FMT + ICI (pooled ORR 43.1% vs. 36.9% for ICI alone in early-phase trials), targeted probiotic supplementation, dietary prebiotics, and antibiotics stewardship. Authors identify the microbiome composition at treatment initiation as the most actionable predictive biomarker window.
- **Link:** [https://doi.org/10.1080/19490976.2026.2652476](https://doi.org/10.1080/19490976.2026.2652476)
