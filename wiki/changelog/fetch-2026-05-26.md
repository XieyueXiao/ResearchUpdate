## Papers Found (2026-05-26)

### 1. Biogeography-associated emergence of enhanced oxygen tolerance in the abundant human gut commensal Segatella copri

- **Journal:** Cell Host & Microbe (IF ~30)
- **Highlights:** Segatella copri (formerly Prevotella copri), one of the most abundant gut commensals, harbours strains that acquired the transcription factor OxyR via horizontal gene transfer, conferring up to 1,000-fold enhanced tolerance to oxygen compared with strains lacking this regulator. These oxygen-tolerant strains are disproportionately prevalent in industrialised countries, suggesting a biogeography-driven selective advantage — possibly linked to the elevated intraluminal oxygen tensions associated with Western diets and colitis. A second transcription regulator, PerR, was shown to control a genetic network essential for bacterial oxygen response; a mouse colonisation model demonstrated that S. copri cannot establish gut residence without functional PerR. The findings resolve a long-standing paradox — how a prototypically anaerobic commensal persists across highly variable luminal oxygen microenvironments — and may inform why S. copri abundance diverges between industrialised and non-industrialised populations.
- **Link:** [doi:10.1016/j.chom.2026.04.006](https://www.cell.com/cell-host-microbe/fulltext/S1931-3128(26)00162-9)
