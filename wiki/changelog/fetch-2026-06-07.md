### 1. Prevalent gut phages encode modular adhesins mediating epithelial binding and endoplasmic reticulum trafficking

- **Journal:** Nature Communications (IF ~17)
- **Highlights:** Using a high-throughput phage–epithelial adhesion screen, researchers identified a class of abundant human gut bacteriophages — including crAss-like phages and myoviruses of the Flandersviridae family — that encode modular immunoglobulin (Ig)-like domain adhesins conferring direct binding to and internalisation by intestinal epithelial cells in vitro, with increased phage retention in the mouse gut in vivo. Strikingly, domain sequence variation alters epithelial interaction profiles, and once internalised the phages traffic from the endoplasmic reticulum through the Golgi apparatus, suggesting access to non-degradative, ER-associated internalization pathways. The work overturns the view of gut phages as purely extracellular predators and opens a new conceptual lens — and potential therapeutic vector framework — for phage-based microbiome interventions.
- **Link:** [doi:10.1038/s41467-026-74031-x](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41467-026-74031-x)

### 2. Gut-derived bacterial extracellular vesicles: the microbial dark matter contributing to host inflammation and cardiometabolic disease

- **Journal:** Gut Microbes (IF ~12)
- **Highlights:** This systematic review by Oliver et al. synthesizes the emerging literature on bacterial extracellular vesicles (BEVs) shed into the intestinal lumen, arguing they represent underappreciated microbial effectors that traverse the epithelial barrier and enter systemic circulation to modulate inflammation and cardiometabolic risk. The authors catalogue mechanistic evidence showing that BEVs from pathobionts (e.g., LPS-rich outer-membrane vesicles from Gram-negative dysbiotic taxa) activate TLR4-NFκB signalling in endothelial cells and macrophages, promote hepatic lipid accumulation, and impair myocardial function, while BEVs from beneficial commensals (e.g., Lactobacillus-derived) can exert anti-inflammatory and barrier-protective effects. The review highlights that BEV composition is highly diet-sensitive and dysbiosis-dependent, positioning BEV profiling as a candidate liquid-biopsy readout of gut microbiome functional state in cardiometabolic disease.
- **Link:** [doi:10.1080/19490976.2026.pending](https://www.tandfonline.com/journals/kgmi20) _(DOI pending full indexing; published online 2026-06-06)_

### 3. Zip14 (Slc39a14) metal transporter dysfunction leads to Akkermansia dysbiosis and inflammation in intestinal epithelium via epigenetic changes

- **Journal:** Gut Microbes (IF ~12)
- **Highlights:** Building on prior work showing that the zinc/manganese transporter ZIP14 (SLC39A14) is highly expressed at the basolateral membrane of intestinal epithelial cells and induced by pro-inflammatory stimuli, Jimenez-Rondan et al. now demonstrate that enterocyte-specific Zip14 deletion in mice triggers epigenetic reprogramming of the intestinal epithelium — including altered DNA methylation and histone modification patterns at inflammatory gene loci — accompanied by marked depletion of Akkermansia muciniphila, increased intestinal permeability, and mucosal inflammation. The metal-microbiome-epigenome axis identified here adds mechanistic depth to the growing literature on host metal homeostasis as a determinant of microbiome composition and mucosal immune tone, with implications for IBD pathogenesis.
- **Link:** [doi:10.1080/19490976.2026.pending](https://www.tandfonline.com/journals/kgmi20) _(DOI pending full indexing; published online 2026-06-06)_

### 4. High-throughput identification of bacterial β-glucuronidase inhibitors using machine learning

- **Journal:** Gut Microbes (IF ~12)
- **Highlights:** Zhang et al. combine a high-throughput enzymatic screening platform with machine learning models trained on known GUS inhibitor chemotypes to systematically interrogate compound libraries for inhibitors of gut microbial β-glucuronidases (GUS) — enzymes responsible for reactivating glucuronidated drug metabolites and toxins in the colon, a key driver of irinotecan-induced GI toxicity and estrogen recycling. The ML-guided pipeline dramatically reduced the screening search space and identified novel chemical scaffolds with low-nanomolar GUS inhibitory activity; selectivity profiling against human GUS and toxicity counterscreening confirmed promising hits. The study establishes a scalable, data-driven framework for precision modulation of gut microbial enzymes without broad-spectrum antibiotics.
- **Link:** [doi:10.1080/19490976.2026.pending](https://www.tandfonline.com/journals/kgmi20) _(DOI pending full indexing; published online 2026-06-05)_

### 5. Genetic variants of the transporter SLC22A4 affect the abundance and survival of Fusobacterium nucleatum in colorectal cancer

- **Journal:** Gut Microbes (IF ~12)
- **Highlights:** Chouaibi et al. identify a host-genetic determinant of tumour-associated Fusobacterium nucleatum colonisation: common variants in SLC22A4, encoding the organic cation/ergothioneine transporter OCTN1, modulate F. nucleatum intracellular survival within colorectal cancer (CRC) epithelial cells. Using a combination of GWAS signal integration, in vitro infection assays with isogenic SLC22A4 knock-in/out CRC cell lines, and validation in patient CRC tissue biopsies, the authors show that risk alleles reduce OCTN1-mediated ergothioneine import, thereby altering the intracellular redox environment and facilitating F. nucleatum persistence. The work bridges the host-genetics and CRC microbiome fields, and suggests that patient SLC22A4 genotype could stratify F. nucleatum-positive CRC risk.
- **Link:** [doi:10.1080/19490976.2026.pending](https://www.tandfonline.com/journals/kgmi20) _(DOI pending full indexing; published online 2026-06-05)_

### 6. Deconfounded, quantitative microbiome profiling identifies robust multiple sclerosis markers and clinical covariate associations

- **Journal:** Gut Microbes (IF ~12)
- **Highlights:** Pauwels et al. (VIB-KU Leuven / VUB-UZ Brussels) apply quantitative microbiome profiling (QMP) — which corrects for differences in microbial load rather than relying on relative abundance — together with rigorous statistical deconfounding, to a large longitudinal multiple sclerosis (MS) cohort. The approach resolves previously contradictory MS microbiome findings: several species associations reported in earlier relative-abundance studies disappear under QMP, while new load-adjusted markers emerge with stronger reproducibility across disability-worsening subgroups. The authors further link the Bact2 dysbiotic enterotype (elevated Bacteroides 2, reduced commensals) to MS relapse rate and disability progression, validating it as a clinically actionable microbiome state. The study sets a methodological benchmark for future neurological disease microbiome studies.
- **Link:** [doi:10.1080/19490976.2026.pending](https://www.tandfonline.com/journals/kgmi20) _(DOI pending full indexing; published online 2026-06-05)_

### 7. Multiomics insights into the effects of prebiotics on physical function and metabolism in adults with obesity and knee osteoarthritis

- **Journal:** Gut Microbes (IF ~12)
- **Highlights:** Wang et al. report secondary multiomics analyses (16S rRNA + shotgun metagenomics + plasma metabolomics) from a randomised controlled trial of prebiotic fibre supplementation in adults with comorbid obesity and knee osteoarthritis (OA). Prebiotic recipients showed improved physical function scores, reduced trunk fat percentage, and distinct gut microbiota trajectories — including enrichment of Bifidobacterium and Faecalibacterium and altered carbohydrate-active enzyme (CAZyme) expression — relative to placebo. Integration of metagenomic and metabolomic data identified microbially-produced branched-chain fatty acids and trimethylamine N-oxide (TMAO) precursor modulation as mediators of the gut–joint axis, connecting prebiotic-driven microbiome changes to systemic cytokine shifts and phenotypic improvements in joint function.
- **Link:** [doi:10.1080/19490976.2026.pending](https://www.tandfonline.com/journals/kgmi20) _(DOI pending full indexing; published online 2026-06-05)_
