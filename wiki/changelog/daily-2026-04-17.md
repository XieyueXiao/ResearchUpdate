## New Themes Emerging (Papers 28–38)

### 7. Gut bacteria as direct metabolic actors in cancer
Three papers this round (##28, 30, and the prior ISME #23) describe gut or gastric bacteria not merely associating with cancer but **actively driving it through specific metabolite production**: *S. anginosus* methionine fuels gastric cancer one-carbon metabolism; *B. animalis* depletion permits oleic-acid-driven CRC metastasis. This moves well beyond compositional correlations toward mechanism-first cancer microbiology.

### 8. Muscle and metabolic performance as gut microbiome outputs
Paper #29 (*Roseburia inulinivorans* + muscle strength) adds to a growing body of evidence that commensals regulate not just gastrointestinal or immune phenotypes but systemic metabolic performance including physical capacity and arguably healthspan. Combined with #2 (cognitive aging via GPR84) and #35 (olive oil + cognition via *Adlercreutzia*), the gut-to-systemic-performance axis is becoming a major 2026 theme.

### 9. Circadian biology as a gut microbiome output
Paper #36 establishes TMA–TAAR5 as a gut-to-circadian signalling axis, raising the possibility that diet-induced microbiome changes alter not just metabolites but the timing machinery of host physiology. This connects gut microbiology to the circadian medicine literature in a mechanistic way previously lacking.

### 10. Olive oil polyphenols as microbiome modulators
Paper #35 distinguishes virgin from refined olive oil in their microbiome and cognitive effects, implicating oleocanthal/oleacein rather than oleic acid per se. This precision-nutrition finding has immediate dietary advice implications and complements #20 (fibre personalisation) and #3 (diet–microbiome associations).

## Updated Contradictions / Tensions

- **Akkermansia muciniphila dual role confirmed in a third context**: Paper #33 shows A. muciniphila *increasing* post-surgically in Crohn's patients who relapse — yet it is being developed as a probiotic. This is now the third independent observation of context-dependent harm (alongside #22 mucin-degradation review and the probiotic literature). The signal is strong enough to warrant a dedicated synthesis page.
- **F. prausnitzii mechanism**: Papers #32 and #33 together show F. prausnitzii is protective in both immune (monocyte IL-10) and compositional (mucosal stability in Crohn's) terms. Its depletion appears at multiple disease stages. Converges with mGem review in mBio (#37 supporting literature).
