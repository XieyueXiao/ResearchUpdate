### 1. Experimental human colonisation with non-toxigenic Clostridioides difficile: a placebo-controlled randomised clinical trial

- **Journal:** Nature Communications (IF ~17)
- **Highlights:** Hensen, Harmanus, Verbeek-Menken et al. conducted the first placebo-controlled RCT of deliberate human colonisation with a non-toxigenic strain of *Clostridioides difficile* (NTCD) to investigate microbiome-mediated colonisation resistance. Healthy volunteers receiving antibiotics were randomised to oral NTCD spores or placebo; colonisation success and gut microbiome dynamics were tracked longitudinally. The study demonstrated that antibiotic-mediated disruption of the commensal gut microbiota — particularly with prolonged treatment — markedly increases susceptibility to *C. difficile* engraftment, while a compositionally intact microbiome confers resistance. The trial establishes a safe clinical platform for mechanistic study of colonisation resistance in humans and validates NTCD as a potential prophylactic strategy against toxigenic *C. difficile* infection by competing for the same ecological niche, paving the way for live biotherapeutic approaches targeting recurrent CDI prevention.
- **Link:** [doi:10.1038/s41467-026-74327-y](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41467-026-74327-y) *(DOI reported by index; direct journal page inaccessible for confirmation)*

### 2. Early-life proteomic and microbiome features signal obesity risk across 26 years of follow-up

- **Journal:** mSystems (IF ~6)
- **Highlights:** Ahrens, Dias, Hyötyläinen, Anderson White, Orešič, Triplett, and Ludvigsson leveraged the Swedish ABIS birth cohort (n = 16,683) to identify early-life biological signals predicting obesity up to age 26 (mean follow-up 25.3 years). Using cord blood proteomics and gut microbiome profiling at age one, integrated with machine-learning models, the study showed that in addition to parental BMI, specific proteomic and microbial markers present in infancy significantly predict long-term obesity risk. Key signals implicated bile acid signalling as a mechanistic bridge connecting early microbiome composition with later adiposity and insulin dysregulation. This represents one of the longest-follow-up multi-omic studies linking neonatal/infant gut microbiome features to adult metabolic outcomes, underscoring early-life microbiome programming as a plausible therapeutic target for obesity prevention.
- **Link:** [doi:10.1128/msystems.01424-25](https://doi.org/10.1128/msystems.01424-25)
