## Papers Found (2026-07-11)

### 1. Human gut flagellome profiling using FlaPro reveals TLR5-related phenotype-specific alterations in IBD
- **Journal:** Gut Microbes (IF ~12)
- **Highlights:** Bacterial flagellin is a potent TLR5 ligand, but the diversity and immunogenic potential of the gut "flagellome" (the collective flagellin repertoire of the microbiome) has been hard to profile at scale. Bogdanova et al. developed FlaPro, a computational pipeline that quantifies gut flagellin diversity from metagenomic data and functionally annotates variants using a machine-learning model trained on flagellins with known TLR5-binding/stimulatory activity. Applied to a multi-omics IBD cohort, FlaPro revealed markedly reduced flagellome diversity in IBD patients versus controls, along with a lower ratio of immunologically "silent" to TLR5-stimulatory flagellin variants, linking altered flagellin composition to inflammatory signaling in IBD. The tool establishes flagellome features as a new, functionally interpretable biomarker class for microbiome-wide association studies, extending beyond taxonomic/gene-abundance profiling to immune-relevant protein-variant function.
- **Link:** [bioRxiv preprint, DOI: 10.1101/2025.06.30.660334](https://doi.org/10.1101/2025.06.30.660334) (Gut Microbes DOI pending confirmation — direct page-fetch verification blocked this cycle, see Notes)

### 2. Beyond the gut: the multisite microbiota and its emerging role in brain health
- **Journal:** Gut Microbes (IF ~12)
- **Highlights:** Gut-brain axis research has dominated microbiome-neuroscience literature, but Jabbari Shiadeh et al. argue in this review that other body-site microbiota — oral, skin, respiratory, and urogenital — are increasingly implicated in neuroimmune signaling and neurological outcomes yet remain comparatively understudied. The review synthesizes emerging evidence for these non-gut microbiome-brain links (e.g., oral pathobionts and neuroinflammation, skin/respiratory microbiota and immune priming) while explicitly cautioning that most such associations are indirect, heterogeneous across studies, and not yet causally established. The authors propose a "multisite microbiota-brain" framework as a research priority, positioning gut-brain-axis work as one component of a broader whole-body microbiome-neuroscience research program.
