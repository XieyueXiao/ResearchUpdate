### 1. Artificial intelligence-driven donor-recipient gut microbiome matching for optimized fecal microbiota transplantation (MOZAIC)

- **Journal:** Cell Reports (IF ~8)
- **Highlights:** Siew Chien Ng, Jun Yu Peng, and colleagues at CUHK developed MOZAIC (Microbiome Matching Optimization via Artificial Intelligence), an AI framework that integrates multi-kingdom (bacterial, fungal, viral) and functional metagenomics profiles to predict post-FMT microbial convergence of recipients toward donors. Across 515 FMTs spanning 30 cohorts and 12 diseases (including rCDI, IBD, metabolic syndrome), MOZAIC achieves AUC 0.88 and accuracy/recall >0.80 for convergence prediction, with 78.7% accuracy for clinical outcome prediction; retrospective simulation suggests a 1.44-fold improvement in clinical response rate over unmatched FMT (49.4% → 71.0% in rCDI). The work establishes donor-recipient microbiome convergence as the principal determinant of FMT efficacy and provides a data-driven matching tool for prospective trial design.
- **Link:** [doi:10.1016/j.celrep.2026.115834 (pii S2211-1247(26)00379-7)](https://www.cell.com/cell-reports/fulltext/S2211-1247(26)00379-7)

### 2. Gut microbial metabolism of Flutamide attenuates its therapeutic efficacy against prostate cancer

- **Journal:** Gut Microbes (IF ~12)
- **Highlights:** Shujing Li et al. demonstrate that flutamide — a first-generation antiandrogen drug for recurrent prostate cancer — is substantially biotransformed by gut bacteria into inactive/antagonistic metabolites: FLU-6 via nitroreduction and FLU-9 via N-acetylation, with *Escherichia coli* and other intestinal Enterobacteriaceae identified as primary metabolizing species. Ex vivo human stool incubations and germ-free mouse rescue experiments confirm that antibiotic co-administration partially restores flutamide anti-tumour efficacy by reducing microbial drug metabolism. The findings establish a pharmacomicrobiomics basis for the wide inter-patient variability in flutamide response and suggest that microbiome profiling or targeted antibiotic co-dosing could improve outcomes in patients with androgen-receptor-positive prostate cancer.
- **Link:** [doi:10.1080/19490976.2026.2682803](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/19490976.2026.2682803)

### 3. Colonic nutritional and physicochemical parameters drive gut microbiota dysbiosis in obesity: what we learned from the Ob-M-ARCOL model development

- **Journal:** Gut Microbes (IF ~12)
- **Highlights:** Ophélie Uriot et al. used the Ob-M-ARCOL (Obese-Microbiota-ARtificial COLon) *in vitro* fermentation model — designed to replicate colonic physicochemical and nutritional conditions of obese individuals — to disentangle environmental drivers of obesity-associated dysbiosis from the microbial donors themselves. Transferring healthy donor microbiota into the Ob-M-ARCOL environment (characterised by higher fat flux, altered pH, and reduced mucin) reproduced key dysbiotic hallmarks: depletion of *Faecalibacterium prausnitzii*, enrichment of *Enterobacteriaceae*, and reduced SCFA output. The results argue that the colonic luminal environment is a primary architect of obesity-associated dysbiosis, independent of the baseline microbiota composition, with implications for designing interventions that target host physiology rather than microbiota composition alone.
- **Link:** [doi:10.1080/19490976.2026.XXXXXXX](https://www.tandfonline.com/journals/kgmi20) *(DOI pending full indexing — published online 9 Jun 2026)*

### 4. The gut-bone axis: impact of diet on gut microbiome and osteoporosis *(bonus — Bone Research, Nature portfolio)*

- **Journal:** Bone Research (Nature portfolio; IF ~12)
- **Highlights:** Yue Wang, Miao Xu, Qinzuo Dong et al. present a comprehensive review synthesising evidence on how dietary patterns modulate gut microbiota composition and metabolic activity to influence skeletal health, published as volume 14, article 61. The review covers SCFA-driven Treg induction (reducing osteoclastogenesis), secondary bile acid–VDR signalling in osteoblasts, tryptophan–serotonin–kynurenine axis contributions to bone mineral density, and how specific dietary components (fermentable fibre, polyphenols, omega-3 fatty acids, calcium-phosphate ratio) differentially reshape the microbiome toward or away from bone-protective profiles. The authors propose personalised nutritional strategies targeting the gut-bone axis as a novel adjunct to pharmacological osteoporosis therapy, with microbiome biomarkers identified as candidate treatment-response predictors in high-risk populations (post-menopausal women, elderly).
- **Link:** [doi:10.1038/s41413-026-00550-4](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41413-026-00550-4)
