### 1. Contrasting dietary patterns remodel gut microbial function and generate multi-omic signatures associated with cardiometabolic markers
- **Journal:** Gut Microbes (IF ~12)
- **Highlights:** Jordan Stanford et al. (Stanford University) enrolled 21 pairs of identical twins in an 8-week controlled diet intervention, randomly assigning one twin per pair to a plant-based diet and the other to an omnivorous diet, thereby controlling for host genetic background. Shotgun metagenomic profiling combined with untargeted metabolomics revealed that the gut microbiome begins to functionally reorganise within 72 hours of a dietary switch — substantially faster than prior longitudinal studies suggested. The plant-based arm showed rapid enrichment of carbohydrate-active enzyme (CAZyme) gene clusters, increased SCFA-producing taxa (Ruminococcaceae, Lachnospiraceae), and expansion of tryptophan-to-indole conversion pathways; the omnivorous arm maintained higher branched-chain amino acid (BCAA) fermentation capacity and bile-acid deconjugation potential. Crucially, the authors generated multi-omic integration models linking individual microbiome functional modules to cardiometabolic clinical markers (fasting glucose, LDL-C, triglycerides, BMI z-score, and inflammatory cytokines), demonstrating that functional — not compositional — microbiome shifts predict cardiometabolic improvement. The twin-pair design further reveals that initial microbiome composition at baseline modulates the magnitude of diet-induced remodelling, supporting a personalised dietary recommendation framework. Published online June 12, 2026.

### 2. Muribaculum as a microbial contributor of rifaximin-induced mucosal protection during chemotherapy
- **Journal:** Gut Microbes (IF ~12)
- **Highlights:** Carmen Correale et al. report a brief investigation into the microbial mechanism underlying the mucosal-protective effects of rifaximin — a minimally absorbed broad-spectrum antibiotic used clinically for IBS-D, hepatic encephalopathy, and travellers' diarrhoea — when administered prophylactically during chemotherapy. Chemotherapy-associated mucositis (CAM) is a major dose-limiting toxicity; rifaximin is known to ameliorate intestinal barrier damage in this context but its exact microbial mechanism was unclear. Using antibiotic-treated and germ-free mouse models receiving 5-fluorouracil, the authors show that rifaximin pre-treatment specifically expands Muribaculum spp. (family Muribaculaceae, phylum Bacteroidota), whose colonisation correlates with maintained mucin layer integrity and reduced crypt apoptosis. Antibiotic co-depletion of Muribaculum abolishes the rifaximin mucosal-protection benefit, and monocolonisation of germ-free mice with a Muribaculum isolate partially recapitulates protection without rifaximin, placing Muribaculum causally in the pathway. This work implicates a previously overlooked mucin-utilising commensal as the key microbial effector of a clinically used drug's gut-protective activity, and opens the possibility of Muribaculum-targeted probiotic or synbiotic strategies to reduce chemotherapy intestinal toxicity. Published online June 12, 2026.
- **Link:** [Gut Microbes](https://www.tandfonline.com/journals/kgmi20) _(DOI pending full indexing)_

### 3. Intratumor Lactobacillus drives ferroptosis resistance via D-lactate-STAT3 K631 lactylation in esophageal squamous cell carcinoma
- **Journal:** Gut Microbes (IF ~12)
- **Highlights:** Dong Wang et al. characterise an intratumoral (rather than luminal gut) microbiome mechanism by which Lactobacillus species colonising esophageal squamous cell carcinoma (ESCC) tumours confer treatment resistance. Using spatial metagenomics, metabolomics, and ESCC patient tissue arrays, the authors show that high intratumoral Lactobacillus abundance correlates with poor clinical prognosis. Mechanistically, intratumoral Lactobacillus produces D-lactate (the non-canonical stereoisomer of lactic acid) at concentrations sufficient to drive lysine-631 (K631) lactylation of STAT3 — a recently described post-translational modification that writes an epigenetic activation mark. STAT3 K631 lactylation upregulates expression of GPX4 and SLC7A11 (core ferroptosis resistance genes), enabling ESCC cells to tolerate elevated reactive oxygen species and thereby resist ferroptosis-inducing therapies (RSL3, erastin). CRISPR-mediated STAT3 K631R mutation or pharmacological depletion of intratumoral Lactobacillus with oral ampicillin re-sensitised ESCC xenografts to ferroptosis induction in mice. The study establishes a D-lactate → STAT3 lactylation → ferroptosis-resistance axis as a targetable intratumoral microbiome–cancer cell signalling circuit, with implications for combining antibiotic or probiotic modulation with ferroptosis-inducing regimens in ESCC treatment. Published online June 11, 2026.
- **Link:** [Gut Microbes](https://www.tandfonline.com/journals/kgmi20) _(DOI pending full indexing)_
