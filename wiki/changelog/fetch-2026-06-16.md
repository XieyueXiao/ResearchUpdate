### 1. Human immunodeficiency virus-associated gut microbiome impacts systemic immunodeficiency and susceptibility to opportunistic gut infection
- **Journal:** Nature Microbiology (IF ~28)
- **Highlights:** Bashiardes, Heinemann, Adlung et al. (Ben-Gurion University of the Negev / Hadassah-Hebrew University Medical Center) performed shotgun metagenomic profiling of stool from ~70 people living with HIV (PLWH) in Israel and a similar cohort in Ethiopia, sampled longitudinally across the course of infection. HIV-associated dysbiosis was geographically divergent: a Bacteroides-to-Prevotella shift in the Israeli cohort versus expansion of multiple Enterobacteriaceae (including E. coli and Klebsiella quasivariicola) in the Ethiopian cohort, the latter raising concern for bacterial translocation and sepsis risk in resource-limited settings. To test causality, the authors performed fecal microbiome transplantation into germ-free/antibiotic-treated mice using donor stool from PLWH with high versus low peripheral CD4+ T-cell counts: transplants from high-CD4+ donors (but not low-CD4+ donors) expanded colonic epithelium-associated CD4+ T cells and improved clearance of Cryptosporidium parvum oocysts, directly linking microbiome composition to host immune reconstitution and opportunistic-infection resistance. The work positions the gut microbiome as both a biomarker and a potential causal lever for immunodeficiency severity in HIV, motivating microbiome-targeted adjuncts to antiretroviral therapy. Published online February 16, 2026; missed in all prior fetches (2026-04-28 through 2026-06-15) — surfaced today via broader search.
- **Link:** [doi:10.1038/s41564-025-02253-8](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41564-025-02253-8)

### 2. Diet and microbiome shape small-molecule cytokinin pools in mammals
- **Journal:** Gut Microbes (IF ~12)
- **Highlights:** Othman, Bencurova, Ferretti, Bork, Dandekar, Naseem et al. report that cytokinins (CKs) — adenine-derived small-molecule hormones best known from plants — are detectable in sera and tissues of pigs, dogs, mice, cats, and humans, albeit at lower levels than in plants. Feeding/starvation experiments in C57BL mice showed strong dietary modulation of circulating CK pools, while germ-free mice had markedly reduced CK levels relative to conventionally raised mice, implicating the gut microbiome as a major contributor to mammalian CK pools. However, phylogenetic analysis of the tRNA-isopentenyltransferase 1 (TRIT1) enzyme across mammalian genomes supports a parallel endogenous (host) biosynthetic route, indicating CK pools in mammals arise from a three-way interplay of diet, microbiome metabolism, and host tRNA-derived synthesis. The review proposes CKs as an underexplored class of diet-microbiome-host signaling molecules warranting investigation in metabolic and immune contexts. Published online June 12, 2026.
- **Link:** [Gut Microbes](https://www.tandfonline.com/journals/kgmi20) _(DOI pending full indexing)_
