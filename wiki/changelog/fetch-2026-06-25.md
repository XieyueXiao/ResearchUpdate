### 1. Urolithin A activates aryl hydrocarbon receptor-NLRP6-mediated pathways in intestinal epithelial cells to modulate mucosal immunity and strengthen gut barrier integrity

- **Journal:** Nature Communications (IF ~17)
- **Highlights:** Urolithin A (UroA) is a microbiota-derived metabolite produced from dietary polyphenols (pomegranates, walnuts, berries) by gut bacteria including Enterocloster species. Researchers at the University of Louisville showed, using intestinal epithelial cell models and murine colitis systems, that UroA selectively activates the aryl hydrocarbon receptor (AhR) in intestinal epithelial cells — a previously undescribed specificity in the context of gut barrier biology. This AhR activation triggered the NLRP6 inflammasome, resulting in downstream secretion of barrier-repair molecules, increased mucin production, and enhanced antimicrobial peptide release without inducing pathological inflammation. The study is the first to mechanistically link a diet-derived microbial metabolite to the AhR–NLRP6 axis in intestinal epithelial cells, defining a new gut–diet–microbiome–immunity circuit with direct relevance to IBD and barrier dysfunction. The findings position UroA and NLRP6-activating strategies as tractable therapeutic leads for intestinal barrier restoration.
- **Link:** [doi:10.1038/s41467-026-73760-3](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41467-026-73760-3)

### 2. Angptl4 integrates dietary and microbial signals to disrupt gut barrier function in MASH

- **Journal:** Nature Communications (IF ~17)
- **Highlights:** A team at Nanyang Technological University Singapore (Chua D., Low Z.S., Kim J.H.S. et al.) used intestinal-specific Angptl4 (angiopoietin-like 4) knockout mice, humanized microbiota colonization models, transcriptomics, and a human fecal cohort to identify intestinal Angptl4 as a central hub integrating dietary fat and gut microbial signals to destabilize the gut barrier and drive early hepatic oxidative stress in metabolic dysfunction-associated steatohepatitis (MASH). High-fat diet upregulated intestinal Angptl4 in a microbiota-dependent manner; its deletion protected against barrier disruption, bacterial translocation, and MASLD progression. In human subjects, fecal Angptl4 levels tracked with dysbiosis and metabolic dysfunction, capturing a gut barrier-related pathological dimension distinct from circulating endotoxemia markers. The results position intestinal Angptl4 as a precision-medicine target for barrier-targeted MASLD interventions, linking the diet–microbiota–gut-liver axis through a druggable molecular node. Published May 22, 2026; not captured in any prior fetch due to indexing lag.
- **Link:** [doi:10.1038/s41467-026-72575-6](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41467-026-72575-6)

### 3. Modelling the role of the microbiome in antimicrobial resistance across scales

- **Journal:** Nature Microbiology (IF ~28)
- **Highlights:** Pagani L., León-Sampedro R., Amicone M. et al. present a Perspective proposing the first integrative framework for modelling microbiome–antimicrobial resistance (AMR) interactions across biological scales — from within-host ecological competition to population-level transmission dynamics. The authors synthesize evidence that the gut and other body-site microbiomes are active participants in AMR ecology: commensal communities mediate colonization resistance against resistant pathogens, serve as reservoirs for horizontal gene transfer, and shape the evolutionary trajectory of resistance alleles under antibiotic selection pressure. The Perspective outlines mechanistic categories (ecological exclusion, gene-transfer networks, immune priming, metabolic cross-feeding affecting antibiotic efficacy) and identifies critical data gaps in surveillance datasets that prevent current AMR predictive models from accounting for microbiome state. A roadmap for integrating metagenomics-resolved resistome and taxonomic data into transmission models is proposed, with implications for one-health AMR stewardship and clinical microbiome-informed prescribing.
- **Link:** [doi:10.1038/s41564-026-02390-8](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41564-026-02390-8)
