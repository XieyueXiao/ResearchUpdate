### 1. Dereplication-Assisted Culturomics Enables Strain-Level Ecological Analysis of the Human Gut Microbiome

- **Journal:** Gut Microbes (IF ~12)
- **Highlights:** Yeo and colleagues present a dereplication-assisted culturomics pipeline designed to improve the throughput and ecological resolution of culture-based gut microbiome studies. By applying genome-based dereplication to collapse redundant isolates before downstream analysis, the approach enables recovery of strain-level diversity that is systematically underrepresented in both conventional culture workflows and shotgun metagenomics; the authors demonstrate its utility for mapping ecologically meaningful strain variation across donors and disease states. The study provides a practical methodology for translating culturomics data into quantitative ecological inference at the strain level, filling a key gap between culture collections and population-scale metagenomics.

### 2. The Impact of Gut Microbiota and Metabolite-Driven Immune Cell Spatiotemporal Dynamics on Tumors

- **Journal:** Gut Microbes (IF ~12)
- **Highlights:** In this brief report, Ding and colleagues examine how gut-derived microbial metabolites shape the spatiotemporal distribution and functional states of immune cells within the tumor microenvironment. Integrating spatial transcriptomics with microbiome-metabolome profiling, the study maps how specific bacterial metabolites — including short-chain fatty acids, secondary bile acids, and tryptophan-derived indoles — create immunostimulatory or immunosuppressive micro-niches within tumors, altering the colocalization patterns of cytotoxic T cells, myeloid cells, and regulatory T cells. Findings highlight the gut–tumor immune axis as a tractable therapeutic target, with implications for predicting immunotherapy response based on microbiome metabolite signatures.
- **Link:** DOI pending full indexing (published online 2026-05-28 in *Gut Microbes*)

### 3. Myco-Foods and the Gut Microbiome: Impacts of Mycelial Extracts, Biomass, and Mold-Fermented Foods

- **Journal:** Gut Microbes (IF ~12)
- **Highlights:** Keigler and colleagues present a comprehensive review of how myco-foods — including mycelial biomass preparations, fungal extracts, and mold-fermented foods such as tempeh, miso, and koji-based products — interact with the gut microbiome. The review synthesises evidence from clinical trials and preclinical models showing that β-glucans, chitin, and bioactive secondary metabolites from edible fungi selectively enrich SCFA-producing bacteria (notably Bifidobacterium and Faecalibacterium), modulate the mycobiome–bacteriome balance, and exert prebiotic-like immunomodulatory effects through Dectin-1 and TLR2 signalling. Myco-foods emerge as a structurally and functionally distinct dietary category with potential applications in gut microbiome-targeted interventions, distinct from conventional plant-fibre prebiotics.
- **Link:** DOI pending full indexing (published online 2026-05-27/28 in *Gut Microbes*)

### 4. Decoding the Microbiome: Artificial Intelligence-Targeted Gut Microenvironment Breakthroughs in Personalized Cancer Therapy

- **Journal:** Gut Microbes (IF ~12)
- **Highlights:** Liu and colleagues review how artificial intelligence and machine learning are being applied to decode the complex gut microenvironment in the context of cancer therapy, synthesising recent advances in microbiome-guided oncology across immune checkpoint blockade, chemotherapy, and targeted therapy settings. The review covers AI-driven multi-omics integration platforms that link microbial taxonomic and functional profiles, host immune phenotypes, and tumour microenvironment data to predict treatment response; large-language-model-based tools are highlighted for their potential to generate personalised microbiome intervention recommendations from electronic health record and sequencing inputs. Key challenges discussed include training data heterogeneity, the need for prospective validation cohorts, and regulatory pathways for AI-microbiome companion diagnostics in oncology.
- **Link:** DOI pending full indexing (published online 2026-05-29 in *Gut Microbes*)

### 5. Dual Human Milk Oligosaccharide–Fibre Utilisation Drives Gut Microbiome Selection During Weaning

- **Journal:** Nature Communications (IF ~17)
- **Highlights:** Analysing metagenomes of faecal samples and enriched microbial communities from an infant-mother dyad cohort across three weaning stages, researchers show that the capacity to degrade both human milk oligosaccharides (HMOs) and dietary fibres simultaneously is a defining metabolic hallmark of the transitional gut microbiota during weaning. Taxa capable of dual HMO–fibre utilisation — primarily specialised Bifidobacterium and Bacteroides lineages — are preferentially selected into the core adult-like microbiome during the solid-food introduction window, while HMO-specialists without fibre-degrading capacity decline. The findings establish dual substrate utilisation as a key ecological filter shaping microbiome assembly at weaning and suggest that synbiotic interventions pairing specific HMOs with complementary dietary fibres could optimise the developmental microbiome transition.
- **Link:** Preprint: [doi:10.1101/2025.07.24.666491](https://doi.org/10.1101/2025.07.24.666491); journal DOI pending full indexing (published in *Nature Communications* 2026-05-29)

### 6. Human Milk Oligosaccharide Mediates Mutualism Between *Escherichia coli* and *Bifidobacterium bifidum*

- **Journal:** Nature Communications (IF ~17) — **newly confirmed; published April 2026, missed in prior catch-up fetches**
- **Highlights:** Seki, Dieli-Crimi, and colleagues performed longitudinal shotgun metagenomics on stool from 41 healthy, term-born, breastfed infants and their 30 mothers, revealing that 2′-fucosyllactose — the dominant HMO in human milk — mediates an unexpected mutualism between commensal *E. coli* and *Bifidobacterium bifidum*. Strain-resolved profiling demonstrated that *B. bifidum* is auxotrophic for cysteine and relies on *E. coli* to supply this amino acid in exchange for cooperative 2′-FL degradation, a cross-feeding interaction that keeps *E. coli* at low, stable abundances while sustaining Bifidobacterium-dominant early-life microbiota. The findings reframe *E. coli* in the breastfed infant gut not as a mere pathobiont but as a conditionally beneficial partner whose persistence is governed by HMO-driven ecological exchange, with implications for infant formula design and probiotic strategies targeting the developmental microbiome.
- **Link:** [doi:10.1038/s41467-026-71764-7](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41467-026-71764-7)

### 7. Metabolite Mimicry Identifies Butyrate Analogs with Select Protective Functions in the Intestinal Mucosa

- **Journal:** PNAS (IF ~12) — **newly confirmed; published 2026, not captured in any prior fetch**
- **Highlights:** Dowdell, Roer, and Colgan (University of Colorado Anschutz) applied a metabolite mimicry strategy — systematically synthesising structural analogues of the gut microbial metabolite butyrate — to dissect which of butyrate's pleiotropic intestinal effects are mediated by specific chemical moieties versus shared scaffold features. Screening identified 3-chlorobutyrate (3-Cl BA) as a compound that recapitulates butyrate's beneficial enhancement of epithelial barrier formation and wound healing *in vitro* while lacking its detrimental influences on intestinal stem cells and its capacity to stabilise HIF or serve as a mitochondrial fuel substrate in colonocytes. In a DSS colitis mouse model, 3-Cl BA was protective in contrast to native butyrate, establishing metabolite mimicry as a precision strategy for engineering microbiome-metabolite-inspired therapeutics with improved safety profiles for inflammatory bowel disease.
- **Link:** [doi:10.1073/pnas.2527863123](https://doi.org/10.1073/pnas.2527863123)
