### 1. 15-strain live biotherapeutic product or same donor fecal microbiota transplant for recurrent *Clostridioides difficile* infection: a randomized phase 1b trial

- **Journal:** Nature Medicine (IF ~82)
- **Published:** 2026-06-02
- **Authors:** Grinspan AJ et al. (Icahn School of Medicine at Mount Sinai; co-senior and co-corresponding author Ari Grinspan)
- **Highlights:** This phase 1b randomized trial (n = 18) is the first head-to-head comparison of a same-donor-sourced undefined fecal microbiota transplant (FMT) against an in vitro-manufactured, fully defined 15-strain live biotherapeutic product (LBP), designated MTC01, for the treatment of recurrent *Clostridioides difficile* infection (rCDI). Participants were randomized to four arms — low- and high-dose FMT and low- and high-dose LBP (4–5 per arm) — and received a single endoscopic infusion. The primary safety and efficacy endpoint at 8 weeks showed comparable rCDI prevention: 7 of 9 LBP patients vs. 8 of 9 FMT patients remained recurrence-free. Crucially, MTC01 engraftment in the recipient gut was superior to FMT at the higher dose, demonstrating that a precisely defined, scalable drug product can out-engraft undefined stool-based therapy despite equal or superior clinical efficacy. The platform represents a major step toward standardized, manufactured microbiome therapeutics that eliminate donor screening variability, pathogen transmission risk, and lot-to-lot inconsistency inherent to FMT, and supports a regulatory path for LBPs as approvable drugs for rCDI and other indications.
- **Link:** [doi:10.1038/s41591-026-04442-2](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41591-026-04442-2)

### 2. Engineered *Bacteroides thetaiotaomicron* sense gut inflammation and deliver therapeutic molecules to alleviate colitis in mice

- **Journal:** Nature Microbiology (IF ~28)
- **Published:** ~2026-06-01 (press coverage and indexing confirmed this week)
- **Authors:** [Primary authors not yet fully indexed]; Jennifer A. Philips authored the companion News & Views commentary in the same issue
- **Highlights:** This study introduces "Btbots" — an engineered strain of the commensal gut bacterium *Bacteroides thetaiotaomicron* — that uses a synthetic genetic AND circuit to detect two concurrent inflammatory signals in the gut: nitric oxide (a marker of macrophage activation) and deoxycholic acid (a secondary bile acid elevated during colitis). Only when both signals are simultaneously sensed does the circuit activate expression and secretion of two therapeutic proteins: trefoil factor 3 (TFF3), which promotes epithelial repair, and interleukin-35 (IL-35), a potent anti-inflammatory cytokine. In multiple mouse models of colitis, Btbot colonization significantly attenuated disease activity, demonstrating the power of two-input logic gating to achieve precise, inflammation-conditioned in-situ therapy. The use of a native gut commensal rather than a foreign chassis (e.g., *E. coli* Nissle) offers improved colonization stability and safety profile. This work advances the concept of programmable living therapeutics and provides a blueprint for multi-signal sensing in gut bacteria as a general framework for targeted delivery of biologics to inflamed tissue.
- **Link:** [doi:10.1038/s41564-026-02378-4](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41564-026-02378-4)
