### 1. Regulation of gut epithelial barrier and tuft/goblet cell responses by microbiome repair: Opportunities and future directions
- **Journal:** PNAS (IF ~12)
- **Highlights:** Wang Z, Xie J, Liu S, Han M, and Wang L present a Perspective synthesising current knowledge on how microbiome-directed therapeutic interventions reshape the intestinal epithelial barrier at a cell-type-resolved level, responding to and extending findings from prior PNAS research on microbiome-directed complementary food (MDCF-2) in undernourished children. The authors highlight that microbiome repair is accompanied by increased expression of junctional proteins — including JAMs, Claudins, and ZO-1 — operating between enterocytes, tuft cells, goblet cells, and enteroendocrine cells, indicating a coordinated barrier-sealing response. They examine the nuanced role of microbial metabolites: butyrate, while broadly associated with barrier protection, is shown to suppress tuft cell differentiation via HDAC3 inhibition in intestinal stem cells, thereby dampening type 2 immunity — a cautionary signal for one-size-fits-all butyrate supplementation. The Perspective maps remaining gaps (how specific taxa drive tuft vs. goblet cell specification; how the magnitude of barrier restoration relates to clinical outcomes in malnutrition; the translational pathway from gnotobiotic models to human trials) and proposes mechanistic research agendas. Published in PNAS vol 123, no 24, June 16, 2026.
- **Link:** [DOI: 10.1073/pnas.2535289123](https://doi.org/10.1073/pnas.2535289123)

### 2. From diet to function: using synthetic microbial communities to map gut microbial interactions
- **Journal:** npj Biofilms and Microbiomes (Nature portfolio; IF ~9, bonus journal not on primary target list)
- **Highlights:** Senoo DKJ, Acton L, and Hall LJ (Quadram Institute and partner institutions) provide a comprehensive review of synthetic microbial community (SynCom) approaches as defined experimental systems for dissecting how dietary substrates reshape gut microbial community structure, interspecies metabolism, and host-microbiome crosstalk. The review covers SynCom design rationale — including strain selection from culturomics collections, community complexity tradeoffs, and the transition from binary co-cultures to ecologically realistic consortia — and evaluates experimental platforms ranging from batch fermentation and continuous-flow gut simulators to gnotobiotic mouse colonisation and intestinal organoid models. A substantial section addresses multi-omics readouts (metagenomics, metatranscriptomics, metabolomics, metaproteomics) and how mathematical modelling frameworks (Lotka-Volterra, genome-scale metabolic models, flux balance analysis) can integrate these data to predict community behaviour under dietary perturbation. The authors propose minimum reporting standards (SynCom-MIMARKS extensions) to improve cross-study reproducibility, and outline future directions including multi-kingdom SynComs that incorporate commensal fungi, phages, and protists, as well as AI-enabled function-first design of communities optimised for target metabolic outputs. An important methodological reference for researchers studying diet–microbiome interactions. Published June 15, 2026.
- **Link:** [DOI: 10.1038/s41522-026-01012-9](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41522-026-01012-9)
