## Papers Found (2026-05-03)

### 1. Sulfated bile acid produced by a human gut commensal alleviates paediatric sepsis in mice
- **Journal:** Nature Microbiology (IF ~28)
- **Highlights:** Using paediatric sepsis cohorts and gnotobiotic mouse models, this study identifies *Enterococcus raffinosus* as the dominant gut-resident producer (≥80%) of deoxycholic acid 3-sulfate (DCA-3S) — challenging the conventional hepato-centric model of bile acid sulfation. In vitro reconstitution and targeted metabolomics in children with sepsis showed that DCA-3S levels significantly mediated the inverse association between *E. raffinosus* abundance and sepsis severity. Germ-free mice colonised with DCA-3S-producing strains had markedly improved survival in caecal-ligation-puncture models, pointing to a gut-encoded bile-acid circuit as a tractable target for sepsis prevention in neonates and young children.
- **Link:** [DOI: 10.1038/s41564-026-02351-1](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41564-026-02351-1)

### 2. A microbiota-IPA axis facilitates intestinal stem cell-mediated regeneration in colitis through a Hopx-associated program
- **Journal:** Nature Communications (IF ~17)
- **Highlights:** *Blautia coccoides*, consistently depleted in IBD patients and DSS-colitis mice, was shown to produce indole-3-lactic acid (ILA) from tryptophan; commensal consortia then convert ILA to indole-3-propionic acid (IPA), which stimulates β-hydroxybutyrate (BHB) synthesis in intestinal epithelial cells. BHB epigenetically activates HOPX⁺ reserve intestinal stem cells (ISCs), driving regeneration of the LGR5⁺ ISC pool and accelerating mucosal healing after injury. Supplementation of IPA or *B. coccoides* in colitis mice recapitulated these effects, identifying the microbiota→ILA→IPA→BHB→HOPX⁺ ISC axis as a mechanistic bridge between gut dysbiosis and impaired mucosal repair in IBD. (Companion paper: see paper #3.)
- **Link:** [DOI: 10.1038/s41467-026-70062-6](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41467-026-70062-6)

### 3. Microbiota-derived IPA protects against colitis by regulating intestinal HMGCS2-mediated ketogenesis to facilitate mucosal healing
- **Journal:** Nature Communications (IF ~17)
- **Highlights:** This companion study to paper #2 establishes the mechanistic link at the epithelial metabolic level: IPA produced by gut commensals upregulates HMGCS2 (3-hydroxy-3-methylglutaryl-CoA synthase 2) in intestinal epithelial cells, driving localised ketogenesis and BHB production. HMGCS2-knockout mice showed delayed mucosal healing and worse colitis scores, confirming that ketogenesis is rate-limiting for IPA-mediated protection. Single-cell transcriptomics revealed HMGCS2 induction specifically in colonocytes flanking crypt stem-cell zones, tying metabolic reprogramming to spatial stem-cell niche support. Together, papers #2 and #3 define a two-node microbiota-metabolite-stem-cell circuit with potential for IBD therapy.
- **Link:** [DOI: 10.1038/s41467-026-69341-z](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41467-026-69341-z)

### 4. The landscape of tissue-resident microbiota across normal, polyp, and colorectal cancer tissues
- **Journal:** Nature Communications (IF ~17)
- **Highlights:** A comprehensive cross-sectional analysis of 1,134 clinical biopsy specimens spanning normal colonic mucosa, precancerous polyps, and colorectal cancer (CRC) used 16S rRNA amplicon and shotgun sequencing to resolve tissue-resident microbiota (TRM) profiles at each disease stage. Distinct microbial community structures distinguished all three diagnostic groups, with progressive enrichment of *Fusobacterium nucleatum*, pks⁺ *Escherichia coli*, and *Peptostreptococcus* from normal → polyp → CRC. Integrative analyses with tumor immune microenvironment data showed TRM composition correlated with local CD8⁺ T-cell infiltration and mismatch-repair status but not overtly with tumour stage or survival — suggesting TRM is an early driver rather than a late modifier of CRC biology. Microbial signatures distinguishing polyp from normal tissue point toward microbiome-based early-detection strategies.
- **Link:** [DOI: 10.1038/s41467-026-69705-5](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41467-026-69705-5)

### 5. Gut microbiome–produced bile acid metabolite lengthens the circadian period in host intestinal cells
- **Journal:** PNAS (IF ~12)
- **Highlights:** A phenotypic screen of a focused library of gut microbial metabolites against circadian reporter cells identified lithocholic acid (LCA), a secondary bile acid produced by intestinal bacteria, as a potent dose-responsive lengthener of the circadian period of hPer2 transcription in human colonic organoids. Mechanistically, LCA inhibits the CK1δ/ε–PP1 phosphatase feedback loop and stabilises CRY2 protein, prolonging the ~24-hour clock cycle. In vivo LCA feeding in mice altered circadian gene expression in distal ileum and colon. The findings establish a direct molecular channel by which gut microbial bile-acid metabolism entrains the peripheral intestinal clock, with implications for understanding how dysbiosis or antibiotic-mediated LCA depletion may disrupt circadian rhythms and linked metabolic processes.
- **Link:** [DOI: 10.1073/pnas.2506313123](https://doi.org/10.1073/pnas.2506313123)
