### 1. Genome-Wide Sweeps Create Ecological Units in the Human Gut Microbiome

- **Journal:** Nature (IF ~64)
- **Highlights:** Yu, Polz and colleagues at the University of Vienna used large-scale comparative metagenomics and a "reverse ecology" framework to show that genome-wide selective sweeps — in which a well-adapted clone outcompetes close relatives and then rediversifies — are pervasive across human gut bacterial populations and produce discrete, epidemiologically structured sub-species lineages that the authors term ecological units. Applying this approach across geographically and ethnically diverse human cohorts, they found that these sweep-derived lineages are associated with distinct host conditions including colorectal cancer, inflammatory bowel disease, type 2 diabetes, and advanced age, indicating genuine functional and ecological differentiation below the species level. The findings challenge the common practice of treating gut bacterial species as homogeneous entities in microbiome–disease association studies, and suggest that sweeps can propagate globally within decades, producing population structures akin to global epidemics — with direct implications for biomarker discovery and precision microbiome therapies.
- **Link:** [doi:10.1038/s41586-026-10476-w](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-026-10476-w)

### 2. Drug-Target Homologs Mediate Non-Antibiotic Drug Effects on Gut Bacterial Growth — A Strategy to Reverse MTX-Induced Dysbiosis

- **Journal:** Nature Communications (IF ~17)
- **Highlights:** This Nature Communications study uncovers a previously unrecognized mechanism by which non-antibiotic drugs disrupt the gut microbiome: many therapeutic compounds inadvertently inhibit bacterial enzymes that are homologs of their human drug targets, collaterally impairing the growth of sensitive commensal taxa. The authors demonstrate this principle using methotrexate (MTX), an immunosuppressant and chemotherapeutic agent that causes significant gut dysbiosis as a clinical side effect, and show that the dysbiosis can be reversed by a universal homology-based competition strategy — supplementing bacteria with small molecules that outcompete the drug for its homologous bacterial target. The work provides a framework for predicting microbiome off-target effects of any drug whose human target has a bacterial homolog, and opens a practical therapeutic avenue for mitigating drug-induced dysbiosis without altering drug efficacy.
- **Link:** [https://www.nature.com/ncomms/articles?year=2026](https://www.nature.com/ncomms/articles?year=2026) (confirmed Nature Communications May 4, 2026; specific DOI pending full indexing)

### 3. Oral Microbiome Signatures Predict Biological Age and Host Health

- **Journal:** Nature Communications (IF ~17)
- **Highlights:** Using oral microbiome 16S rRNA sequencing from two U.S. NHANES cohorts totalling 4,675 participants (validated in a third cohort of ~1,300), researchers developed an Oral Microbiome Aging Acceleration (OMAA) Score — the residual from a model predicting chronological age from 64 age-dependent bacterial species. An elevated OMAA Score independently predicted all-cause mortality (HR = 1.05, P = 0.024), frailty (OR = 1.05, P = 0.008), and susceptibility to major age-related chronic diseases including cardiovascular and metabolic conditions, establishing the oral microbiome as a non-invasive quantitative biomarker for biological aging distinct from gut aging clocks. Notable taxa driving the clock include Rothia (positively associated with frailty), Scardovia (reflecting altered carbohydrate metabolism), and Filifactor (linked to periodontal inflammation); aging was associated with loss of commensals such as Streptococcus and Veillonella and enrichment of Fusobacterium, Treponema, and Porphyromonas.
- **Link:** [doi:10.1038/s41467-026-72096-2](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-72096-2)

### 4. A Global Metagenomic Atlas of Aging Identifies a Microbiota Phase Transition Associated with Disease Risk

- **Journal:** npj Biofilms and Microbiomes (IF ~9)
- **Highlights:** Fu, Zhang, He et al. constructed an interpretable gut microbiota age clock using shotgun metagenomic data from 8,115 fecal samples across five continents, discovering a critical inflection point at 56–60 years of chronological age validated in an independent cohort of 2,263 metagenomes. Before and after this transition, the association between gut microbiota age and multiple diseases shifts substantially: the perturbation is characterised by a decline in ecological stability and major abundance changes in core species. Within-species analyses revealed phylogenetic divergence for seven age-related taxa — including Escherichia coli — alongside functional shifts in older individuals toward enhanced cell motility, carbohydrate metabolism, and horizontal gene transfer capacity. The results position the 56–60 year window as a potentially actionable microbiome health checkpoint and provide a globally calibrated reference for evaluating microbiome-aging interventions.
- **Link:** [doi:10.1038/s41522-026-00970-4](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41522-026-00970-4)

### 5. The Celiac Microbiome Repository (CMR): Integrating Global Celiac Disease Gut Microbiome Datasets Reveals Persistent Dysbiosis Across Disease Stages

- **Journal:** Communications Medicine (Nature Portfolio; published May 7, 2026)
- **Highlights:** Prendergast, Bishop et al. integrated gut microbiome sequencing datasets from celiac disease (CeD) patient cohorts spanning multiple disease stages — active disease, treated (gluten-free diet), and refractory — generating the Celiac Microbiome Repository (CMR). Meta-analysis across these globally sourced datasets identified a consistent core dysbiosis signature in CeD: loss of butyrate-producing bacteria (particularly Faecalibacterium prausnitzii, Roseburia, and Clostridiales spp.), altered mucin-associated bacterial communities, and enrichment of opportunistic microbes. Critically, these shifts persisted even after gluten-free dietary intervention, suggesting that microbiome normalisation lags behind clinical remission and that microbiome-targeted co-interventions may be warranted. The CMR itself is offered as an open community resource to standardise future celiac microbiome research across cohorts.
- **Link:** [doi:10.64898/2026.03.28.715053](https://www.biorxiv.org/content/10.64898/2026.03.28.715053v1) (bioRxiv preprint; published Communications Medicine May 7, 2026)
