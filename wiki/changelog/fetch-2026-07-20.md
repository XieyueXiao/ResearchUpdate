### 1. Enteric-colonized Cronobacter spp. can disrupt the central nervous system without translocation across the host barriers
- **Journal:** Gut Microbes (IF ~12)
- **Highlights:** Review/research by Jillinda Yi Ling Toh et al. examining how Cronobacter spp., an enteric pathogen best known for causing severe neonatal infections, can produce neurological disruption without physically crossing the gut epithelial or blood-brain barriers. The work synthesizes evidence for indirect, barrier-independent routes (e.g., microbial metabolites, immune/vagal signaling) by which a gut-resident pathogen can influence CNS function, extending the gut-brain axis literature to a barrier-independent mechanistic model relevant to neonatal and pediatric infection risk.
- **Link:** Gut Microbes (Taylor & Francis), published online 2026-07-16; DOI unresolved (tandfonline.com returned HTTP 403 to automated fetch this cycle — see access note below)

### 2. Lactobacillus johnsonii mediates the protective effects of pristimerin against ulcerative colitis and concomitant liver injury through remodeling hepatic lipid metabolism via LXRα–SCD1 axis
- **Journal:** Gut Microbes (IF ~12)
- **Highlights:** Study showing that pristimerin (a quinonemethide triterpenoid) ameliorates DSS-induced ulcerative colitis and associated liver injury in a gut-liver axis model, with the protective effect mediated by Lactobacillus johnsonii enrichment. Mechanistically, L. johnsonii-dependent signaling remodels hepatic lipid metabolism through the LXRα–SCD1 axis, linking a specific gut commensal to a druggable host lipid-metabolic pathway in a combined gut-liver inflammatory injury model. This resolves a lead flagged as unconfirmed in the 2026-07-19 fetch (inconsistent title/scope details across search snippets at that time).
- **Link:** Gut Microbes (Taylor & Francis), published online 2026-07-17; DOI unresolved (access blocked, see note below)
