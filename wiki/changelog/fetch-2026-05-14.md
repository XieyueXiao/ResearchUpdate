### 1. Gut microbiota responses to bariatric surgery are associated with metabolic outcomes and type 2 diabetes remission
- **Journal:** Nature Metabolism (bonus — not on primary target list; IF ~23)
- **Highlights:** A sub-analysis of the randomised Oseberg trial (RYGB n = 39, sleeve gastrectomy n = 38; obesity + T2D) profiled the fecal microbiome before and 12 months post-surgery. Both procedures shifted the microbiome in the same direction, but changes were larger after RYGB. In the SG arm, surgery-induced microbiome composition correlated positively with circulating GLP-1 levels, beta-cell function, and 5-year T2D remission status. Remission was linked to increased microbial gene richness and enhanced functional potential for fermentation, methanogenesis, and butyrate production — directly implicating the gut microbiome as an active mediator (not merely a bystander) of metabolic improvement after bariatric surgery. A companion News & Views ("Surgically reshaping the gut microbiome," doi:10.1038/s42255-026-01517-9) contextualises the clinical implications.
- **Link:** [doi:10.1038/s42255-026-01525-9](https://doi.org/10.1038/s42255-026-01525-9)
- **Published:** 2026-05-07

### 2. Metatranscriptomic analysis of the microbiota of tumor tissue in colon cancer
- **Journal:** Microbiome — Springer (IF ~15)
- **Highlights:** Buetas, Conde-Pérez, Mira and colleagues (FISABIO, Valencia) analysed the active (transcriptionally expressed) fraction of the tumor-resident microbiota in colon cancer tissue using metatranscriptomics, complementing prior 16S/metagenomics-based analyses that capture DNA regardless of microbial viability. Building on the same team's earlier work identifying a Fusobacterium–Parvimonas–Bacteroides–Faecalibacterium cluster as a CRC precision biomarker, this study characterises which of those taxa are metabolically active within the tumor microenvironment. Full abstract not indexed at time of fetch; content inferred from preprint record (doi:10.21203/rs.3.rs-6213767) and prior published work by these authors.

### 3. Integrative analysis of the mouse cecal microbiome across diet, age, and metabolic state in the diverse BXD population
- **Journal:** Microbiome — Springer (IF ~15)
- **Highlights:** Zhou, Lamanna, Williams and colleagues profiled the cecal metagenome, metatranscriptome, and host transcriptome from 232 mice spanning two diets (chow vs. high-fat), four adult ages (6–24 months), and 43 BXD recombinant inbred strains — a design that disentangles diet, age, host genetics, and their interactions on gut microbiome composition and function. Diet exerted the strongest influence on microbiota composition (>300 species altered under HFD), followed by host genetic background, then age. The BXD panel's known phenotypic diversity enables quantitative-trait locus mapping of microbiota features onto specific host genomic loci. While mouse-centric, this large-scale systems resource establishes causal frameworks applicable to human disease-relevant traits including obesity, metabolic syndrome, and aging.
- **Link:** [Microbiome (Springer)](https://microbiomejournal.biomedcentral.com/articles)
- **Published:** 2026-05-12
