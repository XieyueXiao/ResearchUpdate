### 1. Biogeography-associated emergence of enhanced oxygen tolerance in the abundant human gut commensal *Segatella copri*

- **Journal:** Cell Host & Microbe (IF ~30)
- **Highlights:** Youssef El Mouali et al. (Helmholtz Centre for Infection Research, Braunschweig) discovered that a subset of *Segatella copri* — one of the most abundant human gut commensals — has acquired an oxygen-response transcriptional regulator, OxyR, via horizontal gene transfer from other Bacteroidales, conferring 100- to 1,000-fold greater aerotolerance compared to OxyR-negative strains. Comprehensive comparative genomics of >1,000 *S. copri* genomes and metagenomic surveys across 39 countries revealed a striking biogeographic pattern: OxyR-positive strains are prevalent in industrialised nations but virtually absent in traditional-lifestyle populations in Africa and parts of Asia, and are undetectable in ancient human gut samples. The authors propose that intermittent oxygen influx driven by antibiotic-associated dysbiosis and reduced human-to-human fecal-oral transmission creates selective pressure uniquely present in industrialised gut environments, enabling OxyR+ strains to colonise and persist during transient aerobic episodes that kill obligately anaerobic competitors. The findings reframe *S. copri* population structure as an ecologically differentiated species shaped by modernisation, with OxyR carriage potentially relevant to IBD susceptibility and antibiotic recovery dynamics.
- **Link:** [doi:10.1016/j.chom.2026.04.006](https://www.cell.com/cell-host-microbe/fulltext/S1931-3128(26)00162-9)

### 2. Maternal diet shapes neonatal microbiome ontogenesis and neurometabolic resilience

- **Journal:** Gut Microbes (IF ~12)
- **Highlights:** Saurabh Kadyan et al. present a comprehensive review synthesising evidence that maternal dietary patterns during pregnancy and lactation are primary determinants of neonatal microbiome assembly trajectories and downstream neurometabolic programming. The review traces mechanistic chains from specific dietary components — fermentable fibres, polyphenols, omega-3 fatty acids, and tryptophan — through maternal gut microbial metabolite (SCFA, indole, bile acid) production, transplacental and milk-mediated transfer, to neonatal microbiome seeding and maturation, with particular emphasis on how early microbiome composition influences HPA-axis calibration, brain serotonin/BDNF levels, and metabolic set-points. Evidence from gnotobiotic mouse models, birth cohort studies, and dietary intervention trials is integrated to show that suboptimal maternal diets — high in ultra-processed foods, low in dietary diversity — impair infant *Bifidobacterium*/*Lactobacillus* colonisation and elevate *Enterobacteriaceae*, with lasting consequences for allergy, obesity, and neurodevelopmental disorder risk. The authors propose maternal dietary microbiome profiling as a precision strategy to identify high-risk mother-infant dyads and guide targeted prebiotic/probiotic interventions during the periconceptional and lactation windows.
- **Link:** [doi:10.1080/19490976.2026.2690xxx](https://www.tandfonline.com/journals/kgmi20) *(DOI pending full indexing — published online 10 Jun 2026)*

### 3. The protease code of the bacterial gut microbiota: ecological regulation, barrier disruption, and rewiring host defense and signaling

- **Journal:** Gut Microbes (IF ~12)
- **Highlights:** Ulrich Eckhard et al. present a systematic review of the "protease code" — the ensemble of microbially secreted and cell-surface proteases that collectively shape gut ecology and host–microbiome crosstalk. The review catalogues protease families across dominant gut taxa (Bacteroidota, Firmicutes, Proteobacteria) and maps their ecological functions: competitive exclusion via bacteriocin-processing and quorum-sensing peptide cleavage, mucin degradation and barrier disruption, inactivation of host antimicrobial peptides and pattern-recognition receptors, and remodelling of the intestinal proteome to redirect innate immune signalling. The authors highlight protease-dependent pathogenic mechanisms in IBD (elevated *Bacteroides* and *Proteobacteria* metalloproteases degrading epithelial junctions), IBS (serine protease-PAR2 axis driving visceral hypersensitivity), and CRC (fragilysin/BFT-type metalloproteases from *B. fragilis* cleaving E-cadherin), while also reviewing emerging therapeutic strategies — protease inhibitors, protease-resistant probiotic engineering, and fecal protease activity as non-invasive disease biomarker. The work provides an integrated framework linking microbial protease ecology to host pathophysiology across multiple GI conditions.
- **Link:** [doi:10.1080/19490976.2026.2691xxx](https://www.tandfonline.com/journals/kgmi20) *(DOI pending full indexing — published online 10 Jun 2026)*
