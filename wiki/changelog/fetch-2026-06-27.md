## Papers Found (2026-06-27)

### 1. Comprehensive gut microbiome meta-analysis reveals robust colorectal cancer signature
- **Journal:** Cell Host & Microbe (IF ~30)
- **Highlights:** An international team from the Mi-EOCRC consortium (Zeller and Zimmermann groups, EMBL Heidelberg, LUMC, Germany/Switzerland/Netherlands) reanalyzed 6,779 publicly available gut microbiome sequencing profiles from 27 studies alongside 906 intestinal tissue samples, making this one of the largest single-disease gut microbiome meta-analyses to date. Computational approaches were developed to integrate datasets generated with different sequencing methods at scale, enabling both taxonomic profiling down to bacterial strains and functional analysis of virulence factors. A machine-learning classifier trained on the combined profiles consistently distinguished colorectal cancer from non-cancer microbiomes across datasets, populations, and age-of-onset groups; the CRC-associated microbiome signature was linked to lower dietary fibre intake and could be attenuated through fibre-focused dietary interventions. Comparison of stool-based signals with microbiome data from tumor tissue suggests that CRC-associated microbiome changes may emerge early in disease development, supporting the utility of stool microbiome screening as a non-invasive early detection complement to existing methods.
- **Link:** [doi:10.1016/j.chom.2026.05.030](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.chom.2026.05.030)

### 2. Gut Proteobacteria glycine metabolism regulates neuroplasticity, motivation, and reinstatement of cocaine self-administration in mice
- **Journal:** Gut Microbes (IF ~12)
- **Highlights:** Susana Delgado Ocaña et al. describe a bidirectional gut-brain axis mechanism through which cocaine exposure reshapes the intestinal microbiome to amplify addictive behaviour. Cocaine increases intestinal norepinephrine levels sensed by the bacterial adrenergic receptor QseC, which promotes selective colonisation of γ-Proteobacteria in the gut; the enriched γ-Proteobacteria then deplete the neuroactive amino acid glycine — used as a nitrogen source — both in the gut lumen and cerebrospinal fluid, enhancing cocaine-induced neuroplasticity and motivation for self-administration. Glycine repletion reversed this enhancement, and intestinal colonisation by glycine-uptake-deficient γ-Proteobacteria mutants did not alter the host's cocaine response. Transcriptomic profiling of the nucleus accumbens implicates γ-Proteobacteria-modulated glycine levels in cocaine-induced transcriptional plasticity through the glutamatergic transmission pathway, identifying a microbiome-derived metabolite axis as a candidate target for intervention in substance use disorders.
