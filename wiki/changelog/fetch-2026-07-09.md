## Papers Found (2026-07-09)

### 1. The gut microbiota directs vitamin A flux to regulate intestinal T cell development
- **Journal:** Cell Host & Microbe (IF ~30)
- **Highlights:** Vitamin A availability during early life is a critical determinant of intestinal T cell development, but how the gut microbiota controls its distribution to the immune system was unclear. Using gnotobiotic and antibiotic-treated mouse models, UT Southwestern researchers (Srinivasan, Dende, Ruhn, et al.) show that commensal gut bacteria stimulate intestinal epithelial cells to produce the vitamin A-binding protein serum amyloid A (SAA), which shuttles vitamin A to intestinal immune cells; these cells then carry the nutrient to draining mesenchymal lymph nodes over an ~3-day cellular relay that activates a transcriptional program driving T cell maturation and gut-homing. Germ-free mice show a collapsed SAA-vitamin A relay, with developing T cells failing to mature or migrate to the small intestine, and the pathway is most active during the early-life immune-programming window. The work identifies a previously unrecognized microbiota-nutrient-immune axis and nominates SAA/vitamin A flux as a therapeutic target for disrupted immune development.
- **Link:** [DOI: 10.1016/j.chom.2026.05.019](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.chom.2026.05.019)
