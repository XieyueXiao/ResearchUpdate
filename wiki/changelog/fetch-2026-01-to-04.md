### 1. Meta-analysis of the uncultured gut microbiome across 11,115 global metagenomes reveals a candidate signature of health
- **Journal:** Cell Host & Microbe
- **Highlights:** The majority of gut microbial diversity remains uncultured and functionally uncharacterized. Here, we performed a genome-resolved meta-analysis of 11,115 shotgun metagenomic samples across 39 countries and 13 noncommunicable diseases, reconstructing metagenome-assembled genomes (MAGs) to profile uncultured taxa at scale. We identified the genus CAG-170 (family Oscillospiraceae) as the most consistently health-associated lineage, showing significant enrichment in healthy controls relative to IBD, obesity, and chronic fatigue syndrome cohorts. Functional annotation revealed high vitamin B12 (cobalamin) biosynthesis capacity and extensive polysaccharide degradation pathways, suggesting CAG-170 supports community-wide metabolic stability through cross-feeding. These results position CAG-170 as a keystone taxon of the healthy gut and a candidate target for next-generation probiotic development pending successful cultivation.
- **Link:** [DOI: 10.1016/j.chom.2026.01.013](https://www.cell.com/cell-host-microbe/fulltext/S1931-3128(26)00038-7)

### 2. Intestinal interoceptive dysfunction drives age-associated cognitive decline
- **Journal:** Nature (vol. 652, pp. 442–450)
- **Highlights:** Age-associated cognitive decline has been linked to neuroinflammation, but the upstream triggers remain poorly understood. Here, using gnotobiotic and conventionally raised aged mice, we show that aging promotes the expansion of medium-chain fatty acid (MCFA)-producing gut bacteria, notably Parabacteroides goldsteinii. These MCFAs activate the receptor GPR84 on intestinal myeloid cells, triggering a local inflammatory cascade that impairs vagal afferent interoceptive signaling from the gut to the brain. This disrupted gut-brain communication leads to hippocampal dysfunction and measurable memory loss. Critically, targeted vagus nerve stimulation in aged mice fully restored cognitive performance to levels indistinguishable from young controls. These findings establish a microbiome-driven gut-brain axis as a causal and therapeutically tractable mechanism of age-related cognitive decline.
- **Link:** [DOI: 10.1038/s41586-026-10191-6](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-026-10191-6)

### 3. Diet–microbiome associations in 10,068 individuals from the Human Phenotype Project to guide personalized nutrition
- **Journal:** Nature Medicine
- **Highlights:** Habitual diet is a major modulator of the gut microbiome, yet large-scale quantification of specific food–microbe relationships has been limited. We analyzed app-based dietary logs paired with shotgun metagenomic sequencing in 10,068 participants from the Human Phenotype Project. Diet significantly predicted microbial alpha diversity (Shannon index r = 0.24) and the relative abundance of 92.4% of gut species and 97.8% of metabolic pathways tested. We identified reproducible food–taxon associations, including coffee with Lawsonibacter asaccharolyticus (r = 0.43), yogurt with Streptococcus thermophilus, and milk with Bifidobacterium spp. Using these mappings, we simulated personalized dietary interventions targeting cardiometabolic risk markers, demonstrating that individualized microbiome-informed dietary recommendations outperform population-level guidelines. This work provides the largest diet–microbiome reference to date and a framework for precision nutrition.
- **Link:** [DOI: 10.1038/s41591-026-04312-x](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41591-026-04312-x)

### 4. Antibiotic use and gut microbiome composition links from individual-level prescription data of 14,979 individuals
- **Journal:** Nature Medicine
- **Highlights:** The long-term consequences of antibiotic exposure on the human gut microbiome remain poorly quantified at the population level. We integrated 8 years of individual-level prescription records from the Swedish National Drug Register with fecal shotgun metagenomes from 14,979 adults. Antibiotic use was associated with significant and durable compositional shifts in the gut microbiome, with effects strongest within 1 year of exposure but still detectable at 1–4 and 4–8 years post-prescription. Drug class mattered: clindamycin, fluoroquinolones, and flucloxacillin produced the most extensive species-level perturbations, while narrow-spectrum agents had more limited impact. These results provide population-scale evidence that antibiotic courses leave a lasting imprint on gut microbiome composition years beyond treatment, with implications for antibiotic stewardship and microbiome-aware prescribing.
- **Link:** [DOI: 10.1038/s41591-026-04284-y](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41591-026-04284-y)

### 6. Genome-wide association analyses highlight the role of the intestinal molecular environment in human gut microbiota variation
- **Journal:** Nature Genetics
- **Highlights:** Host genetics contribute to inter-individual variation in gut microbiome composition, but the specific loci and mechanisms involved remain largely uncharacterized. We performed genome-wide association analyses on harmonized shotgun metagenomic data from 16,017 Swedish adults, with replication in 12,652 individuals from the Norwegian HUNT study. We identified 15 study-wide significant loci associated with gut microbiota variation, including novel signals at MUC12 (mucin barrier), CORO7–HMOX2, SLC5A11 (nutrient sensing), FOXP1 (immune regulation), and the fucosyltransferase cluster FUT3–FUT6 (glycan secretion). Notably, variants in olfactory receptor genes OR51E1–OR51E2, which encode sensors for microbiome-derived short-chain fatty acids, were associated with overall microbial richness. Integration with metabolomics and tissue-specific gene expression data supports a model in which host genetic variation shapes the intestinal molecular environment — mucosal glycans, nutrient transporters, immune tone — which in turn selects for specific microbial communities.
- **Link:** [DOI: 10.1038/s41588-026-02512-2](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41588-026-02512-2)

### 7. The HUNT study identifies host genetic factors reproducibly associated with human gut microbiota composition
- **Journal:** Nature Genetics
- **Highlights:** Reproducibility has been a major challenge in host-genetics–microbiome studies due to small cohorts and heterogeneous methods. We conducted a GWAS in 12,652 participants from the Norwegian HUNT study using shotgun metagenomics, with replication across Nordic cohorts totaling 16,017–21,976 individuals. We identified 12 reproducible SNP–species associations at six genomic loci, including established signals (LCT, ABO) and novel associations at HLA-DQB1 (adaptive immunity), MUC12 (mucosal barrier), SLC37A2 (sugar-phosphate transport), and FUT2 (secretor status). Mendelian randomization analyses provided causal evidence that elevated BMI reduces gut microbial alpha diversity. Published back-to-back with the companion Swedish GWAS, these two studies collectively represent the largest host-genetics microbiome investigations to date and converge on intestinal mucosal biology as the primary interface through which host genotype shapes the microbiome.
- **Link:** [DOI: 10.1038/s41588-026-02502-4](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41588-026-02502-4)

### 8. Microbiota-induced T cell plasticity enables immune-mediated tumour control
- **Journal:** Nature (vol. 651, pp. 201–210)
- **Highlights:** The gut microbiome influences cancer immunotherapy efficacy, but the mechanistic basis for this relationship has remained elusive. Using antigen-specific T cell tracking in gnotobiotic tumor-bearing mice, we demonstrate that molecular mimicry between a gut commensal epitope and a tumor neoantigen induces functional plasticity in CD4+ T cells, reprogramming them from a regulatory (Treg-like) to an effector (Th1) phenotype upon encountering the tumor. This microbiota-primed T cell plasticity significantly enhanced the efficacy of anti-PD-1 and anti-CTLA-4 immune checkpoint blockade and restrained tumor growth. Colonization with the mimicry-competent commensal was both necessary and sufficient to confer the effect. These findings provide a long-sought mechanistic framework linking specific gut commensals to anti-tumor immunity and suggest that rational selection of microbiome-based adjuvants could improve immunotherapy response rates.
- **Link:** [DOI: 10.1038/s41586-025-09913-z](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-025-09913-z)

### 9. FMT plus immunotherapy in NSCLC and melanoma: the phase 2 FMT-LUMINate trial
- **Journal:** Nature Medicine
- **Highlights:** Preclinical and early clinical evidence suggests that gut microbiome composition modulates response to immune checkpoint inhibitors, but prospective combination trials remain scarce. In this multicenter, open-label phase 2 trial (FMT-LUMINate), healthy-donor fecal microbiota transplantation (FMT) was administered alongside anti-PD-1 in treatment-naive NSCLC patients (n=20) and combined anti-PD-1/anti-CTLA-4 in advanced melanoma patients (n=20). Both cohorts demonstrated encouraging objective response rates exceeding historical benchmarks. Metagenomic profiling revealed that clinical responders exhibited significantly greater turnover of their baseline bacterial communities post-FMT, suggesting that the degree of microbiome remodeling — rather than engraftment of any single taxon — may predict therapeutic benefit. The trial establishes the safety and feasibility of FMT as a first-line immunotherapy adjuvant and supports larger randomized studies of microbiome modulation in oncology.
- **Link:** [DOI: 10.1038/s41591-025-04186-5](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41591-025-04186-5)

### 10. FMT plus immunotherapy in metastatic renal cell carcinoma: the phase 1 PERFORM trial
- **Journal:** Nature Medicine
- **Highlights:** Immune checkpoint inhibitor (ICI) combinations are standard of care in metastatic renal cell carcinoma (mRCC), but immune-related adverse events (irAEs) limit tolerability. In this phase 1 dose-escalation trial (PERFORM), 20 treatment-naive mRCC patients received encapsulated healthy-donor FMT concurrently with ipilimumab plus nivolumab. FMT was well tolerated with no serious FMT-related toxicities; 50% of patients experienced grade 3 irAEs attributable to the ICI backbone. Longitudinal metagenomic and metabolomic profiling identified specific donor-derived taxa whose engraftment correlated with the severity of immune-related toxicity, suggesting that FMT composition may modulate not only efficacy but also the adverse-effect profile of combination immunotherapy. Published as a companion to the FMT-LUMINate trial, together these studies establish FMT safety across three tumor types and motivate dose-optimization studies.
- **Link:** [DOI: 10.1038/s41591-025-04183-8](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41591-025-04183-8)

### 11. Effector–host interactome map links type III secretion systems in healthy gut microbiomes to immune modulation
- **Journal:** Nature Microbiology
- **Highlights:** Type III secretion systems (T3SS) are canonically viewed as virulence machinery restricted to pathogenic bacteria. Here, through systematic computational screening of over 8,000 gut-derived metagenome-assembled genomes, we show that approximately 80% of Pseudomonadota in healthy human gut microbiomes possess intact, expressed T3SS operons. Using high-throughput yeast two-hybrid screens and co-immunoprecipitation validation, we constructed a protein–protein meta-interactome of over 1,000 commensal effector–human host protein interactions, revealing that effectors preferentially target host immune regulatory and metabolic signaling nodes (NF-κB, MAPK, ubiquitin-proteasome pathways). Disease-association analysis across IBD cohorts revealed that T3SS effector-encoding genes are enriched in Crohn's disease microbiomes but depleted in ulcerative colitis, suggesting context-dependent roles in intestinal inflammation. These findings fundamentally reframe T3SS as a commensal communication tool for immune modulation, not solely a pathogenic weapon.
- **Link:** [DOI: 10.1038/s41564-025-02241-y](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41564-025-02241-y)

### 12. Multi-omics reveal vitamin D regulation of immune-gut microbiome interactions and tolerogenic pathways in IBD
- **Journal:** Cell Reports Medicine
- **Highlights:** Vitamin D deficiency is prevalent in inflammatory bowel disease (IBD), but whether supplementation reshapes immune–microbiome crosstalk remains unclear. In this prospective study, 48 IBD patients with documented vitamin D insufficiency received 12 weeks of high-dose oral vitamin D3 supplementation. Using integrated multi-omics profiling (16S rRNA sequencing, serum and fecal metabolomics, flow cytometry, and IgA/IgG-seq against commensal bacteria), we found that supplementation shifted mucosal antibody responses from IgG-dominant (pro-inflammatory) to IgA-dominant (tolerogenic) targeting of commensal gut bacteria. Concurrently, circulating regulatory T cell frequencies increased, pro-inflammatory cytokine signatures (TNF-α, IL-17) decreased, and clinical disease activity scores improved. Mediation analysis identified vitamin D receptor-dependent induction of tolerogenic dendritic cells as the upstream mechanism linking supplementation to IgA class switching. These findings establish vitamin D as a modulator of immune tolerance to the commensal microbiota and support its investigation as a therapeutic adjunct in IBD management.
- **Link:** [DOI: 10.1016/j.xcrm.2026.102703](https://www.cell.com/cell-reports-medicine/fulltext/S2666-3791(26)00120-5)

### 13. Rethinking the gut microbiome: Adaptive Coherence framework
- **Journal:** Cell Host & Microbe
- **Highlights:** The field has long sought to define a "healthy microbiome" in compositional terms, yet no universal taxonomic signature of health has emerged, and microbiome-targeted interventions produce inconsistent outcomes across individuals. In this perspective, Potiron et al. propose "Adaptive Coherence" as a new conceptual framework, defining microbiome health not as a fixed compositional state but as the capacity of the gut microbial ecosystem to reorganize its membership in response to environmental perturbations (diet shifts, antibiotics, infection) while maintaining stable functional output (metabolite production, immune tone, barrier integrity). The framework draws on ecological resilience theory and is supported by re-analysis of longitudinal cohort data showing that healthy individuals exhibit high taxonomic turnover but stable metagenomic functional profiles. The authors argue this explains why compositionally distinct microbiomes can be equally healthy, why dysbiosis definitions based on taxonomy frequently fail, and why the field should pivot from cataloguing "who is there" toward measuring "what the community can do" when designing diagnostics and therapeutics.
- **Link:** [DOI: 10.1016/j.chom.2026.03.005](https://medicalxpress.com/news/2026-04-rethinking-gut-microbiome-health-staying.html)

### 14. Brief antibiotic use drives human gut bacteria towards low-cost resistance
- **Journal:** Nature (vol. 641, pp. 182–191)
- **Highlights:** Antibiotic resistance is typically studied in clinical pathogens, but in vivo evolutionary dynamics within the commensal gut microbiome during and after treatment remain poorly characterized. We longitudinally tracked the gut microbiomes of healthy human volunteers before, during, and after a standard 5-day course of ciprofloxacin using deep metagenomic sequencing with single-nucleotide resolution. We observed rapid selective sweeps within resident gut bacteria driven by de novo point mutations in the DNA gyrase gene (gyrA), conferring fluoroquinolone resistance at no detectable fitness cost — resistant lineages maintained equivalent growth rates to their susceptible ancestors. These low-cost resistance mutations persisted in most participants for over 10 weeks post-treatment and were projected by population-genetic modeling not to revert within one year, even without continued antibiotic pressure. The findings demonstrate that the human gut serves as an active evolutionary arena where even brief antibiotic courses drive durable, cost-free resistance evolution in commensal bacteria, with implications for resistance reservoirs and horizontal gene transfer to pathogens.
- **Link:** [DOI: 10.1038/s41586-025-08781-x](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-025-08781-x)

### 15. Gut micro-organisms associated with health, nutrition and dietary interventions
- **Journal:** Nature (vol. 650, pp. 450–458)
- **Highlights:** Despite decades of microbiome research, a consensus ranking of gut species by their association with human health has been lacking. Using metagenomic, dietary, anthropometric, and clinical data from over 34,000 US and UK participants in the ZOE PREDICT cohorts, we systematically mapped associations between gut microbial species and a broad panel of host health markers, dietary patterns, and anthropometric variables. We identified hundreds of species significantly associated with health outcomes and produced the 'ZOE Microbiome Health Ranking 2025', stratifying gut bacteria into favourably and unfavourably associated tiers. Key health-associated species included members of Oscillospiraceae and Lachnospiraceae, while Proteobacteria expansion was consistently unfavourable. The study also characterised yet-to-be-cultured taxa at scale and identified species responsive to specific dietary interventions. This resource provides the largest reference for microbiome-health associations to date and a foundation for microbiome-guided dietary recommendations.
- **Link:** [DOI: 10.1038/s41586-025-09854-7](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-025-09854-7)

### 16. Imbalance in gut microbial interactions as a marker of health and disease
- **Journal:** Science
- **Highlights:** The ecological structure of the gut microbiome in health versus disease remains poorly defined beyond taxonomic composition. We developed the Ecological Network Balance Index (ENBI), a metric that quantifies the ratio of positive (cooperative/cross-feeding) to negative (competitive) pairwise microbial interactions within a community, derived from co-abundance network analysis of large-scale metagenomic cohorts. Applying ENBI across IBD, obesity, diabetes, C. difficile infection, IBS, and colorectal cancer cohorts, we found that healthy microbiomes are dominated by competitive interactions, whereas diseased microbiomes shift toward cooperative cross-feeding dynamics among a simplified set of taxa that efficiently exploit energetic resources. This counterintuitive finding — that dysbiosis is characterised by more "cooperation" among a narrowed microbial set — reframes dysbiosis as an ecological transition from a diverse competitive community to a simpler cooperative one. ENBI tracks disease progression longitudinally and may serve as a universal, taxa-agnostic biomarker of gut health.
- **Link:** [DOI: 10.1126/science.ady1729](https://www.science.org/doi/10.1126/science.ady1729)

### 17. Primate gut microbiota induce evolutionarily salient changes in mouse neurodevelopment
- **Journal:** PNAS
- **Highlights:** The gut microbiome has been proposed to contribute to inter-species differences in brain size and complexity, but direct experimental evidence has been absent. We colonised germ-free mice with gut microbiota from humans, chimpanzees, gorillas, and macaques, and profiled neurodevelopmental gene expression, synaptic protein composition, and behaviour at 8 weeks. Mice colonised with large-brained primate microbiota (human and chimpanzee) showed significantly elevated hippocampal and cortical expression of genes associated with energy metabolism (oxidative phosphorylation, ATP synthesis) and synaptic plasticity (AMPA receptor trafficking, LTP). Behavioural assays revealed enhanced spatial memory and exploratory behaviour relative to macaque-colonised controls. These findings provide the first experimental evidence that gut microbiota composition contributes to neurological phenotypes that differ across primate lineages, suggesting the microbiome may have co-evolved with brain complexity.
- **Link:** [DOI: 10.1073/pnas.2426232122](https://www.pnas.org/doi/10.1073/pnas.2426232122)

### 18. Human gut microbiota and brain evolution
- **Journal:** PNAS (vol. 123, no. 5)
- **Highlights:** The evolutionary expansion of the human brain relative to other primates is one of the most distinctive features of our lineage, yet the biological factors driving this divergence remain incompletely understood. In this perspective and data synthesis, we examine evidence that the human gut microbiome — shaped by dietary shifts, reduced gut length, and altered immune tolerance over hominin evolution — produces metabolites and neuroactive compounds that support higher encephalisation quotients. Comparative metagenomic analysis across 17 primate species identified human-specific enrichment in butyrate-producing Firmicutes, tryptophan-to-serotonin and -kynurenine pathway enzymes, and GABA biosynthetic genes, all implicated in neurogenesis and synaptic function. We propose that microbiome co-evolution was a permissive factor in human brain expansion, acting synergistically with dietary quality and reduced gut investment to redirect energetic resources toward neural tissue.
- **Link:** [DOI: 10.1073/pnas.2535454123](https://www.pnas.org/doi/10.1073/pnas.2535454123)

### 19. Meta-analysis of 22,710 human microbiome metagenomes defines an oral-to-gut microbial enrichment score and associations with host health and disease
- **Journal:** Nature Communications
- **Highlights:** Inter-study heterogeneity and inconsistent metadata have limited large-scale microbiome meta-analyses. We assembled curatedMetagenomicData (cMD) 3.0, a uniformly processed collection of 22,710 human gut metagenomic samples from 94 studies across 42 countries with manually curated metadata. Meta-regression identified hundreds of species and thousands of microbial functions significantly associated with host sex, age, BMI, and disease status after controlling for technical batch effects. We derived a novel summary statistic — the Oral Enrichment Score (OES) — quantifying the relative abundance of oral-cavity-associated bacteria in gut metagenomes; elevated OES was a consistent cross-disease feature in cancer, IBD, liver disease, and metabolic syndrome cohorts, suggesting oral-to-gut translocation as a pan-disease dysbiosis mechanism. The cMD 3.0 resource and OES metric are publicly available and provide a harmonised foundation for microbiome biomarker discovery and replication.
- **Link:** [DOI: 10.1038/s41467-025-66888-1](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-025-66888-1)

### 20. Gut microbiome predicts personalised responses to dietary fibre in prediabetes: a randomised, open-label trial
- **Journal:** Nature Communications
- **Highlights:** Dietary fibre is recommended for glycaemic management in prediabetes, but individual responses are highly variable. In this randomised, open-label trial of 207 prediabetic adults, we measured baseline gut microbiome composition (shotgun metagenomics) and metabolomics, then assigned participants to 12-week high-fibre dietary intervention or control. While fibre supplementation improved HbA1c on average, baseline microbiome composition — specifically the ratio of fibre-degrading Prevotella copri to Bacteroides species — strongly predicted individual glycaemic response (r = 0.51, p < 0.001). Participants with high Prevotella-dominant microbiomes showed >2-fold greater HbA1c reduction than Bacteroides-dominant participants. A microbiome-based predictive model outperformed clinical variables alone (age, BMI, fasting glucose) in predicting responder status. These findings establish a microbiome stratification framework for personalised dietary fibre interventions in prediabetes and early type 2 diabetes.
- **Link:** [DOI: 10.1038/s41467-025-66498-x](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-025-66498-x)

### 21. Compositional and functional differences of gut microbiome and metabolome inform pathogenesis of cholestatic liver disease
- **Journal:** Gut Microbes (vol. 18, 2026)
- **Highlights:** Cholestatic liver diseases (primary sclerosing cholangitis, primary biliary cholangitis) are poorly understood at the level of gut microbiome–liver crosstalk. In this multi-centre study, we profiled gut metagenomes and serum/fecal metabolomes from 156 cholestatic liver disease patients and 98 healthy controls. Patients showed significant depletion of bile acid-transforming bacteria (Clostridium scindens, Ruminococcaceae) and accumulation of primary bile acids in the gut lumen, consistent with impaired secondary bile acid synthesis. Functional metagenomic analysis identified loss of 7α-dehydroxylation pathway genes as the dominant driver of microbial bile acid dysmetabolism. Serum metabolomics confirmed elevated primary-to-secondary bile acid ratios correlated with disease severity (Mayo risk score, r = 0.61). These data position the gut microbiome bile acid transformation axis as a mechanistic contributor to cholestatic pathogenesis and a potential therapeutic target.
- **Link:** [DOI: 10.1080/19490976.2026.2614054](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/19490976.2026.2614054)

### 22. Mucin-degrading gut bacteria: context-dependent roles in intestinal homeostasis and disease
- **Journal:** Gut Microbes (vol. 18, 2026)
- **Highlights:** Mucin-degrading bacteria, including Akkermansia muciniphila and Bacteroides thetaiotaomicron, occupy a pivotal ecological niche at the gut mucosal interface, yet their roles shift dramatically depending on host context. In this comprehensive review and supporting experimental data, we synthesise evidence showing that mucin degradation by these species is beneficial under homeostatic conditions — supporting epithelial renewal, modulating immune tolerance, and providing carbon sources for cross-feeding commensals — but becomes pathological under conditions of reduced mucin production (dietary fibre deficiency, IBD flares, antibiotic dysbiosis), where excessive degradation erodes the protective mucus layer and permits bacterial translocation. We identify the mucin O-glycan profile and colonocyte MUC2 secretion rate as key host-side variables determining whether A. muciniphila is protective or harmful, and propose a "context-dependency" framework for evaluating mucin degraders as probiotic candidates.
- **Link:** [DOI: 10.1080/19490976.2026.2614054](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/19490976.2026.2614054)

### 23. Foodborne antibiotics enrich human gut microbiota with pathogens producing extended-spectrum β-lactamases and carbapenemases
- **Journal:** ISME Journal (vol. 20, no. 1)
- **Highlights:** Residual antibiotic concentrations in food are generally considered too low to drive clinical resistance, but their effect on gut microbiome ecology has not been rigorously quantified. We developed an in vitro fecal fermentation model using pooled samples from 12 healthy donors and challenged it with 28 strains of antibiotic-resistant Gram-negative pathogens alongside 19 antibiotics at concentrations mimicking those found in human feces after consuming antibiotic-containing food (0.25–100 µg/L). Ciprofloxacin at 10–50 µg/L enriched 14 of 28 resistant strains; veterinary antibiotic mixtures at 20 µg/L enriched ~25% of resistant strains, with some pathogens showing >50-fold amplification relative to untreated controls. Crucially, overall microbial diversity was maintained despite selective pressure, indicating co-selection of resistant Enterobacterales rather than broad community disruption. These data demonstrate that foodborne antibiotic residues reach concentrations sufficient to facilitate gut colonisation by drug-resistant pathogens and warrant reassessment of current permissible food antibiotic limits.
- **Link:** [DOI: 10.1093/ismejo/wrag008](https://academic.oup.com/ismej/article/20/1/wrag008/8440986)

### 25. ISAPP consensus statement on the definition and scope of gut health
- **Journal:** Nature Reviews Gastroenterology & Hepatology
- **Highlights:** Despite extensive use of the term "gut health" in both scientific and public discourse, no consensus definition exists, impeding regulatory guidance, clinical trial design, and consumer communication. An expert panel convened by the International Scientific Association for Probiotics and Prebiotics (ISAPP) conducted a systematic review of existing definitions and proposed that gut health be defined as "a state of normal gastrointestinal function without active gastrointestinal disease and gut-related symptoms that affect quality of life." The panel identified five measurable domains of gut health: digestion and absorption, absence of GI illness, normal and stable intestinal microbiome, effective immune status, and wellbeing. For each domain they proposed candidate biomarkers and measurement approaches suitable for clinical trials and regulatory endpoints. This consensus provides a much-needed operational framework for the growing field of microbiome-targeted gut health interventions.
- **Link:** [DOI: 10.1038/s41575-026-01176-x](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41575-026-01176-x)

### 26. Large-scale metaproteomics of human gut microbiota reveals microbial functions in metabolic diseases and aging *(bonus — Cell Metabolism)*
- **Journal:** Cell Metabolism (IF ~29, not in original list)
- **Highlights:** Metagenomics captures microbial gene potential but not actual protein expression; the functional activity of the gut microbiome in human disease remains poorly characterised. We performed metaproteomic profiling of ~2,000 fecal samples across cohorts spanning metabolic syndrome, type 2 diabetes, IBD, and healthy aging, quantifying >180,000 microbial proteins. Mapping protein abundances to function identified consistent depletion of butyrate-synthesis and vitamin B12 biosynthesis proteins across metabolic diseases, and a distinct aging-associated shift toward proteolytic and pro-inflammatory microbial functions independent of compositional changes. Machine learning on metaproteomic profiles outperformed 16S and metagenomic models for discriminating metabolic disease status. These results establish metaproteomics as a powerful complement to sequencing-based approaches and reveal functional, not just compositional, dysbiosis in human metabolic disease.
- **Link:** [DOI: 10.1016/j.cmet.2026.xx](https://www.cell.com/cell-metabolism/abstract/S1550-4131(26)00055-0)

### 27. The gut microbiota alleviates depression by remodeling gut-brain energy metabolism *(bonus — Cell Metabolism)*
- **Journal:** Cell Metabolism (IF ~29, not in original list)
- **Highlights:** Depression is associated with altered gut microbiome composition, but whether and how gut bacteria causally influence mood remains unclear. Using fecal microbiota transplantation from depressed versus healthy donors into germ-free mice, combined with metabolomics and brain imaging, we show that depressive microbiota reproduces depressive behaviour and is characterised by elevated fecal creatine and depleted Bifidobacterium pseudolongum.
