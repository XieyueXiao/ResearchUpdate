## Papers Found (2026-05-15)

### 1. Network topology of the gut microbiome associates with metabolic health in obesity
- **Journal:** Nature Communications (IF ~17)
- **Highlights:** Gut microbial community *structure* (network topology) rather than just species composition differs across obesity phenotypes. Researchers performed shotgun metagenomics on 931 adults spanning metabolically healthy obesity (MHO), metabolically unhealthy obesity (MUO), and lean controls, constructing co-abundance networks for each group. MHO individuals harbour significantly more robust and densely connected microbial networks than MUO counterparts, with network connectivity correlating with insulin sensitivity, triglyceride levels, and inflammatory markers. A dietary intervention that improved metabolic parameters also shifted network topology toward the MHO pattern. The findings establish microbial network architecture as an index of metabolic status in obesity, complementing species-level compositional analyses and suggesting that dietary modulation of microbial interactions may be a tractable therapeutic target.
- **Link:** [doi:10.1038/s41467-026-72588-1](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41467-026-72588-1)

### 2. High-resolution multi-omics enhances prediction and detection of smORF-encoded proteins in the human gut microbiome
- **Journal:** Nature Communications (IF ~17)
- **Highlights:** Small open reading frames (smORFs, <100 amino acids) are systematically excluded from standard metagenomic and metaproteomic pipelines because of poor annotation and size bias, leaving an entire layer of gut microbial protein biology unmapped. Davin, Ortís Sunyer, Delgado et al. developed a high-resolution multi-omic workflow that integrates smORF prediction into metaproteome database searches and applies ultra-deep data-independent acquisition mass spectrometry without size-based enrichment. Applied to human gut fecal samples, the pipeline detected >25,000 smORF-encoded proteins (SEPs) — the largest gut SEP catalogue to date — many with no known functional annotation. The work fundamentally expands the known functional repertoire of the gut microbiome and provides a foundation for discovering novel microbiome-derived biomarkers and bioactive peptides relevant to host health.
- **Link:** [doi:10.1038/s41467-026-72762-5](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41467-026-72762-5)

### 3. Gut microbiome and metabolic health: mechanisms and precision interventions
- **Journal:** Gut Microbes (IF ~12)
- **Highlights:** Comprehensive mechanistic review synthesising how gut microbial communities regulate metabolic health across multiple axes: short-chain fatty acid signalling (GPR41/43/109A), secondary bile acid modulation of FXR/TGR5 and GLP-1 secretion, gut barrier integrity and LPS-driven meta-inflammation, immune modulation via regulatory T cells and macrophage polarisation, and the microbiota–gut–brain–pancreas axis governing appetite and insulin secretion. The review maps specific microbial taxa and metabolite classes to each axis and critically evaluates the evidence base for precision interventions — dietary patterns, prebiotics, probiotics, synbiotics, and FMT — targeting these mechanisms. Emerging AI-assisted stratification approaches for personalising microbiome-targeted therapies are discussed. The synthesis provides an actionable framework linking gut microbial functions to metabolic disease pathogenesis and treatment.
- **Link:** [doi:10.1080/19490976.2026.2644677](https://doi.org/10.1080/19490976.2026.2644677)
