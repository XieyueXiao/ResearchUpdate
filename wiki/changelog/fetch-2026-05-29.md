### 1. Microbiome functional gene pathways are indicative of cognitive performance in older adults at risk for Alzheimer's disease

- **Journal:** Gut Microbes (IF ~12)
- **Highlights:** Using the Gut-brain Alzheimer's disease Inflammation and Neurocognitive Study (GAINS) cohort, Zeamer and colleagues profiled gut metagenomes from older adults spanning the spectrum from healthy to mild cognitive impairment (MCI) to Alzheimer's disease (AD). Gut microbiome composition correlated significantly with objective cognitive measures in both the MCI and AD groups; functional pathway analysis identified a set of microbial metabolic gene pathways whose expression levels track cognitive performance independently of species composition, pointing to a "functional microbiome" model in which specific microbially-derived metabolites mediate cognitive decline. Findings suggest that microbial functional profiling, rather than compositional profiling alone, may improve early detection of Alzheimer's-associated gut dysbiosis and identify tractable therapeutic targets.

### 2. The gut–bone axis: microbial metabolism and nutritional interventions for bone health

- **Journal:** Gut Microbes (IF ~12)
- **Highlights:** Rodriguez-Bryant and colleagues present a systematic review and data synthesis on the gut–bone axis, cataloguing the mechanisms by which gut microbial metabolites — including short-chain fatty acids, secondary bile acids, and tryptophan-derived indoles — influence bone remodelling, calcium-phosphorus homeostasis, and osteoclast/osteoblast balance. The review integrates evidence from gnotobiotic and microbiome-transfer animal models with human cohort data, documenting how dysbiosis-driven reductions in SCFA production and altered bile acid profiles promote pro-inflammatory cytokine environments that accelerate bone loss. Nutritional interventions (prebiotics, probiotics, synbiotics, dietary fibre) that restore beneficial taxa and their metabolite output are assessed as candidate strategies for microbiome-targeted management of osteoporosis and metabolic bone disease.

### 3. Immune surveillance and microbial escape in the aging host: Why does the microbiome lose its balance?

- **Journal:** PLOS Biology (IF ~9; **bonus** — not on primary target list; news released 2026-05-27)
- **Highlights:** Researchers at the Leibniz Institute on Aging—Fritz Lipmann Institute (FLI) and Friedrich Schiller University Jena (Cluster of Excellence "Balance of the Microverse") propose a new theoretical framework for age-related gut microbiome instability. Contrary to prevailing models that attribute dysbiosis to intrinsic changes in microbial populations, the authors argue that the primary driver is the progressive erosion of host immune surveillance capacity. In a healthy adult, the immune system actively constructs and maintains the microbial niche; as immune function declines with age, individual taxa can gain disproportionate representation and disrupt the equilibrium that had been actively enforced. The framework synthesises evidence from T-cell ageing, IgA repertoire narrowing, mucosal barrier decline, and innate immune blunting, and proposes immune reconstitution or targeted microbiome-immune co-interventions as strategies to prevent inflammaging-associated dysbiosis.
- **Link:** [doi:10.1371/journal.pbio.3003815](https://doi.org/10.1371/journal.pbio.3003815)
