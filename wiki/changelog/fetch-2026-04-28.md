### 1. Microbiome signature of Parkinson's disease in healthy and genetically at-risk individuals

- **Journal:** Nature Medicine (IF ~82)
- **Published:** 21 April 2026
- **Highlights:** A UCL-led international team analysed fecal and clinical data from 271 people with Parkinson's disease, 43 carriers of the GBA1 risk variant (pre-symptomatic), and 150 healthy controls recruited in the UK and Italy. Over a quarter of gut microbial species (176 of ~600 profiled) differed significantly in abundance between Parkinson's patients and healthy controls; 142 of these same species also differed in GBA1 carriers before any motor symptoms appeared, with the carrier microbiome occupying an intermediate state between health and disease. People with Parkinson's showed enrichment of pro-inflammatory taxa (Bifidobacterium longum, B. dentium, Streptococcus mutans, Lactobacillus paragasseri) and depletion of butyrate-producing commensals (Roseburia intestinalis, R. inulinivorans, Faecalibacterium spp.), suggesting that microbiome-based biomarkers could flag Parkinson's risk years before clinical diagnosis and that gut-targeted interventions may warrant investigation.
- **Link:** [10.1038/s41591-026-04318-5](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41591-026-04318-5)

### 2. Gut microbiome features associated with Bifidobacterium colonization predict personalized probiotic persistence patterns

- **Journal:** Nature Communications (IF ~17)
- **Published:** 21 April 2026
- **Highlights:** Researchers harmonised 51,244 gut metagenomes from 149 cohorts across 45 countries to identify host microbiome features that predict whether an orally administered Bifidobacterium probiotic will successfully colonise. They introduced species-specific "Receptive Scores" derived from the baseline community composition that outperform current trial-and-error supplementation strategies; a low Receptive Score for B. longum, for example, correlates with competitive exclusion by resident Bacteroides. The study provides a ready-to-use framework for personalised probiotic prescription and highlights compositional plasticity at the Bifidobacterium–Bacteroides interface as a key determinant of probiotic efficacy.
- **Link:** [10.1038/s41467-026-72289-9](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41467-026-72289-9)

### 3. Industrialization increases the estrogen-recycling capacity of the gut microbiome

- **Journal:** PNAS (IF ~12)
- **Published:** April 2026 (vol 123, no 16; accepted 23 February 2026)
- **Highlights:** Using shotgun metagenomic data from 24 human populations across four continents, the authors show that industrialised populations harbour up to seven-fold greater gut microbial capacity to recycle oestrogens—via beta-glucuronidase-producing bacteria that deconjugate oestrogen glucuronides in the intestine, enabling reabsorption—compared with non-industrialised populations. Formula-fed infants show similarly elevated recycling capacity relative to breastfed infants. The findings link industrialisation and early-life feeding practices to altered oestrogen exposure trajectories, with potential implications for oestrogen-sensitive diseases including breast cancer, endometriosis, and polycystic ovary syndrome.
- **Link:** [10.1073/pnas.2523589123](https://doi.org/10.1073/pnas.2523589123)

### 4. Navigating multi-omic integration methods for human microbiome research

- **Journal:** Nature Microbiology (IF ~28)
- **Published:** April 2026
- **Highlights:** Muller, Bamberger & Borenstein provide a comprehensive methodological review of multi-omic integration strategies applicable to human microbiome studies, covering metagenomics–metabolomics co-analysis, host-microbiome transcriptomic coupling, and cross-modal dimensionality reduction approaches such as MOFA and DIABLO. The review benchmarks methods across simulated and real datasets, identifies pitfalls of naive concatenation approaches, and proposes a decision framework based on study design (cross-sectional vs. longitudinal), sample size, and hypothesis type. A key recommendation is that paired host–microbiome sampling designs substantially outperform unpaired cross-study integration for mechanistic inference.
- **Link:** [10.1038/s41564-026-02328-0](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41564-026-02328-0)

### 5. Habitual coffee intake shapes the gut microbiome and modifies host physiology and cognition

- **Journal:** Nature Communications (IF ~17)
- **Published:** April 2026
- **Highlights:** In two randomised clinical trials in healthy volunteers, habitual coffee consumption (≥3 cups/day) was associated with reproducible shifts in gut microbiota composition, including increased relative abundance of Lawsonibacter asaccharolyticus and reduced Erysipelotrichaceae, partially independent of caffeine. Metagenomic and metabolomic profiling implicated altered short-chain fatty acid and bile acid metabolism as downstream consequences. Participants in the high-coffee arm also showed improved performance on working-memory tasks and reduced circulating inflammatory markers, suggesting a microbiota–gut–brain axis mechanism linking dietary polyphenols to cognitive function.
- **Link:** [10.1038/s41467-026-71264-8](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41467-026-71264-8)

### 6. Gut microbiota reshapes host energy metabolism to modulate depressive behaviors

- **Journal:** Gut Microbes (IF ~12)
- **Published:** 23 April 2026
- **Highlights:** Pu Lei et al. demonstrate in germ-free and antibiotic-depleted mouse models that transplantation of microbiota from clinically depressed donors induces depressive-like behaviour and widespread disruption of host energy metabolism, including impaired mitochondrial oxidative phosphorylation in the prefrontal cortex and altered hepatic gluconeogenesis. Metabolomic profiling identified reductions in circulating tryptophan and isobutyrate as key mediators; isobutyrate supplementation partially reversed depressive phenotypes, nominating this microbial metabolite as a therapeutic candidate for microbiome-targeted antidepressant strategies.

### 7. Microbial metabolite Enterobactin impairs mitochondrial respiration and alleviates colitis

- **Journal:** Gut Microbes (IF ~12)
- **Published:** 23 April 2026
- **Highlights:** Vinita Kushwaha et al. show that Enterobactin, an iron-chelating siderophore secreted by Enterobacteriaceae, suppresses mitochondrial complex I activity in colonic epithelial cells, paradoxically reducing oxidative stress and mucosal inflammation in DSS-colitis and IL-10-knockout mouse models. Mechanistically, Enterobactin-mediated iron sequestration limits ROS production by electron transport chain complexes and blunts NF-κB signalling. These findings reframe a canonical bacterial virulence factor as a context-dependent anti-inflammatory metabolite and open a new avenue for siderophore-based therapy in IBD.
- **Link:** [https://www.tandfonline.com/journals/kgmi20](https://www.tandfonline.com/journals/kgmi20) *(DOI pending full indexing)*

### 8. Rethinking microbiome health through functional dynamics

- **Journal:** Cell Host & Microbe (IF ~30)
- **Published:** 9 April 2026
- **Highlights:** Sahar El Aidy and colleagues argue that defining microbiome health by compositional stability or proximity to a "normal" reference is conceptually flawed given the extraordinary inter-individual variation in healthy microbiomes. They propose "Adaptive Coherence" as a replacement framework: microbiome health is the capacity of the microbial community to reorganise in response to perturbation (diet, antibiotics, infection) while maintaining metabolic and immune functions. The perspective synthesises evidence from germ-free colonisation studies, longitudinal resilience data, and ecological theory to explain why identical interventions produce divergent outcomes and why functional readouts (SCFA production, colonisation resistance, immune tone) are more clinically actionable than taxonomic composition.
- **Link:** [10.1016/j.chom.2026.03.005](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.chom.2026.03.005)
